# Poisson process factorization for mutational signature analysis with genomic covariates

- **Authors:** Alessandro Zito, Giovanni Parmigiani, Jeffrey W. Miller
- **Year:** 2025 (arXiv v2: 14 Feb 2026)
- **Journal:** arXiv preprint (stat.ME)
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2510.26090
- **BibTeX key:** Zito2025
- **Source:** PDF

## Key Contribution

Poisson process factorization (PPF) is a new probabilistic generalization of Poisson NMF for
mutational signatures that models each patient's mutation stream as an inhomogeneous Poisson
point process along the genome, allowing signature *activities* to vary locus-by-locus as a
log-linear function of genomic covariates (histone marks, replication timing, methylation, copy
number, GC content, etc.). This is the first method to *jointly* infer signatures, their
genome-wide activity functions, and the magnitude of covariate effects — rather than fitting
signatures first and correlating second (post-hoc). The model also enables attribution of
individual mutations to signatures via position-specific posterior probabilities, which vary
with local genomic context.

## Methods

**Model.** Mutations of type $i$ in patient $j$ are modelled as realisations of an
inhomogeneous Poisson point process with intensity

$$\lambda_{ij}(t) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} r_{ik} \, \vartheta_{kj}(t),$$

where $r_{ik}$ is the $k$-th mutational signature (Dirichlet-constrained, 96-channel SBS) and

$$\vartheta_{kj}(t) = \tfrac{1}{2} \phi_{kj} \, c_j(t) \, e^{\boldsymbol{\beta}_k^\top \mathbf{x}(t)}$$

is a locus-specific activity function incorporating the patient's copy number $c_j(t)$, a
patient-level baseline $\phi_{kj}$, shared genomic covariate vector $\mathbf{x}(t)$, and
per-signature regression coefficients $\boldsymbol{\beta}_k$. NMF is recovered as a special
case when activities are constant.

**Inference.** Two algorithms are provided:

1. *MAP estimation* via a majorization–minimization update (Lee–Seung style multiplicative
   rules for signatures and baseline activities; Newton's method with step capping for
   $\boldsymbol{\beta}_k$).
2. *Full Bayesian MCMC* using conjugate Gibbs steps for signatures, activities, and relevance
   weights (data-augmentation trick on mutation-to-signature assignments), plus elliptic slice
   sampling for $\boldsymbol{\beta}_k$.

**Automatic rank selection** uses compressive Bayesian hyperpriors from the authors' prior work that
shrink superfluous signature activities to zero.

**Data.** Simulations with $J = 40$ patients, $K_0 = 8$ true signatures, $L = 10$ covariates
(two scenarios: independent and correlated). Real data: ICGC Breast-AdenoCA, 113 patients,
707,104 SBS mutations; 11 genomic covariates binned at 2 kb resolution (Table 1): nucleosome
occupancy, H3K27ac, H3K4me1, H3K4me3, H3K9me3, H3K27me3, H3K36me3, CTCF binding, replication
timing, DNA methylation, GC content. Patient-specific copy numbers provided genome-wide.

## Key Findings

**Simulations.** PPF with covariates outperforms standard Poisson NMF (CompNMF) in recovering
true signature profiles ($R_0$) and total activities ($\Theta_0$) in both independent and
correlated covariate scenarios. All four methods compared correctly recover $\hat{K} = 8$
signatures (except one dataset outlier). Intensity function RMSE is comparable between MAP and
MCMC but substantially better than NMF without covariates.

**ICGC breast cancer (de novo).** Nine signatures recovered ($\hat{K} = 9$), matching:

| PPF sig | Best COSMIC match | Cosine similarity | Biological process |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|--------------------|
| Sig1    | SBS13 (APOBEC)    | 0.86              | APOBEC cytidine deaminase |
| Sig2    | SBS5 (aging)      | 0.78              | Clock-like |
| Sig3    | SBS40a            | 0.79              | Ubiquitous in tumours |
| Sig4    | SBS2 (APOBEC)     | 1.00              | APOBEC |
| Sig5    | SBS3 (HRD)        | 0.86              | Homologous recombination deficiency |
| Sig6    | SBS1 (methylation)| 0.98              | Methylation/deamination at CpG |
| Sig7    | SBS18 (ROS)       | 0.86              | Reactive oxygen species |
| Sig8    | SBS98             | 0.69              | Low match |
| Sig9    | SBS10b (POLE)     | 0.83              | DNA polymerase proofreading |

Key covariate effects (Figure 4c):

1. **Methylation → SBS1 (Sig6):** large positive coefficient (0.42), consistent with CpG
   deamination mechanism; PPF recovers SBS1 with near-perfect cosine similarity (0.98), whereas
   CompNMF and SignatureAnalyzer recover contaminated versions.
2. **Late replication timing → increased mutation rate:** a one-SD increase in late replication
   corresponds to a 113% increase in oxidative-damage (Sig7/SBS18), a 23% increase in SBS1
   (Sig6), and a 32% increase in APOBEC SBS2 (Sig4). The other APOBEC signature (Sig1/SBS13)
   is *reduced* by late replication (−10% per SD).
3. **H3K9me3 and H3K27me3 (heterochromatin) → universally nonnegative effect** across all
   signatures; H3K36me3 (transcription elongation mark) is inhibitory for Sig5/SBS3,
   Sig6/SBS1, and Sig7/SBS18, consistent with mismatch repair recruitment.
4. **Patient clustering by copy-number-adjusted activities** reveals three groups: (1) 67
   patients with moderate methylation/ROS signatures (Sig6 + Sig7); (2) 34 patients with high
   SBS3/HRD activity (clinically relevant for PARP inhibitor response); (3) 12 patients with
   APOBEC-dominant signatures (Sig1 + Sig4) plus hypermutated patients.

**Signature refitting.** Fixing COSMIC signatures and estimating covariate effects and
activities: SBS2, SBS3, SBS5, SBS13 are the dominant contributors; SBS6, SBS20, SBS26, SBS17a
are identified as negligible. Locus-specific attribution probabilities differ from NMF for
13.2% of mutations (95,042 out of 707,104), notably rerouting ambiguous SBS3/SBS8 attributions
based on local heterochromatin context.

**Reconstruction error.** PPF: 14.01; CompNMF: 14.63; SignatureAnalyzer: 13.37 — comparable
performance at aggregate counts, but PPF additionally provides the region-specific intensity
fit unavailable to the other methods.

## Relevance

**hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and (agnostic covariate association recovers known aetiologies).** This paper is
arguably the most direct methodological prior art for hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and. Key connections:

- PPF is a generative model that *simultaneously* infers signatures and their associations with
  genomic covariates — the formal analogue of the phenome-wide covariate scan proposed in hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and,
  but operating at the genomic-locus level rather than the per-sample level.
- The positive-control recoveries are exemplary: methylation → SBS1 (CpG deamination),
  APOBEC → strand-asymmetric enrichment, HRD → SBS3, replication timing → oxidative damage.
  These are precisely the aetiological links that the hypothesis expects to recover as validation.
- hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and operates on per-sample exposures (NMF $H$ matrix columns) as outcomes and regresses
  clinical/molecular covariates against them; PPF does this within-patient at the locus level.
  The two approaches are complementary: PPF is richer but requires WGS with positional
  information, while the cross-study approach works on panel-level aggregated mutation
  counts.
- The "post-hoc is blind to confounders" critique PPF levels at SigProfilerTopography also
  applies to naive cross-study regression: without jointly modelling copy number, GC content,
  and replication timing, apparent signature–covariate associations can be driven by
  confounders. This is a caution for the hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and design.
- The specific quantified effects (Table 1 covariates, Figure 4c regression heatmap) provide a
  benchmark: any cross-study association analysis should recover the same directional effects
  for well-powered signatures and well-measured covariates.

## Limitations

- Requires WGS data with precise mutation positions; not applicable to panel-sequencing data or
  the aggregated per-study SBS count tables that the cBioPortal pipeline produces.
- Genomic covariates are assumed to be shared across patients (only copy number is
  patient-specific in the current implementation); patient-specific epigenomes (chromatin
  accessibility, methylation arrays) would require extended model with higher compute.
- Signatures are held fixed (constant trinucleotide frequencies) across the genome; strand
  asymmetries and replication-strand effects require further model extension (noted in
  Discussion).
- Scalability: the breast cancer run required a 128 GB RAM server; computation per MCMC
  iteration is expensive at 2 kb resolution across 3 billion base pairs.
- Simulation sample size is small ($J = 40$ patients, 20 datasets per scenario); real-data
  validation is limited to a single cancer type (breast adenocarcinoma).
- The paper is a preprint; peer review outcomes are pending.

  exposures regressed against proxy covariates (e.g., tissue-level replication timing

- Compare PPF's locus-specific attribution to the cross-study aggregate mutation frequency
  tables: do cancer types with high heterochromatin fractions show systematically elevated
  SBS18 or SBS98?

- The compressive hyperprior from Zito & Miller (2024) may be worth adopting in the pipeline's
  own NMF rank selection.
