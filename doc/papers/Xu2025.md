# Landscape of human protein-coding somatic mutations across tissues and individuals

- **Authors:** Huixin Xu, Rob Bierman, Dayna Akey, Cooper Koers, Troy Comi, Claire McWhite, Joshua M. Akey
- **Year:** 2025 (bioRxiv preprint posted January 8, 2025)
- **Journal:** bioRxiv (preprint; PMID 39829890)
- **DOI:** 10.1101/2025.01.07.631808
- **BibTeX key:** Xu2025
- **Source:** PMC full text (PMC11741334) + PubMed metadata + web search

## Key Contribution

Xu et al. present the first systematic, multi-tissue exome characterization of protein-coding somatic mutations across healthy human tissues using a novel probabilistic caller (mSOMA) designed for multi-tissue datasets lacking paired tumor-normal controls. Working from 265 deep-exome samples (median 140x coverage, ~2,649x cumulative per donor) spanning 46 tissues from 14 GTEx donors, they identify 8,470 somatic SNVs and characterize how mutation burden, mutational spectra, functional severity, and developmental origin vary across tissues and individuals. The study provides a quantitative pan-tissue baseline for protein-coding somatic mutations in phenotypically normal tissue — the null-model landscape that cancer studies must be calibrated against.

## Methods

### Dataset

- 265 exome samples from 14 GTEx donors, 46 tissues (median 17.5 tissues per donor).
- Donor age: 20–69 years (mean 51.7); 8 female / 6 male.
- Mean exome coverage after QC: 140x; cumulative per-donor coverage: ~2,649x.
- Protected mapped reads deposited in dbGaP phs000424.v7.

### mSOMA (multi-tissue SOmatic Mutation Analyzer)

A new probabilistic somatic variant caller specifically designed for multi-tissue, no-normal-pair data. Key features:

- Models alternative allele counts using a **Beta-binomial distribution**, fitting separate error parameters for each of the 96 trinucleotide contexts to capture sequence-composition-specific technical artifacts.
- Null hypothesis: alternative alleles arise from technical error. Alternative hypothesis: a true somatic variant exists.
- Detection threshold: p < 10^-8 (very conservative to control FDR across 265 samples).
- Validated via simulation: at 2% VAF with 200x coverage, power = 12.1%; at 7.5% VAF, power = 100%. FDR ≤ 18.9% at 2% VAF. In three technical-replicate pairs, 91–95% of discovery variants replicated.
- Performance vs. Mutect2 on 10 colon samples: mSOMA detected 6–19x more candidate SNVs than Mutect2 using esophagus or stomach as a pseudo-normal reference; 60–64% of Mutect2 calls were recovered in mSOMA output (mSOMA is more sensitive for mosaicism in bulk tissue).
- Software: open-source Docker image at https://github.com/AkeyLab/mSOMA.

### Mutation signature decomposition

SigProfilerAssignment / SignatureAnalyzer applied to the 96-trinucleotide profile; COSMIC SBS signatures referenced for assignment.

## Key Findings

### Mutation burden: ubiquitous and tissue-variable

- 8,470 autosomal somatic SNVs detected across 265 samples; **100% of donors and 100% of tissue samples** carried detectable somatic mutations.
- Median VAF: 2.3% (range 0.6%–17.2%), consistent with clonal fractions from single stem-cell expansions in bulk tissue.
- Normalized burden ranged from **0.21 mut/Mb (cerebellar hemisphere) to 2.49 mut/Mb (vagina)**; per-donor range 0.42–1.45 mut/Mb.
- Donor and tissue variables together explain **32.3% of normalized burden variance** (15.2% from donor identity, 17.1% from tissue identity). Tissue and donor effects are roughly equal contributors.

### Mutational spectrum

- Dominant change types: **C>T (46.2%) and C>A (33.7%)**, then T>C (13.8%), T>A (2.6%), C>G (1.2%).
- **SBS18** (C>A, reactive oxygen species / oxidative damage) detected across **all 46 tissues** — a universal normal-tissue signature.
- **SBS7b** (C>T, UV exposure) restricted to sun-exposed skin — tissue-specific.
- No other signatures were identified as pan-tissue; the rest of the spectrum varies by tissue.

### Functional distribution

- 56.4% missense; 20.7% synonymous; 13.5% intronic; 4.4% stop-gain; 2.4% splice variants.
- Observed missense-to-synonymous ratio: **2.73**, notably higher than germline expectations from multiple mutation models tested. The authors suggest this reflects differential DNA-repair pathway activity and environmental sensitivity across tissues compared to germline.
- **11% of somatic SNVs were deleterious** by CADD ≥ 30. By functional class: 98.4% of stop-gain, 94.0% of splice, 8.3% of missense, and 0% of synonymous/intronic/UTR variants were deleterious.

### Mutational hotspots (somatic variant clusters)

Four 50-bp windows showed statistically significant somatic clustering across unrelated donors:

1. **GLI1 exon 12**: 4 missense mutations in 4 donors from sun-exposed skin, non-sun-exposed skin, prostate, and testis — suggesting a recurrent normal-tissue hotspot crossing tissue lines.
2. **GALC exon 15**: 4 variants (2 pathogenic missense + 2 synonymous).
3. **CACNA1C**: 1 cluster.
4. **DDX60**: 1 cluster.

GLI1 is notable as a cancer-relevant gene (Hedgehog pathway); its presence as a normal-tissue hotspot warrants attention in cancer frequency outputs.

### Chromatin accessibility — opposite to cancer

In contrast to cancer studies where open chromatin is depleted of somatic mutations (transcription-coupled repair), normal-tissue somatic variants in this dataset were **enriched in DNase I hypersensitive sites** (p = 3.4×10^-5), specifically at **CTCF-binding regions** (p = 2.0×10^-4). Proximal enhancer-like sequences showed no enrichment (p = 0.53). This inversion relative to cancer suggests different damage/repair dynamics operating in normal, non-proliferating tissue.

### Cell proliferation and aging

- Significant positive correlation between tissue proliferation rate (MKI67 expression) and somatic mutation burden: Pearson R = 0.35 (p = 0.018), driven by C>T transitions (R = 0.39, p = 0.0072).
- **Aging effect is proliferation-dependent**: CpG>T mutations associate with age only in high-proliferation tissues (top 30% MKI67 score, R = 0.26, p = 0.025); no age-TMB correlation in low-proliferation tissues (p = 0.49). This implies that bulk aging effects on somatic mutation burden are largely a function of cell-division rate, not chronological age per se in non-proliferative compartments.

### Shared mutations across tissues (developmental origins)

- 95 somatic mutations shared across multiple tissues within single donors.
- Shared variants had slightly higher VAF than unique mutations (3.0% vs. 2.5%, p = 7.4×10^-4) and marginally lower CADD scores (15.9% vs. 18.3% deleterious, p = 0.044).
- **72.6% of shared mutations spanned tissues from different germ layers**, indicating early embryonic or fetal acquisition before germ-layer commitment.
- Endoderm showed significantly lower proportions of shared mutations relative to mesoderm and ectoderm, possibly reflecting a developmental bottleneck in endodermal lineages.

### Clinically relevant mutations (CRMs)

- 54 CRMs identified (0.7% of all somatic SNVs): 45 in HGMD, 26 in ClinVar, 4 in cancer databases.
- **13 of 14 donors carried at least one CRM**; mean 3.93 CRMs/donor; maximum 7/donor.
- 42.2% of CRMs were autosomal dominant; 48.9% autosomal recessive; 8.9% both.
- **72% of CRMs occurred in tissues not directly affected by the associated disease phenotype**, suggesting widespread cryptic molecular events in phenotypically normal individuals.
- CRM mutational spectrum differs from non-CRM variants (p = 2.8×10^-4): overrepresentation of C>T transitions (consistent with CpG methylation deamination at disease-associated hotspots) and specific amino acid changes (Arg 16.4%, His 14.5%, Cys 7.3%, Trp 5.5%).

### Comparison to cancer driver gene lists

The paper found **no statistically significant evidence for positive selection on known cancer genes** (Bailey 2018 / COSMIC drivers) in this normal-tissue cohort. The authors attribute this to the small sample size (14 donors) and the relatively young median age (~52 years). This is consistent with Martincorena 2017's conclusion that dN/dS ≈ 1 in most normal tissues at the pan-gene level; positive selection signals for specific drivers emerge mainly in epithelial tissues with high clonal expansion (esophagus, skin, endometrium) and require larger cohorts.

## Relevance

This paper provides the most comprehensive pan-tissue protein-coding somatic mutation baseline available for healthy human tissue, and is directly relevant to three aspects of this project:

**1. Null-model calibration for cross-study frequency tables**

The normalized per-tissue background rates (0.21–2.49 mut/Mb) are the empirical floor against which our cBioPortal gene×cancer mutation frequency ratios should be compared. The paper establishes that any gene achieving cross-study elevated frequency in our pipeline must exceed this background, and that background varies substantially by tissue type. The **tissue-explains-17% / donor-explains-15% of burden variance** finding quantifies the confounding we should expect across cBioPortal study cohorts even before tumor biology is considered.

**2. Chromatin accessibility inversion as a pipeline annotation signal**

The unexpected enrichment of normal-tissue somatic variants at CTCF and DNase-accessible sites — opposite to the cancer depletion pattern — provides a novel candidate feature for distinguishing background normal-tissue signal from tumor-specific mutation enrichment. Mutations enriched at CTCF/DHSs are more likely to be normal-tissue background; mutations at promoter / coding regions depleted of DNase accessibility are more likely to be tumor-specific.

**3. Ubiquitous CRMs and pan-tissue molecular events**

The finding that virtually every adult (13/14 donors) carries multiple somatic CRMs in tissues not phenotypically associated with the corresponding disease has direct relevance to our matched-normal stratification. It implies that the "normal" tissue used as a matched control in paired-normal sequencing studies is not mutation-free — it carries its own somatic CRM burden. Studies using highly contaminated normal tissue (e.g., blood as a matched normal in patients with CHIP) will have more of these normal-tissue variants subtracted from the tumor call set, potentially masking true tumor variants.

**4. Relationship to Yoshida 2026**

This paper and Yoshida 2026 are complementary: Yoshida reviews tissue-specific clonal expansion dynamics and driver gene repertoires from high-resolution single-cell / LCM approaches (qualitative depth); Xu et al. provide the exome-wide quantitative bulk-tissue null model covering 46 tissues (breadth and quantification). Together they bracket the normal-tissue somatic mutation space.

## Limitations

- **Small N**: 14 donors is statistically underpowered for detecting positive selection on cancer genes. The absence of a selection signal is consistent with low power, not with biological neutrality.
- **Bulk exome, not single-cell**: captures only clonal or semi-clonal variants (VAF ≥ 0.6%); rare, private, or sub-clonal normal-tissue mutations are invisible.
- **Age distribution**: mean 51.7 years; 43% under 50. Older populations (the typical cBioPortal cancer cohort) would be expected to carry higher per-tissue somatic burden than reported here.
- **No tissue-specific positive-selection analysis by tissue**: the authors do not report per-tissue dN/dS by gene or identify tissue-specific cancer-gene enrichment within their cohort, limiting direct comparison to cancer-cohort frequency outputs.
- **GTEx donors are post-mortem**: agonal state and post-mortem interval are potential confounders for some mutation signatures, particularly oxidative damage (SBS18).
- **Missense-to-synonymous ratio > 2.73 is unexplained**: the authors flag this as unexpected relative to germline models but do not provide a mechanistic explanation; it could reflect tissue-specific repair, hotspot bias, or a technical artifact of the Beta-binomial model.

## Follow-up

- Read the Martincorena 2017 dN/dS paper more carefully — the authors' "no positive selection" finding is relative to this framework.
- Compare GLI1 mutation rates in our cBioPortal outputs across tumor types where GLI1 is a putative driver (basal cell carcinoma, medulloblastoma, pancreatic cancer) vs. tissues where the GLI1 exon 12 normal-tissue hotspot was detected (skin, prostate, testis) — is the hotspot inflating the GLI1 cancer-frequency signal?
- The CTCF/DHS inversion finding suggests a potential annotation: flag somatic variants in our pipeline that fall at CTCF-bound loci as more likely to be normal-tissue background. This would require cross-referencing gene-level mutation tallies with CTCF ChIP-seq peak annotations — feasible as a supplementary annotation step.
- Consider requesting access to the dbGaP dataset (phs000424.v7) to extract per-tissue, per-gene somatic mutation burden tables as a formal background model for the cbioportal pipeline.
- Compare the 0.21–2.49 mut/Mb normal-tissue range against our per-study normalized mutation counts to identify cBioPortal studies where the reported mutation rate is within or below the normal-tissue background range — those studies may have systematic issues (targeted panels with very small callable space, or extreme hypomutation biases).
