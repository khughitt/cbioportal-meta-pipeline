# Profound Tissue Specificity in Proliferation Control Underlies Cancer Drivers and Aneuploidy Patterns

- **Authors:** Laura M. Sack, Teresa Davoli, Mamie Z. Li, Yuyang Li, Qikai Xu, Kamila Naxerova, Eric C. Wooten, Ronald J. Bernardi, Timothy D. Martin, Ting Chen, Yumei Leng, Anthony C. Liang, Kathleen A. Scorsone, Thomas F. Westbrook, Kwok-Kin Wong, Stephen J. Elledge

## Key Contribution

This tissue-specific proliferation-control note links topic:lineage-addiction-and-cell-of-origin-driver-specificity and discussion:0008-tissue-cell-type-specificity-of-cancer-drivers.

This paper demonstrates that dosage-dependent control of cell proliferation is profoundly
tissue-specific: when the same set of ~17,000 human open reading frames (ORFs) is screened for
growth-promoting (GO) or growth-inhibiting (STOP) effects in different cell types, the overlap
between cell types is strikingly low for GO genes (~7–8%) but much higher for STOP genes (>50%).
These tissue-specific GO genes are selectively enriched in somatic copy number amplifications
from cognate tumor types and improve prediction of tissue-specific aneuploidy patterns,
providing a mechanistic link between the cell-type-specific epigenetic landscape, the gene
networks that govern proliferation, and the recurrent SCNA and driver patterns observed in each
cancer lineage. The study also expands the cancer driver catalog by 147 amplified and 107
deleted genes not previously linked to cancer.

## Methods

**Experimental system — ORF gain-of-function screens:**
Two modular, barcoded libraries spanning ~17,000 human ORFs (~5 barcodes per ORF; in total
close to 30,000 unique barcoded elements) were transduced into three non-transformed human cell
lines in separate screens:

- hTERT-immortalized human mammary epithelial cells (HMECs) — breast lineage
- Human pancreatic nestin-expressing epithelial cells (HPNEs) — pancreatic lineage
- IMR90 fibroblasts — mesenchymal lineage

An inducible doxycycline/rtTA system controlled ORF expression. Cells were passaged for ~10
population doublings with/without doxycycline; barcode abundance was quantified by sequencing
and differential enrichment/depletion scored with edgeR (FDR < 0.05 threshold). Enriched
barcodes (GO genes) identify genes whose overexpression promotes proliferation; depleted
barcodes (STOP genes) identify genes whose overexpression inhibits proliferation.

**shRNA loss-of-function screens:**
Complementary shRNA screens for proliferation-essential (STOP gene validation) and
proliferation-suppressive (TSG-like) loci were performed in cell-type panels to cross-validate
the ORF findings and distinguish overexpression artifacts from genuine dosage effects.

**In vivo validation:**
A sublibrary of 309 GO, 131 STOP, and 46 neutral HMEC genes was transduced into MCF7 breast
cancer cells; doxycycline-inducible xenograft tumors were grown in mice and barcode frequencies
measured in excised tumors. Screen performance was correlated between in vitro (HMEC) and in
vivo (MCF7 xenograft) results.

**SCNA enrichment analysis:**
GO and STOP genes from each cell type were tested for enrichment in focal and arm-level somatic
copy number amplifications/deletions from TCGA tumor cohorts (pan-cancer and cancer-type
matched). Concordance between cell-type identity of screened cells and tumor type of the SCNA
dataset was the key variable tested.

**Aneuploidy prediction:**
Arm-level and whole-chromosome aneuploidy frequencies across breast cancer (BRCA) and pancreatic
adenocarcinoma (PAAD) tumors were modeled using gene expression and SCNA data; models were
evaluated with and without tissue-specific GO gene information to assess whether GO gene sets
improve tumor-type-matched aneuploidy pattern prediction.

## Key Findings

**~10% of the human genome controls proliferation in a dosage-dependent manner:**
Across the three cell types, approximately 391 growth-promoting (GO) genes and 1,269
growth-inhibiting (STOP) genes passed the FDR < 0.05 threshold, consistent with roughly 10%
of screened genes having dosage-sensitive proliferation effects.

**Oncogenic GO genes are profoundly tissue-specific; TSG-like STOP genes are largely conserved:**
- Only 19 genes scored as GO genes in both HMECs and HPNEs, representing 7.5–8.5% overlap.
- Only 3 genes were shared GO hits across all three cell lines (HMEC, HPNE, IMR90).
- Pearson correlation between full GO screen profiles was only r = 0.3–0.4 across cell types.
- By contrast, >50% of HPNE STOP genes were also depleted in HMECs, with >100 shared STOP
  genes across all three lines — STOP gene function is broadly conserved across lineages.

**Known cancer drivers are enriched in GO/STOP gene sets:**
- GO genes showed >100% enrichment among pan-cancer oncogenes.
- STOP genes showed >100% enrichment among pan-cancer tumor suppressors.
- Both sets showed >95% enrichment in tumor-type-specific driver catalogs.
- Expected tissue-specific drivers were recovered: GATA3 (depleted in HMECs, consistent with
  breast TSG role), RAS-pathway genes (enriched in HPNEs, consistent with pancreatic cancer).

**KRTAP (keratin-associated protein) family: novel tissue-specific oncogenic driver family:**
Keratin-associated proteins were among the most striking novel GO hits:
- 4 of the top 10 HMEC GO genes were KRTAPs (p = 3.3 × 10⁻⁸)
- 16 of the top 50 HMEC GO genes were KRTAPs (p = 4.0 × 10⁻²⁸)
- KRTAPs were neutral in HPNE and fibroblast screens
- KRTAP-mediated proliferation in HMECs operates through ~2-fold upregulation of E2F1 mRNA and
  protein; shRNA knockdown of E2F1 reversed the KRTAP-induced proliferation enhancement.

**Tissue-specific GO genes predict tissue-matched SCNA patterns:**
- HMEC GO genes were enriched >2-fold in breast cancer (BRCA) amplicons relative to HPNE GO
  genes (p = 0.03).
- HPNE GO genes were enriched >2-fold in pancreatic adenocarcinoma (PAAD) amplicons relative
  to HMEC GO genes (p = 0.02).
- Both HMEC and HPNE GO genes showed >50% enrichment in pan-cancer focal amplifications
  (HMEC p = 0.01; HPNE p = 0.03).
- HPNE GO genes showed greater enrichment in gastrointestinal adenocarcinomas; HMEC GO genes
  showed greater enrichment in reproductive system tumors.

**Tissue-specific GO genes improve aneuploidy pattern prediction in matched tumors:**
- Breast cancer arm-level aneuploidy: r improved from 0.45 to 0.51 (p improved from 0.002 to
  0.0005) when HMEC GO genes were added.
- Breast cancer whole-chromosome aneuploidy: r improved from 0.26 (p = 0.1) to 0.40 (p = 0.03).
- Pancreatic cancer arm-level aneuploidy: r improved from 0.44 (p = 0.003) to 0.49 (p = 0.0008).
- Crucially, using mismatched GO gene sets (wrong cell type for the tumor type) either failed
  to improve or impaired predictions, confirming the effect is tissue-lineage-specific.

**Novel driver catalog expansion:**
- 147 amplified GO genes as candidate oncogenic drivers (133 not previously linked to cancer).
- 107 deleted STOP genes as candidate tumor suppressor drivers.
- At least 41 genes appear to act cooperatively as co-drivers (p = 0.0012).

**In vivo validation confirms in vitro screen performance:**
HMEC GO and STOP gene behavior in MCF7 xenograft tumors correlated at r = 0.82 with in vitro
HMEC screen results. GO genes enriched in tumors; STOP genes depleted. KRTAP GO genes were
significantly enriched in tumors.

**Unsupervised clustering of tumor types by SCNA reflects anatomical relatedness:**
Hierarchical clustering of cancer types by SCNA patterns grouped gastrointestinal tumors
(PAAD, colorectal, stomach) together and reproductive system tumors (BRCA, ovarian, uterine)
together — consistent with the shared lineage of those tissues shaping similar aneuploidy
landscapes.

## Relevance

**Core experimental evidence for tissue-context-dependent driver effect (not just expression):**
This paper provides direct experimental proof for the central claim of the tissue-specificity
thread: the same gene can be a potent GO (oncogenic) gene in one cell type and completely
neutral in another. Critically, this is a functional screen measuring proliferation effect —
not merely differential expression — making it a mechanistic complement to papers like
Haigis et al. [@Haigis2019] (which addresses why specific driver mutations are selected in particular
tumor lineages). Together, this paper and Haigis et al. [@Haigis2019] cover both the functional consequence
(proliferation outcome differs by tissue context) and the selective pressure (somatic
evolution preferentially fixes lineage-compatible drivers).

**Relevance to question:0042-driver-normal-expression-tissue-cell-type-specificity (OG vs TSG tissue-bias sub-question):**
The paper provides one of the clearest direct answers in the literature to the OG-vs-TSG
dimension of question:0042. GO genes (oncogene surrogates — gain-of-function proliferation promoters)
are profoundly tissue-specific: only ~7–8% overlap between two epithelial cell types. STOP
genes (TSG surrogates — overexpression inhibitors / dosage-sensitive inhibitors) show much
greater conservation across lineages (>50% overlap). This asymmetry has a clear mechanistic
interpretation: TSGs tend to operate via conserved core cell cycle / apoptosis machinery,
whereas OG effects depend on tissue-specific transcription factor landscapes and signaling
network architectures that differ between lineages. This directly supports the hypothesis that
oncogenes show stronger tissue-bias in their driver roles than tumor suppressors.

**Relevance to cBioPortal mutation frequency matrices:**
The SCNA enrichment findings (tissue-specific GO genes selectively enriched in matched tumor
amplicons) provide experimental validation for patterns observable in our cross-study
gene × cancer mutation frequency matrices. If the tissue-matched GO genes reported here
correspond to frequently amplified/mutated genes in our pipeline's per-cancer-type frequency
tables, that constitutes an independent validation path for the biological signal in our
aggregated mutation data.

**Linkage to lineage-addiction / cell-of-origin driver specificity:**
The mechanistic framework proposed — that the tissue-specific epigenetic chromatin landscape
determines which transcriptional targets are accessible downstream of a given driver signal —
is directly the "cell-of-origin" hypothesis for driver specificity. The paper argues that
lineage determines the network topology, and network topology determines which dosage changes
are growth-promoting. This is the foundational logic for `topic:lineage-addiction-and-
cell-of-origin-driver-specificity`.

## Limitations

- Only three non-transformed human cell lines were screened (HMEC, HPNE, IMR90); the full
  breadth of human cancer lineages (e.g., hematopoietic, neuronal, renal, urothelial) remains
  uncharacterized by this approach.
- ORF overexpression may not faithfully reproduce the actual dosage changes seen in cancer
  (focal amplifications produce moderate gene copy gain, not saturating overexpression);
  some GO gene calls may reflect overexpression artifacts at non-physiological levels.
- The screens are dosage-specific (gain-of-function for GO, gain of expression for STOP) and
  do not capture loss-of-function tumor suppressor mechanisms that operate through
  haploinsufficiency or complete deletion rather than overexpression.
- The inducible expression system was validated in immortalized cell lines, not in primary or
  patient-derived organoid systems that might better preserve lineage-specific epigenetic states.
- Aneuploidy prediction improvement is modest in absolute terms (e.g., r from 0.44 to 0.49
  for PAAD arm-level), suggesting tissue-specific GO genes account for a portion but not the
  majority of aneuploidy patterning.
- The 133 novel amplified GO gene drivers lack experimental validation beyond the screen
  proliferation readout and SCNA correlation; functional causal validation in tumor models
  remains to be done for most.
