# Mutational signatures: emerging concepts, caveats and clinical applications

- **Authors:** Gene Koh, Andrea Degasperi, Xueqing Zou, Sophie Momen, Serena Nik-Zainal
- **Year:** 2021
- **Journal:** Nature Reviews Cancer
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41568-021-00377-7
- **BibTeX key:** Koh2021
- **Source:** PDF

## Key Contribution

This review provides a comprehensive, critical synthesis of the mutational signatures field as of mid-2021, covering the biological underpinnings of signature generation, methodological caveats in signature extraction and assignment, experimental validation strategies, and emerging clinical applications. The authors emphasize that signatures remain largely observational associations — causation is not established — and that rigorous use of the framework requires awareness of overfitting, tissue specificity, and the mismatch in power between WGS, WES, and targeted panels. They conclude by outlining clinical applications centered on HRD detection, MMR deficiency, APOBEC-related immunotherapy response, and POLE dysregulation.

## Methods

This is a narrative review synthesizing the primary literature through mid-2021. The paper draws on results from the major COSMIC signature catalogues (v2: 30 SBSs; v3: 49 SBSs; v3.1: 53 SBSs), pan-cancer WGS/WES analyses (notably Alexandrov et al. 2013 and 2020), breast cancer rearrangement signature studies (Nik-Zainal et al. 2016), and experimental validation studies using iPSC systems, organoids, and model organisms. No new data are generated; the review synthesizes published methods and results.

## Key Findings

**Signature classes and their channels**
- SBSs are the most mature class: 96-channel trinucleotide representation is standard; 1,536-channel expansions exist but are seldom used; collapsed 6-channel representations lose biological resolution.
- DBSs are defined by 78 strand-agnostic channels; IDs (indels) by 83 channels. Rearrangement signatures (RSs) use 32 channels classifying clustered vs non-clustered events by rearrangement type and size.
- COSMIC v3 (2020) expanded to 49 SBSs, 11 DBSs, and 17 IDs; v3.1 raised SBSs to 53. Some new signatures (SBS31, SBS35 = platinum; SBS90 = duocarmycin) reflect iatrogenic exposures.

**Biological underpinnings**
- A single primary DNA lesion can be acted on by multiple repair or replication pathways, yielding disparate mutational outcomes. The review illustrates this for APOBEC deamination: uracil can pair with A (SBS2, C>T), be excised by UNG to form an AP site (SBS2/SBS13 via multiple routes), or trigger REV1-mediated C>G transversion (SBS13) — making the two APOBEC signatures partially dissociable yet both arising from deamination.
- A single defective gene (e.g. BRCA1) can trigger multiple compensatory repair pathways, producing a multi-signature phenotype rather than a single clean signature.
- Association is not causation: SBS1 (clock-like, 5-methylcytosine deamination) correlates with age at diagnosis but this does not imply age *causes* the mutations; SBS17 (T>G at NTT) was linked to gastric acid and 5-FU exposure but is observed in unexposed tissues, and in vitro data now point to oxidized nucleotides in the pool as the mechanistic source.
- SBS18 is attributed to 8-oxo-dGTP mismatch incorporation and is amplified by cellular stressors; it is a common artefact in cultured cells.
- Ongoing/endogenous signatures accumulate continuously (SBS1, SBS5, APOBEC); historical signatures (tobacco SBS4) cease accumulating when the exposure stops.

**Methodological caveats**
- *De novo extraction* (NMF) is an ill-posed mathematical problem with multiple solutions; the number of signatures k is not uniquely determined.
- Signatures without marked spectral characteristics (e.g. SBS3, SBS5, SBS8) are more likely to be miscalled as each other; those with sharp peaks (e.g. SBS17b, SBS7) are more robustly extracted.
- *Signature fitting* (assuming S is known, estimating E) is preferred when reference signatures are available, but fitting all 49–53 COSMIC signatures leads to overfitting and false-positive assignments; restricting to a tissue-relevant subset (12 breast-cancer signatures vs 49 COSMIC) sharply reduces exposure variance and false positives.
- WES captures ~1–3% of the genome; a breast cancer with 4,588 WGS substitutions showed only 43 on matched WES. At WES mutation counts, some signatures may be entirely undetectable and many indel channels will have 0–1 counts. Targeted panels lose virtually all resolution for indels and rearrangements.
- Biopsying multiple sites from the same tumour or replicating the same sample inflates apparent shared-signature counts and should not be presented as independent data for de novo extraction.
- The tissue-specific vs pan-cancer ('global') extraction trade-off: pan-cancer analyses have more power but impose an identity constraint across organs; local analyses permit variation but reduce sample size.

**Experimental validation**
- A comprehensive IARC screen of 79 class I/IIa/IIb carcinogens in an iPSC system generated 53 SBSs from 41 agents; well-characterised mutagens (UV, tobacco, aristolochic acid) showed larger effect sizes than many others, implying that low-effect-size exogenous signatures will be hard to detect in human tumours with overlapping endogenous processes.
- Genotoxin screens also generated 10 IDs and 8 DBSs, revealing novel mechanistic insights (e.g., error-prone base-excision repair in cisplatin/PAH repair; Strauss A rule following double-base substitution repair; pks+ E. coli colibactin → SBS88 + ID18 validated independently).
- Gene-edit screens (CRISPR knockouts of 42 DNA repair/replication genes in iPSCs) showed that MMR genes produce signatures nearly identical to RefSig MMR1/2 in human cancers; most other knockouts produced modest effects, except OGG1/UNG knockouts revealing cooperative sanitization of oxidized guanines.

**Clinical applications**
- *HRD/BRCA deficiency:* HRDetect combines six genomic signatures (2 SBSs, 1 ID, 2 RSs, 1 genome-wide loss-of-heterozygosity measure) and achieves AUC 0.98 for biological BRCA1/2 deficiency prediction in breast/ovarian cancer. In the RIO window trial it identified PARP-inhibitor-sensitive TNBCs irrespective of BRCA germline status; it captured ~30% of HRD tumours missed by targeted sequencing.
- *MMR deficiency:* MMR deficiency associates with MSI, elevated TMB at short tandem repeats, and sensitivity to PD-1/CTLA-4 checkpoint blockade (pembrolizumab FDA-approved pan-tumour). Mutational signature classifiers (MMRDetect) may be more specific than crude TMB for MMR-deficiency calls.
- *APOBEC (SBS2/13):* present at variable levels pan-cancer; consistently elevated in cervical and bladder cancers; correlates with kataegis (clustered hypermutation) and structural variation; associated with APOBEC3A/3B overexpression driven by MAF/MAFB in myeloma; correlates with PD-L1/PD-L2 expression; promising as an immunotherapy response biomarker but requires tumour-evolutionary context (APOBEC is enriched in metastases relative to primaries).
- *POLE (SBS10):* ultra-hypermutator phenotype; strikingly distinctive spectrum; associated with checkpoint inhibitor sensitivity; systematic studies required to confirm pan-cancer benefit.
- *Liquid biopsy:* ctDNA carries the mutational signature of primary and metastatic clones; signatures could in principle enable early detection and monitoring, but sensitivity at low ctDNA fractions remains limiting.

**Timing and clonal dynamics**
- Mutational processes are active in normal and precancerous tissue; nearly three-quarters of stem cell lines carry mutation burdens rivalling frank cancers.
- Sequencing matched primary + metastatic samples reveals (1) all primary processes, (2) iatrogenic signatures from intervening treatment, and (3) signatures enriched in clones that disseminate.

## Relevance

This review is directly foundational for **h08** (agnostic covariate–signature-exposure association; positive-control recovery of known aetiologies).

**H08a positive-control connections:**
- The review confirms that UV→SBS7 (skin), tobacco→SBS4 (lung), APOBEC3 expression→SBS2/13, and MMR-loss/MSI→SBS6/15/26 are the clearest exposure→signature links in the literature, supported by both computational and experimental evidence. This validates the choice of these three arms as positive controls in the pre-registered H08a test.
- The review explicitly notes that APOBEC SBS2/SBS13 are mechanistically dissociable: SBS2 is predominantly C>T transitions (uncorrected deamination paired with A), SBS13 is C>G transversions (REV1-mediated). Co-occurrence is the rule, but their ratio varies. An agnostic scan that associates APOBEC3A/B *expression levels* with per-sample SBS2 vs SBS13 exposures could directly test whether expression resolves this mechanistic split — a specific H08b refinement.
- The distinction between *ongoing* (APOBEC, endogenous) and *historical* (tobacco) signatures matters for the H08a design: a within-tissue association of tobacco-exposure clinical label vs SBS4 is confounded if some patients stopped smoking years before biopsy. The review's explicit discussion of signature *timing* supports conditioning on current/historical exposure status where available.

**H08b discovery connections:**
- The review identifies SBS5 (flat, ubiquitous, unknown aetiology) and SBS40 (also clock-like) as signatures whose biological causes remain unexplained. These are the primary targets for H08b novel-association discovery. The review's discussion of replication stress as a potential secondary amplifier of endogenous signatures (including SBS18, SBS5) suggests that expression modules related to cell-cycle progression or replication-stress response pathways could be candidate H08b associations.
- The association-vs-causation discussion (particularly the SBS1/age and SBS17/gastric-acid case studies) frames exactly the reverse-causation guard in h08: we must not assert that expression upstream causes the signature without mediation evidence.
- The review's caution that SBS18 is elevated in cultured cells/oxidative stress is relevant for cross-study meta-analysis: studies with unusual in vitro components or oxidative-stress-related biology could confound SBS18 associations. The pipeline's study-level annotation should flag this.

**Cross-study meta-analysis connections:**
- The review's discussion of overfitting (fitting all 49–53 COSMIC signatures) directly justifies the pipeline's use of `run_restricted_sigprofiler_assignment.py` with a tissue-relevant restricted set.
- The power analysis (Box 1) documents that WES catches orders-of-magnitude fewer mutations than WGS; indels are near-invisible on WES; panels are essentially blind to rearrangements. The pipeline mixes WGS and WES studies — the review's framework supports applying per-sample TMB as a proxy for per-sample signature reliability, and excluding panel-only cohorts from per-sample signature analyses (q018 constraint).
- The tissue-specific vs global extraction debate (Figs. 3b, 3c) reinforces the within-tissue stratification requirement in H08a: signatures should be fit against a tissue-specific signature set to avoid false positives, and all covariate associations should be run within-tissue stratum.

## Limitations

- The review does not address SV-based signatures in depth beyond breast cancer rearrangement signatures; copy number signatures are noted as less resolved than SBS signatures.
- Coverage of indel signatures is somewhat cursory; the 83-channel ID classification is described but no ID signature is given the detailed biological treatment afforded to SBSs.
- The clinical applications section focuses heavily on breast cancer (HRDetect) and colorectal/endometrial cancer (MMR); other tumour types are less detailed.
- As a 2021 review, it predates the COSMIC v3.3/v3.4 updates and any subsequent experimental validation studies published after July 2021.
- The review explicitly avoids comparing mathematical extraction frameworks (NMF variants, SparSig, SigProfiler vs SignatureAnalyzer, etc.) — practitioners must consult Alexandrov et al. 2020 and Kucab et al. 2019 for that comparison.

- **COSMIC** (https://cancer.sanger.ac.uk/cosmic): reference signatures catalogue
- **Signal** (https://signal.mutationalsignatures.com): web-based signature exploration

- **DeconstructSigs** (R): Rosenthal et al. 2016
- **CaMuS**: Cartolano et al. 2020
- **HRDetect**: Davies et al. 2017; a clinical-grade algorithm, not a public-release tool at time of writing
- **MMRDetect**: Letouz\'e et al. 2017
- **mSINGS, MSIsensor, MSIseq**: MSI calling from NGS data

No new tool is released with this review paper itself.
