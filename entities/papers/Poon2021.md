# Synonymous mutations reveal genome-wide levels of positive selection in healthy tissues

- **Authors:** Gladys Y. P. Poon, Caroline J. Watson, Daniel S. Fisher, Jamie R. Blundell
- **Year:** 2021
- **Journal:** Nature Genetics
- **Volume/Pages:** 53(11): 1597–1605
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41588-021-00957-1
- **PMID:** 34737428
- **BibTeX key:** Poon2021
- **Source:** web search + LLM knowledge (initial); verified against full-text PDF 2026-04-18 (bioRxiv preprint 10.1101/2020.10.08.331405, content consistent with published paper)

## Key Contribution

This synonymous-selection normal-tissue note links question:0006-ch-priority-gene-completeness, paper:LeeSix2018, and paper:Martincorena2018.

The paper demonstrates that synonymous (silent) somatic mutations hitchhiking with positively selected driver clones can serve as a genome-wide readout of total positive selection in healthy tissues — not just in known cancer driver genes. Applied to healthy blood and esophagus, the method reveals that canonical driver gene lists explain only a small minority of detected positive selection in blood (~10% from 20 common high-fitness CH variants; ~5% more from the full 468-gene MSK-IMPACT panel; ~90% total remains unexplained), whereas two genes (NOTCH1 and TP53) together account for more than half of the genome-wide rate of positive selection in esophagus (~60% of clonal expansions explained). The central implication is that a large fraction (~90%) of clonal expansions in blood arise from drivers outside the genes currently monitored by standard cancer-associated gene panels.

## Methods

**Conceptual framework.** The method exploits a population-genetic property: when a driver mutation arises and expands clonally, all synonymous mutations co-occurring in the same cell expand in lockstep as neutral passengers. Because these passengers are not under selection themselves, their variant allele frequency (VAF) distribution follows an analytically predictable shape (a 1/f^2 Luria-Delbrück-like power law for exponentially expanding clones). Any excess of synonymous mutations above a neutral, non-expanding baseline directly quantifies the amount of positive selection that occurred somewhere in the genome, regardless of which (non-synonymous) driver mutation caused the expansion.

**Neutral baseline.** The expected neutral VAF spectrum (synonymous mutations accumulating without clonal expansion) is derived from population-genetic first principles, calibrated for tissue type, sample size, and sequencing depth. The ratio of observed to expected synonymous VAF density at intermediate-to-high VAF bins provides the "missing positive selection" signal.

**Linking passengers to known drivers.** To estimate how much of the excess synonymous burden can be explained by known cancer drivers, the authors model the clonal expansion signatures predicted by catalogued non-synonymous driver mutations (SNVs in established driver genes) and compare that prediction to the observed synonymous passenger counts. The residual fraction represents selection attributable to undiscovered or non-SNV drivers.

**Data.** Blood: ~1500 synonymous variants detected in peripheral blood from a combined cohort of 637 individuals drawn from two studies — Bolton et al. (MSK-IMPACT targeted panel, 468 cancer-associated genes) and Razavi et al. (ultra-deep sequencing of cell-free DNA variants; Nature Medicine 2019). Esophagus: ~600 synonymous variants from 9 individuals (Martincorena et al. 2018, Science — somatic mutant clones colonizing the human esophagus with age). Sequencing platform for blood is targeted panel (MSK-IMPACT) plus ultra-deep sequencing; esophagus data is from endoscopic biopsy WGS/WES.

**Scope.** The analysis is restricted to two tissue types (blood, esophagus) in this paper. The framework is general and, in principle, extensible to any tissue with sufficient sequencing depth and sample count to detect the synonymous VAF excess.

## Key Findings

1. **Genome-wide selection exceeds known driver lists in blood.** The 20 most common high-fitness CH variants account for only ~10% of the observed density of high-VAF synonymous passengers (~20-fold density excess; these 20 variants explain only ~1-fold). Adding all 468 cancer-associated genes in the MSK-IMPACT panel recovers a further ~5%, leaving ~90% of clonal expansions in healthy blood driven by mutations outside both sets. Separately, in a per-individual co-occurrence analysis (Fig. 2C), only ~20–30% of individuals with synonymous passengers had a detectable nonsynonymous driver at higher VAF, confirming that ~70–80% of hitchhiking events had no detectable driver in the panel. The unaccounted selection is consistent with pervasive CH from mosaic chromosomal alterations (mCAs), large structural variants, non-coding mutations, regulatory changes, and unannotated coding drivers.

2. **Esophagus is dominated by two known drivers.** In contrast, NOTCH1 and TP53 SNVs together account for more than half the genome-wide rate of positive selection in normal esophagus (~60% of clonal expansions). The observed density of high-VAF synonymous variants is only ~1.7-fold higher than predicted by NOTCH1 and TP53 selection alone (vs. ~10-fold in blood), suggesting the driver landscape in esophagus is relatively concentrated and well-captured by existing annotations. NOTCH1 fitness effect s=11%/year at haploid rate 3.3×10⁻⁵/year; TP53 fitness effect s=10%/year at haploid rate 1.4×10⁻⁵/year. Per-individual co-occurrence confirms: ~75–80% of individuals with synonymous variants harbour a NOTCH1 or TP53 driver at higher VAF (vs. only ~20–30% in blood with any panel driver).

3. **Tissue-type specificity of selection architecture.** The contrast between blood (diffuse, largely undiscovered selection) and esophagus (concentrated in two genes) implies that the fraction of "cryptic" positive selection is strongly tissue-dependent. Methodologies calibrated on one tissue cannot be naively transferred.

4. **Quantitative framework for estimating total driver mutation rate.** The synonymous-passenger approach yields an absolute estimate of the per-cell-division genome-wide driver rate — a quantity not directly accessible by dN/dS or standard driver-gene counting approaches. The model infers the total driver mutation rate µ_b from the amplitude of the 1/f² passenger spectrum. For blood, the unobserved driver rate is ~9-fold higher than the observed driver rate (Fig. 2B). For esophagus, the 1.7-fold excess of synonymous variants above NOTCH1+TP53 prediction quantifies the residual unobserved driver burden.

## Relevance

**Direct pipeline relevance: false-positive driver signal in cancer cohorts.**
The cbioportal pipeline aggregates mutation frequencies across cBioPortal studies to identify recurrently mutated genes as putative cancer drivers. Genes that are positively selected in *healthy* tissues (particularly blood) will appear elevated in any study that includes blood-derived tumors (leukemias, lymphomas) or suffers from CH contamination in solid tumor panels (unmatched normals). The findings in this paper sharpen the concern already captured in `topic:clonal-hematopoiesis-contamination`:

- The seven Bolton CH-priority genes (DNMT3A, TET2, TP53, ASXL1, CHEK2, PPM1D, PRPF8) [@Bolton2020] capture only a small fraction of blood selection signal. This paper shows ~90% of positive selection in blood lies outside identified driver genes (the 20 most common high-fitness CH variants explain only ~10%; adding the full 468-gene MSK-IMPACT panel recovers only ~5% more) — meaning the vast majority of clonal expansions inflate our cross-study mutation frequencies via loci the current `ch_priority_gene` flag does not cover.
- For esophageal studies, NOTCH1 and TP53 are indeed high-frequency in our esophageal cancer cohorts; this paper establishes a pre-malignant, selection-driven baseline for both, which should be factored into interpretation of high esophageal NOTCH1 frequencies.

**Relationship to dndscv rule.** The existing `code/scripts/run_dndscv.R` rule estimates dN/dS per study using the Martincorena framework [@Martincorena2017]. This paper is from the same Blundell lab ecosystem but addresses a complementary question: rather than per-gene dN/dS, it quantifies the genome-wide total selection rate via synonymous VAF excess. The two methods are conceptually orthogonal: dN/dS identifies *which* genes are under selection; the Poon approach estimates *how much* total selection exists above what known genes explain.

**Implication for Bailey driver overlay.** The Bailey driver list used in `annotate_drivers.py` is derived from cancer cohorts [@Bailey2018]. This paper suggests that some Bailey drivers may have been positively selected in healthy tissue first (particularly blood lineage genes like DNMT3A, TET2), so their frequency in cancer datasets partially reflects pre-malignant clonal dynamics rather than tumor-intrinsic selection pressure.

## Limitations

- **Two-tissue scope.** Results are demonstrated only for blood and esophagus. Extension to other cancer types in the cbioportal pipeline (lung, colon, breast, etc.) requires separate modeling; tissue-specific estimates do not automatically transfer.
- **SNV-only driver accounting.** The ~10–15% of blood selection explained by the MSK-IMPACT panel is based entirely on SNVs in known driver genes. The paper explicitly names mosaic chromosomal alterations (mCAs), large structural variants, non-coding mutations, and epigenetic changes as the likely sources of the remaining ~85–90%. The contribution of mCAs is estimated at less than 10% of missing selection from Y-chromosome loss data alone; the collective mCA burden may be larger but is not fully quantified.
- **Requires deep or single-cell sequencing.** The synonymous VAF excess approach relies on detecting clones at intermediate/high VAF. In bulk exome data from solid tumor cohorts (cBioPortal's primary data type), the signal would be dominated by tumor clones rather than pre-malignant expansions, limiting direct applicability.
- **Healthy tissue only.** The framework is designed for normal, non-cancer tissues. In our pipeline, we work with cancer sequencing data — the conceptual transfer is indirect (informing prior expectations about driver gene elevation), not a direct analysis.
- **Cohort-size sensitivity.** The blood analysis required 637 individuals (combined Bolton + Razavi cohorts) to reliably characterize the synonymous VAF spectrum. The esophagus analysis used only 9 individuals but ~600 variants per biopsy (very high sequencing depth per sample). Applicability to individual small cBioPortal studies (typically tens to low hundreds of patients, bulk exome) is limited for direct spectrum fitting.

## Model / Tool Availability

No standalone software package is described in this paper. The paper states: "All code used in this study will be available on the Blundell lab Github page." The statistical framework builds on population-genetic theory from the Blundell lab (related to the `subclonalSelection` / evolutionary dynamics tools). The dNdScv R package (Martincorena lab) is a related but distinct tool for per-gene dN/dS estimation; Watson et al. 2020 (Science, also Blundell lab) is cited as the prior framework for evolutionary dynamics and fitness landscape of clonal haematopoiesis on which this study builds.

## Follow-up

- **Read next:** Martincorena et al. 2018 (Science) — normal esophagus clone size distribution; provides the esophageal data context. Also: Lee-Six et al. 2018 (Nature) for blood clone dynamics underpinning the blood dataset.
- **Question for this project:** Does the ~90% unexplained positive selection in blood (outside even the 468-gene MSK-IMPACT panel) translate into specific genes or loci beyond the Bolton 7-gene list that we should add to the `ch_priority_gene` flag? The paper implicates mosaic chromosomal alterations (mCAs), large structural variants, non-coding mutations, and regulatory changes as the likely sources. (See `doc/questions/q006-ch-priority-gene-completeness.md`.)
- **Approach implication:** The finding here argues that the current 7-gene `ch_priority_gene` list is a severe lower bound on CH-related signal inflation — the true positive selection in blood is ~10x larger than what the 20 most common CH variants, let alone the 7-gene list, can account for. Most of the missing selection likely comes from mosaic chromosomal alterations, SVs, and non-coding mutations that are invisible to SNV-focused panels. A more conservative approach would be to apply a broader CH exclusion (mCA-aware), use matched-normal studies wherever possible, and restrict "cancer driver" interpretation to non-blood-lineage cancers when working with unmatched-normal studies.
- **Potential extension:** If single-sample or low-purity tumor data in cBioPortal retains pre-malignant clonal structure at low VAF, the synonymous-passenger test could in principle be adapted to detect background selection elevation in our mutation frequency tables.
