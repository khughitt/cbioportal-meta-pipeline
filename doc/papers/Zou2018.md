# Validating the concept of mutational signatures with isogenic cell models

- **Authors:** Xueqing Zou, Michel Owusu, Rebecca Harris, Stephen P. Jackson, Joanna I. Loizou, Serena Nik-Zainal
- **Year:** 2018
- **Journal:** Nature Communications
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-018-04052-8
- **BibTeX key:** Zou2018
- **Source:** Europe PMC full-text XML (PDF corrupted)

## Key Contribution

This paper provides the first rigorous in vitro proof-of-principle that cancer mutational
signatures can be causally reproduced by targeted CRISPR-Cas9 knockouts of specific DNA repair
genes in a near-haploid human cell system (HAP1). By comparing parental clone mutation profiles
with those of seven isogenic subclones per knockout, the authors confirmed that disrupting MSH6,
EXO1, and FANCC generates mutation patterns — across substitutions, indels, and rearrangements —
that closely recapitulate signatures previously extracted in silico from primary tumours. The work
also introduces a bootstrap-based algorithmic framework for detecting low signal-to-noise
mutational signatures in cell-based models, applicable to any future genotoxicity screen.

## Methods

**Experimental system:** Immortalised near-haploid HAP1 cells were used because haploid genomes
require only one allele to be disrupted and require less sequencing depth (~15× WGS vs diploid
~30×). Nine DNA repair genes spanning diverse pathways were knocked out individually via
CRISPR-Cas9 (CHK2, EXO1, FANCC, MSH6, NEIL1, NUDT1, POLB, POLE, POLM). Per knockout, a
"parental clone" carrying the frameshift was propagated for one month (~36 divisions), then seven
isogenic "subclones" were derived and all clones whole-genome sequenced at ~15× depth. De novo
mutations in subclones were identified by subtracting the parental clone mutation set; this
captures mutations arising specifically under the knockout condition.

**Algorithmic signature detection (novel contribution):** Because all parental and subclones
shared a pervasive background Signature 18-like signature (C>A-dominated, likely oxidative
damage from culture), a bootstrap resampling approach on 96-channel substitution spectra was
developed. Two criteria must both be met: (1) the centroid distance between parental and
subclone mutation spectra must exceed the 99th-percentile self-distance of each group (spectrum
shift); and (2) subclone mutation counts must exceed the 99th percentile of the baseline
distribution (count elevation). Once a knockout is confirmed, the gene-specific signature is
extracted as a background-subtracted linear decomposition. Analogous 8-channel (indel) and
10-channel (rearrangement) approaches are used for non-substitution classes.

**Comparison to cancer signatures:** In vitro signatures were compared with 30 COSMIC
substitution signatures (cosine similarity matrix) and six cancer-derived rearrangement
signatures from a prior breast cancer structural-variation study. Genomic architecture analyses
(transcriptional strand bias, replication strand asymmetry, replication timing domain
distribution) of the in vitro signatures were compared against published in vivo analyses of the
same features from 560 breast cancers.

## Key Findings

**Positive-signature knockouts (7 signatures from 3 genes):**

- **MSH6 (MMR):** ~4-fold elevated substitution burden over background; C>T and T>C dominated
  spectrum most similar to COSMIC Signature 20 (cosine 0.91). When restricted to breast,
  colorectal, or endometrial cancer tumours, the in vitro signature is strikingly similar to
  tissue-specific MMR-deficiency signatures. Indel profile shows ~7-fold excess deletions over
  insertions, dominated by 1 bp deletions at polynucleotide repeat tracts — classic MSI
  fingerprint.
- **EXO1 (HR/MMR junction):** Substitution signature dominated by C>A/G>T transversions (peaks
  at GCT, GCC, TCT); most similar to COSMIC Signatures 3 and 5 (cosine 0.71 each). Indel
  pattern: high repeat-mediated 1 bp deletions and long microhomology-mediated deletions. A
  rearrangement signature dominated by medium-to-large tandem duplications (10 kb–1 Mb) closely
  matches cancer Rearrangement Signature 1 (RS1; cosine 0.93), a signature previously of unknown
  genetic aetiology enriched in non-BRCA1/BRCA2 HR-deficient breast cancers.
- **FANCC (Fanconi/HR):** No detectable substitution signature; indel signature dominated by
  long (≥3 bp) microhomology-mediated deletions; rearrangement signature dominated by short
  deletions (1–10 kb), inversions, and short tandem duplications — matching RS3/RS5 associated
  with BRCA1-null tumours.

**Negative knockouts:** CHK2, NEIL1, NUDT1, POLB, POLE, and POLM knockouts did not produce
detectable signatures under these conditions. The lack of a POLE knockout signature is
mechanistically expected because the cancer Signature 10 is caused by dominant-negative POLE
proofreading-domain mutations, not by gene loss.

**Genomic architecture validation:** MSH6 knockout mutations show a notably flat replication
timing profile (excess mutations in early-replicating, gene-rich domains) precisely mirroring
the in vivo MMR-deficiency signature in breast cancer, validating the prior hypothesis that MMR
protects gene-rich early-replicating domains. EXO1 knockout mutations are enriched in late
replication, consistent with in vivo observations. Neither EXO1 nor MSH6 showed transcriptional
strand bias.

**One gene — multiple signatures:** EXO1 and FANCC each generated signatures across multiple
mutation classes (substitution + indel + rearrangement for EXO1; indel + rearrangement for
FANCC), demonstrating that single gene defects can engender multiple concurrent signature types
across mutation classes.

**Mutation rate quantification:** MSH6 ~148 substitutions + ~36 indels per division; EXO1 ~16
substitutions + ~0.58 indels + ~0.19 rearrangements per division; FANCC ~0.58 indels + ~0.68
rearrangements per division.

## Relevance

**Direct relevance to h08 (agnostic covariate association):**

This paper is foundational causal evidence for the central premise of hypothesis h08. The
positive-control arm of h08 (H08a) requires recovering textbook exposure→signature links
*without* being told them — specifically MMR loss↔SBS6/15/20/26 and HR deficiency↔SBS3/rearrangement
signatures. Zou 2018 provides the experimental ground truth: the MSH6 knockout signature
corresponds to COSMIC Signature 20 (and tissue-restricted MMR variants), and FANCC/EXO1 loss
recapitulates HR-deficiency rearrangement signatures. If the agnostic association scan in h08
associates MSI status or MLH1/MSH6/MSH2 expression with the right SBS components, that is
substantiated by this paper's causal experiments.

More specifically:

- **MSH6 → MMR signatures (SBS6/15/20/26):** Zou 2018 shows MSH6 knockout generates a
  substitution signature closest to COSMIC Sig 20. In the h08 project, MSI-H status and
  expression of MMR genes (MLH1, MSH6) are clinical covariates that should associate with these
  SBS components — the Zou 2018 result validates that such an association would reflect true
  causal biology, not tissue confounding.
- **EXO1 → COSMIC Sig 3 / RS1:** EXO1 knockout substitution signature resembles COSMIC Sig 3
  (HR deficiency) and the rearrangement signature is RS1, providing causal evidence that RS1
  reflects a specific form of HR repair dysregulation. BRCA1/BRCA2 mutation status, used as
  an annotation in the cBioPortal cross-study tables, is the expected covariate; this paper
  supports the interpretation of any such association.
- **Negative controls (POLE protein-loss ≠ Sig 10):** The absence of a POLE knockout signature
  reinforces that POLE Signature 10 requires a dominant-negative proofreading mutation, not
  simple gene loss — an important caveat when using POLE mutation status as a covariate in h08.
- **Signal-to-noise modelling:** The background Signature 18-like culture artefact discovered
  here illustrates that cell culture experimental systems carry their own confounds — a parallel
  concern for the cross-study pipeline's artefact signatures (SBS27/43/45–60). The bootstrap
  framework for separating a pervasive background signal from a weak target signal is
  methodologically relevant to any low-signal signature detection problem.

## Limitations

- Only nine genes tested; the six negative-knockout genes may simply require longer culture time,
  higher damage levels, or different cell backgrounds to manifest signatures.
- Near-haploid HAP1 cells are not primary cancer cells; some signatures or copy-number features
  may behave differently in diploid/aneuploid tumour cells.
- The pervasive background signature (Signature 18-like) creates a high noise floor; signatures
  with low per-division mutation rates (e.g., POLB, NEIL1) may have been underpowered.
- Cancer-derived indel signatures were not available for in vitro comparison; rearrangement
  signature comparison was limited to breast cancer-derived RS1–RS6 panels.
- POLE Signature 10 could not be modelled via simple knockout, constraining the scope of
  probing dominant-negative or gain-of-function mutational mechanisms.
