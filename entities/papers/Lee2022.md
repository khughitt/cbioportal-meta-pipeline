# Mutational landscape of normal epithelial cells in Lynch Syndrome patients

- **Authors:** Bernard C. H. Lee, Philip S. Robinson, Tim H. H. Coorens, Helen H. N. Yan, Sigurgeir Olafsson, Henry Lee-Six, Mathijs A. Sanders, Hoi Cheong Siu, James Hewinson, Sarah S. K. Yue, Wai Yin Tsui, Annie S. Y. Chan, Anthony K. W. Chan, Siu Lun Ho, Peter J. Campbell, Inigo Martincorena, Simon J. A. Buczacki, Siu Tsan Yuen, Suet Yi Leung, Michael R. Stratton
- **Year:** 2022
- **Journal:** Nature Communications, vol. 13, article 2710
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-022-29920-2
- **BibTeX key:** Lee2022
- **Source:** PDF (XML full text via Europe PMC)

## Key Contribution

Normal epithelial cells in Lynch Syndrome (LS) patients — who carry a germline heterozygous MMR gene mutation — are genomically stable: their somatic mutation rates and mutational signatures are indistinguishable from wild-type individuals. This contrasts sharply with POLE/POLD1 heterozygous carriers and constitutional MMR deficiency (CMMRD, biallelic), where all cells are hypermutated. A single morphologically normal colonic crypt was identified bearing MMR-deficiency-associated mutational signatures (SBS44, elevated SBS1 and ID1/ID2) without a detectable MSH2 second hit, suggesting that partial MMR dysfunction can precede complete LOH and may represent the earliest stage of LS tumourigenesis.

## Methods

**Cohort:** 10 LS patients (aged 23–78) with germline mutations in MLH1, MSH2, or EPCAM. Normal and neoplastic epithelial tissues from colorectum, small intestine, stomach, and endometrium.

**Sequencing:** 152 individual crypts/glands laser-capture microdissected (LCM) and whole-genome sequenced at ~33× (NovaSeq 6000). Clonal purity confirmed by VAF distribution (peak ≈ 0.5). Organoid cultures from two patients also established, clonally expanded, and sequenced.

**Variant calling:** SBS called with CaVEMan (unmatched normal), INDELs with Pindel; post-processing filters removing common SNPs, mapping artefacts, and LCM-specific artefacts (beta-binomial overdispersion filter). Germline removal via binomial test across patient-specific sample panels.

**Mutational signature extraction:** Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP) applied to phylogenetic-tree branches (branches ≥ 100 mutations); de-novo components then deconvolved into COSMIC reference signatures by cosine similarity. Validated with SigProfiler (NMF). INDEL signatures extracted separately (HDP, 5 components).

**Phylogenetics:** Maximum parsimony with 1,000 bootstrap replicates; variant-to-branch assignment by maximum likelihood.

**Comparisons:** Linear mixed-effects model compared LS vs wild-type mutation rates from 424 crypts in 42 wild-type individuals (prior published dataset).

## Key Findings

1. **Normal LS epithelium is not hypermutated.** Median SBS rates in morphologically normal survey crypts: stomach 38.5/year, small intestine 72.6/year, colon 55.2/year, endometrium 35.9/year — comparable to published wild-type values. LME model found no significant LS status effect (SBS p = 0.11, ID p = 0.14). Organoid extended-culture confirmed no elevated in-vitro mutation rate.

2. **Mutational signatures in normal LS epithelium mirror wild type.** Clock-like SBS1 and SBS5 dominate all crypts. Exposure-specific signatures appeared in expected contexts: SBS35 (platinum) in all intestinal crypts from a FOLFOX-treated patient; SBS2/SBS13 (APOBEC) in some duodenal/ileal crypts; SBS17b (5-FU / oxidative stress) in pyloric antrum of a 5-FU-treated patient; SBS88 (colibactin/pks+ E. coli) ubiquitous in two young siblings but absent from their grandmother — inferring a shared microbiome.

3. **One MMR-deficient morphologically normal crypt detected.** Crypt lo0054 (young MSH2 germline carrier) was IHC-negative for MSH2, carried elevated SBS (~197/year) and ID (~37/year) dominated by SBS44, elevated SBS1, ID1, and ID2 — but lacked ID7, N3, or a somatic MSH2 second hit or LOH. Proposed to represent the earliest stage of LS pathogenesis, with partial MMR dysfunction preceding wild-type allele loss.

4. **Driver mutations in normal LS epithelium occur at normal frequency.** 21.2% of 132 non-neoplastic crypts/glands carried ≥1 cancer driver mutation (24 genes; all heterozygous). APC, KRAS, PIK3CA, ERBB2 among drivers found. No morphological or mutation-burden difference between driver-positive and driver-negative crypts. One chromosome 3p UPD event reverted germline MLH1 N38K to wild type.

5. **Tumour crypts show ~100-fold SBS and ~2000-fold ID elevation vs normal.** Phylogenetic trunks of all three tumours carry ≥50,000 SBSs and driver mutations (APC biallelic, MMR second hit) in the common ancestor, with MMR-deficiency signatures (SBS20, SBS21, SBS26, SBS44, N4; ID1, ID2, ID7, N3) dominating branches. This indicates multiple rounds of clonal expansion preceding the tumour's current clonal architecture.

6. **Temporal ordering of LS tumourigenesis.** MMR wild-type allele loss and APC biallelic inactivation mapped to tumour trunks; KRAS/TP53 hotspot mutations emerged later on branches — consistent with the published MMR deficiency → APC loss → adenoma → carcinoma model. SBS1 enriched in trunks vs branches; MMR-deficiency signatures dominate branches.

## Relevance

This paper is directly relevant to hypothesis **h08** (agnostic covariate–signature association; positive-control recovery of MMR/MSI → SBS6/15/20/21/26/44).

- **MMR-deficiency signature set as a positive control benchmark.** The paper precisely characterises MMR-deficiency-associated SBS signatures (SBS20, SBS21, SBS26, SBS44, and de-novo N4) and INDEL signatures (ID1, ID2, ID7 elevated; de-novo N3 = polynucleotide deletions at microsatellites) in a well-controlled crypt-level WGS setting. This establishes ground truth for what "MMR deficiency" looks like spectrally — a reference against which h08's agnostic association must recover MSI status as a strong predictor of these signatures in cBioPortal/MC3 cohorts.

- **Normal-vs-tumour contrast illuminates the denominator problem.** The ~100-fold SBS / 2000-fold ID jump from normal crypt to tumour illustrates that bulk-tumour signature profiles are dominated by post-MMR-LOH hypermutation. For cross-study aggregation, this means MSI-H samples will strongly influence per-cancer-type signature exposures; the pipeline's `is_hypermutator` flag and `_exclusive` columns (excluding hypermutators) are exactly the right instrument to handle this.

- **SBS88 (colibactin) as a confounder example.** Age-cohort and family/microbiome sharing drove SBS88 in two young siblings but not their older relative — demonstrating that environmental covariates (and their correlated proxies in clinical metadata) are tissue- and cohort-dependent. This is a concrete example of why h08 insists on within-tissue association rather than marginal association.

- **APOBEC (SBS2/SBS13) in small intestine.** The localised APOBEC signal in duodenum/ileum of one patient is consistent with cytidine deaminase activity varying across GI segments. For h08's APOBEC positive-control arm, this reinforces the need to condition on tissue-of-origin.

- **Clock-like signatures provide the "null" baseline.** SBS1 and SBS5 dominate normal crypts regardless of MMR status. In the h08 context, these are the "unexplained" signatures to probe with expression modules — the paper confirms they are the background even in MMR heterozygotes.

- **No connection to the cross-study frequency meta-analysis directly** — this is a normal-tissue single-patient study without population-level mutation frequencies. Its relevance is as a mechanistic and methodological reference.

## Limitations

- Sample size small (10 patients, 152 crypts) — power to detect subtle MMR heterozygote effects is limited (authors acknowledge LME test is inconclusive, not a definitive null).
- Unmatched normal used for SBS calling to capture embryonic variants; this requires careful beta-binomial filtering and may miss very rare somatic events.
- Single MMR-deficient normal crypt found — a frequency estimate would require systematic IHC screening of much larger tissue areas.
- No somatic MSH2 second hit found in the MMR-deficient crypt: could reflect true absence (partial MMR deficiency model) or technical limitation (authors acknowledge this caveat).
- Study confined to germline MMR carriers; does not directly address sporadic MSI-H tumours, which arise via MLH1 promoter hypermethylation and may have a different pre-neoplastic trajectory.
- De-novo signature N4 (SBS) and N3 (ID) are partially unresolved — cosine similarities 0.85 and 0.83 to composite references, leaving uncertainty about their precise aetiology.
