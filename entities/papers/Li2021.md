# A body map of somatic mutagenesis in morphologically normal human tissues

- **Authors:** Ruoyan Li, Lin Di, Jie Li, Wenyi Fan, Yachen Liu, Wenjia Guo, Weiling Liu, Lu Liu, Qiong Li, Liping Chen, Yamei Chen, Chuanwang Miao, Hongjin Liu, Yuqian Wang, Yuling Ma, Deshu Xu, Dongxin Lin, Yanyi Huang, Jianbin Wang, Fan Bai, Chen Wu
- **Year:** 2021
- **Journal:** Nature, vol. 597, pp. 398–403
- **PMID:** 34433965
- **DOI:** 10.1038/s41586-021-03836-1
- **Preprint DOI:** 10.1101/2020.11.30.403436 (bioRxiv, 2020-12-01)
- **BibTeX key:** Li2021
- **Source:** PubMed abstract (confirmed) + web search synthesis + bioRxiv preprint PDF verification 2026-04-18 (10.1101/2020.11.30.403436v1). Full text of published Nature version remains paywalled; all quantitative figures below are from the bioRxiv preprint and may differ in minor respects from the final published version (see Limitations).

## Key Contribution

Li et al. (Peking University / National Cancer Center Beijing) construct the first comprehensive *body map* of somatic mutation accumulation and clonal architecture across nine morphologically normal organs from five healthy donors. Using laser-capture microdissection combined with mini-bulk exome and low-depth whole-genome sequencing on 1,737 tissue samples, they demonstrate that somatic mutation burden, clonal expansion, and driver gene enrichment are highly tissue-specific: the esophagus and cardia undergo macroscopic clonal expansion driven by canonical cancer genes (NOTCH1, TP53), while the intestinal tract accumulates high per-cell mutation burden with only microscopic, compartment-constrained clones. Critically for cross-study cancer genomics, 32 potential driver genes — including NOTCH1, TP53, ARID1A, and ERBB2 — are found mutated at appreciable frequency in morphologically normal tissue, establishing an empirical per-organ background rate of driver-like mutations that precede any malignant transformation.

## Methods

**Sample design:** 1,737 morphologically normal tissue biopsies from 9 anatomic sites across 5 donors (autopsy samples). Sites: bronchus, esophagus, cardia, stomach, duodenum, colon, rectum, liver, and pancreas. Approximately 50 biopsies collected per organ per donor. Donors (from Fig. 1a): PN1 (F, 87 yr), PN2 (F, 87 yr), PN7 (M, 87 yr), PN8 (M, 85 yr), PN9 (F, 93 yr) — 3 female, 2 male; age range 85–93 yr, mean 87.8 yr. Tissue segments (1–5 cm lengths) were cut into ~0.5×0.5 cm pieces, snap-frozen in liquid nitrogen. Five serial cryosections (30 µm thick, 232 µm gaps) per tissue segment were prepared for LCM.

**Sequencing strategy:**
- Mini-bulk whole-exome sequencing: 1,762 biopsies initially; 25 excluded (< 10× average depth); 1,737 retained at average 56× depth. Each biopsy: ~600 cells LCM-captured from a 0.06 mm² area (LMD7000, Leica Microsystems). Exome capture: SureSelectXT Human All Exon V6 (esophagus) or V7 (other tissues); paired-end 2×150 bp on Illumina HiSeq XTen.
- Low-depth whole-genome sequencing: 1,764 biopsies (~1.5×10⁶ uniquely-mapped reads each) — used for somatic copy number analysis. Aligned to hg19 with Bowtie2; CNA calling with R package DNAcopy (CBS algorithm).
- Peripheral blood WBC DNA used as germline comparator for each donor.

**Variant calling:** SNVs called with MuTect v1.1.4; INDELs with GATK Unified Genotyper. Filtering: ≥10× coverage in tissue biopsies, ≥3-fold mutation coverage, MAF > 5%, dbSNP and ESP variants removed. SnpEff v3.0 for annotation.

**Spatial clonal reconstruction:** Phylogenetic analysis (MEGAX) combined with Bayesian Dirichlet process clustering of mutant cell fractions (MCFs) across adjacent LCM biopsies from the same tissue layer, achieving sub-millimetre spatial resolution of clone boundaries. Clonal independent index defined per organ to quantify degree of clonal expansion.

**Driver gene identification:** dNdScv algorithm (Martincorena 2017), applied two ways: (1) all nine organs pooled; (2) each organ separately. Candidate gene set of 126 driver gene candidates derived from published cancer and normal-tissue sequencing studies. Five significance categories; q < 0.1 threshold. TTN excluded due to size bias. 32 potential driver genes identified; pathway enrichment via Reactome FI Cytoscape plugin. Observed/expected ratio (R_o/e) used to quantify organ preference of each driver gene.

**Mutational signature decomposition:** De novo extraction via Bayesian hierarchical Dirichlet process (HDP R package v0.1.5) on coding + non-coding SNVs from exome data, combined per tissue layer per organ. Seven signatures extracted (Sig A–G), matched to COSMIC/PCAWG catalogue by cosine similarity (threshold > 0.85): SBS5, SBS1, SBS22, SBS4, SBS45, SBS13, SBS2. Individual-sample decomposition of liver samples also performed with deconstructSigs v1.8.0.

## Key Findings

### Tissue-specific mutation burden

Mutation numbers (reported as mutations per exome) varied substantially across tissues and donors. Cross-donor median values from Fig. 1b (preprint):

| Tissue | Cross-donor median (mut/exome) |
|---|---|
| Pancreas | 10 (lowest) |
| Colon | 19 |
| Bronchia | 26 |
| Esophagus | 31 |
| Stomach | 31 |
| Duodenum | 35 |
| Rectum | 41.5 |
| Cardia | 44 |
| Liver | 69 (highest) |

Liver had the highest median burden overall (median = 69/exome), substantially higher than other organs. The highest individual-donor median was PN2 liver at 104/exome. Pancreas parenchyma harboured the fewest mutations (median = 10/exome), consistent with slow turnover of pancreatic parenchymal cells. Note: burden is reported per exome, not per Mb; conversion to mutations/Mb would require dividing by exome size (~30–50 Mb depending on capture kit).

Within-donor cross-tissue medians: PN1 = 24, PN2 = 17, PN7 = 28.5, PN8 = 35, PN9 = 43.5. All five donors are elderly (85–93 yr), so these values represent late-life accumulation.

### Clonal expansion patterns — a critical dichotomy

Two fundamentally distinct architectures observed:

**Macroscopic expansion (esophagus and cardia):** Somatic clones expand to several millimetres, covering 2 to >10 LCM biopsies and spreading across 2–3 tissue layers. In PN9 esophagus, large-scale clonal expansions were directly mapped. NOTCH1, TP53, and ARID1A mutations are identified as drivers of esophageal clonal expansion; one example clone harboured a NOTCH1 p.A348D mutation and expanded to intermix with an adjacent FAT1-mutant clone. A third, massively expanded clone (clone 3 in PN9) carried no point driver mutations but ubiquitously harboured CNAs in chromosomes 3, 5, and 7 — a CNA-driven expansion pattern not previously reported.

Copy number alterations were rare overall (91.2% of all 1,764 WGS samples were diploid), but strongly organ-preferential: esophagus samples harboured CNA in 31/41 (75.6%) from PN1, 10/41 (24.4%) from PN8, and 24/50 (48.0%) from PN9. Esophageal CNAs were specifically whole-chromosomal amplifications of chromosomes 3, 5, and 7. Cardia also showed frequent CNAs in 27/34 (79.4%) from PN7 and 15/44 (34.1%) from PN8, but with a different CNA pattern: whole-chromosomal amplifications of chromosomes 2, 7, 8, 13, and 20. Liver, despite high mutational burden and macroscopic clonal expansion, rarely showed CNAs across the genome.

**Microscopic, compartment-confined expansion (colon, rectum, duodenum):** High per-cell mutation burden but clones remain microscopically small and evolve independently (nearly all colon and rectum samples in PN9 originated as independent mutant clones). The intestinal crypt structure physically constrains clonal spread, consistent with the Yoshida 2026 review's mechanistic framing.

**Liver:** Degree of clonal expansion in liver was large and comparable to that in esophagus, but with fewer identified point driver events. In PN2, massive clonal expansion in liver likely relates to overwhelming SBS22 (aristolochic acid) mutagenesis.

MUC6 is confirmed as a putative driver enriched in cardia and stomach normal tissue: 28 unique non-silent mutations in 37 samples; R_o/e = 6.59 (cardia) and 2.14 (stomach). Notably, MUC6 mutation prevalence in normal gastric tissue was significantly higher than in TCGA gastric cancers (P < 10⁻⁷, Fisher's exact test), suggesting it drives normal clonal evolution via a different mechanism than cancer.

### 32 normal-tissue driver genes

32 potential driver genes identified in morphologically normal tissue using dNdScv, including canonical cancer drivers. Key genes with confirmed organ enrichment (R_o/e):

- **NOTCH1** — most frequently mutated overall: 65 unique non-silent mutations in 101 samples. Enriched in esophageal tissue (R_o/e = 2.87). NOTCH1 and TP53 mutations were widely observed across organs but showed strongest enrichment in esophageal tissue specifically; bronchial enrichment of NOTCH1 is not reported in the preprint text.
- **TP53** — enriched in esophageal tissue (R_o/e = 2.92).
- **MUC6** — enriched in cardia (R_o/e = 6.59) and stomach (R_o/e = 2.14). Not previously reported as a normal-tissue driver.
- **ARID1A** — co-occurs with NOTCH1/TP53 in esophageal clones; present across multiple organs.
- **AMER1 and KMT2D** — enriched in liver (R_o/e: 2.21 and 2.76 respectively).
- **ERBB2 and ERBB3** — enriched in rectal tissues (R_o/e: 2.21 and 2.76 respectively).
- **SMARCA4 and CREBBP** — enriched in duodenal tissues (R_o/e: 6.12 and 4.9 respectively).
- **FAT1** — co-occurs in esophageal clones alongside NOTCH1.

Driver frequency by organ: ~73.8% of esophageal samples harboured at least one driver mutation (ranging from 67.5% in PN2 to 81.6% in PN9); ~11% of esophageal samples harboured more than three driver mutations. In contrast, only ~6.5% of pancreas parenchyma samples had driver mutations (ranging from 2% in PN9 to 12.2% in PN1).

The full 32-gene list is in Supplementary Table 8 of the paper (not directly accessible in the preprint text body; the list was constructed from a 126-gene candidate set drawn from pan-cancer dNdScv studies, TCGA organ studies, and prior normal-tissue sequencing studies).

### Ubiquitous SBS1/SBS5 endogenous signatures

Seven de novo signatures extracted (Sigs A–G), matching COSMIC signatures: SBS5, SBS1, SBS22, SBS4, SBS45, SBS13, and SBS2.

SBS1 (age-related, deamination of methylated CpGs) and SBS5 (age-related, unknown mechanism) are present across all normal samples and all organs, but with organ-specific relative contributions conserved across donors: duodenum, colon, and rectum prefer SBS1 over SBS5; bronchia, pancreas, esophagus, and liver show relatively higher SBS5 activity.

Two exogenous signatures were identified:
- **SBS4** (tobacco smoking) — detected in bronchia, esophagus, and liver samples. Multi-tissue detection in single donors confirms systemic carcinogen exposure beyond the primary target tissue.
- **SBS22** (aristolochic acid, AA) — detected in liver samples of three donors (PN1, PN2, PN9) — all three are female. AA mutagenesis was also found in esophagus and duodenum of PN1, stomach of PN2, and duodenum of PN7. No AA mutagenesis had been previously reported in normal or cancerous stomach/duodenum tissue. The dominant SBS22 in liver samples of this Eastern Asian cohort may explain escalated liver cancer incidence in Eastern Asia.

APOBEC-related signatures (SBS2, SBS13) emerged sporadically (e.g., PN1 duodenum, PN7 esophagus layer 3). Intra-tissue heterogeneity of mutational signatures was observed even between adjacent LCM biopsies within the same tissue layer (e.g., PN9 liver).

### First intra-individual cross-organ mutation map

The study's most distinctive methodological contribution is that all nine organs are sampled from the same five donors, enabling direct inter-organ comparison without confounding by individual-level genetic background, age, or carcinogen exposure history. This within-individual design is explicitly described as "the first comprehensive body map of somatic mutations and clonal expansions from the same individual."

## Relevance

This paper is directly relevant to the cbioportal project across three dimensions:

**1. Empirical background rate for normal-tissue driver contamination**

The 32 driver genes found in morphologically normal tissue define the expected false-positive inflation risk in cancer studies where tumor purity is imperfect or normal-tissue sampling is unavoidable (e.g., endoscopic biopsies). NOTCH1 and TP53 enrichment in normal esophageal tissue — the same genes prominent in esophageal cancer — creates a specific contamination risk for esophageal ESCC studies in cBioPortal where biopsy-based sampling is common. This directly extends the logic of `question:0001`.

**2. Organ-specific clonal architecture as a contamination-risk stratifier**

The esophagus/cardia vs. intestinal dichotomy has pipeline implications: esophageal studies carry a higher normal-clone contamination risk than colonic studies, because normal esophageal clones are macroscopic (large VAF) and driver-enriched, whereas normal colonic clones are microscopic (low VAF) and less likely to pass tumor-calling VAF filters. This provides a tissue-specific risk ranking absent from the Yoshida 2026 review.

**3. SBS1/SBS5 as the universal aging baseline**

The demonstration that SBS1/SBS5 dominate all normal tissue provides the empirical anchor for interpreting the mutation-burden floor across cancer types in our pipeline. High per-sample mutation counts in older cohorts are expected even after excluding hypermutators, because the SBS1/SBS5 aging clock runs in all dividing tissues. This contextualizes the hypermutator annotation work (t081/t092–t099) — the "background" is not zero, and its floor rises with age and tissue type.

**4. Within-individual study design as an unconfounded reference**

Unlike cross-cohort normal-tissue studies, the within-individual design isolates tissue-type effects from individual-level exposures. This is relevant to pipeline interpretation: cross-cancer differences in mutation frequency in our gene×cancer matrix partly reflect true tissue-of-origin biology (as shown here) rather than only study-design or cohort effects.

## Limitations

- Very small cohort: 5 donors only. Body-map patterns may not generalize across ethnic groups, age ranges, or exposure histories. The per-tissue driver frequency estimates carry substantial uncertainty at N=5.
- All donors are elderly (85–93 yr) deceased organ donors; mutation burdens are therefore late-life accumulation values and will not directly translate to younger populations. Per-tissue medians cannot be linearly scaled to younger ages without an age-effect model.
- Only 9 organs profiled; major organs (kidney, lung parenchyma, bladder, prostate, ovary, uterus) are absent, limiting the scope of the "body map."
- Mini-bulk WES on ~600 cells can miss rare clones smaller than the biopsy unit; microscopic clones are likely underdetected relative to a single-cell approach.
- Exome sequencing captures coding variants; intronic and non-coding regulatory mutations (potentially driver-relevant in some tissue contexts) are not captured in the exome tier.
- Donors are from an Eastern Asian (Chinese) cohort; the strong SBS22 (aristolochic acid) signal in three of five donors is likely a population-specific exposure pattern. This affects mutation burden ranking (liver is unusually high partly due to AA-driven mutagenesis) and may not generalize to Western cohorts.
- Quantitative values in this summary are from the bioRxiv preprint (v1, 2020-12-01). Minor differences may exist in the published Nature version (2021); the full 32-driver-gene list in Supplementary Table 8 was not accessible in the preprint text and has not been individually confirmed here.

## Model / Tool Availability

- Raw sequencing data: Published paper deposited to EGA (EGAD00001007859; WGS + WES FASTQ files, 1,764 WGS + 1,772 WES samples; requires EGA DAC approval EGAC00001002218). The bioRxiv preprint lists the data repository as Genome Sequence Archive (GSA) in BIG Data Center (https://bigd.big.ac.cn), project PRJCA003552 — this likely changed to EGA upon journal submission.
- Code: Mutational signature analysis — HDP R package v0.1.5 (https://github.com/nicolaroberts/hdp); MCF Bayesian Dirichlet clustering adapted from https://github.com/sfbrunner/liver-pub-repo; driver gene analysis — dNdScv v0.01 (https://github.com/im3sanger/dndscv).
- No standalone software tool released alongside the paper.
