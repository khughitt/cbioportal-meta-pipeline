## Key Contribution

This oncofetal-reprogramming review note links paper:Hanahan2023, paper:Huang2023a, and paper:Huang2025a.

Cancer cells recapitulate and exploit programs of embryonic development — a phenomenon termed oncofetal reprogramming — to drive tumor development, progression, immune evasion, and therapy resistance. This 43-page comprehensive review synthesizes evidence across four interlinked mechanisms: (1) reactivation of embryonic developmental signaling cascades (Wnt, Notch, Hedgehog, Hippo, TGF-β, FGF/FGFR); (2) entry of cancer cells into an embryonic diapause-like dormancy state; (3) oncofetal reprogramming of the tumor microenvironment (TME), particularly macrophages and endothelial cells toward fetal-like phenotypes; and (4) overexpression of embryo/fetal ABC drug-efflux transporters that confer multidrug resistance. The unifying thesis is that cancer cells do not merely acquire new properties but revert to ancient, conserved developmental programs encoded in the genome — programs that were functionally silenced in adult tissues.

## Methods

Narrative review. No primary data generated. Authors searched the literature for evidence connecting embryonic developmental biology with cancer hallmarks, organizing findings into four mechanistic pillars with associated therapeutic implications. Each pathway section reviews normal developmental function, aberrant activation in cancer, downstream consequences for tumor progression and drug resistance, and emerging pharmacological inhibitors in preclinical and clinical trials. Tables 1–3 provide structured summaries of clinical-stage small molecules targeting Wnt, Notch, and Hedgehog signaling respectively, drawn from ClinicalTrials.gov records.

## Key Findings

### 1. Reactivation of embryonic developmental signaling cascades

Five signaling pathways essential for embryogenesis are systematically hijacked in cancer:

**Wnt/beta-catenin pathway:** The first Wnt gene (*Wnt1*) was discovered in mammary tumorigenesis. Stabilized beta-catenin translocates to the nucleus and drives transcription of target genes including *MYCN*, Cyclin `D1`, *HNF1A*, *PPARG*, *MMP7*, and *CD44*. Active Wnt/beta-catenin contributes to T-cell exclusion and anti-PD-L1 resistance. lncRNA SNHG11 activates Wnt/beta-catenin via GSK-3beta ubiquitination in gastric cancer. Receptor FZD7 is associated with aggressiveness in stem-A ovarian cancer.

**Notch pathway:** Downstream target genes include *HES* family, *MYC*, *CDKN1A*, *CCND3*, *HER2*, and *NRARP*. FGF4 secreted by B-cell lymphoma cells activates Notch2 in adjacent cancer cells, promoting chemoresistance. CAF-derived CCL2 upregulates Notch1 and maintains stemness in breast cancer. Stromal-derived exosomes activate STAT1-dependent Notch3 expression.

**Hedgehog (Hh) pathway:** Gli transcription factors drive target genes *GLI1*, *PTCH1*, Cyclin `D1`, Cyclin E1, *HES* family, *HIP*, and *BMI1*. In glioma, stemness-related genes *Sox2*, *Olig2*, *Bmi1*, and *CD133* are upregulated via Hh signaling. CAF-secreted TGF-beta2 activates Gli2 under hypoxia. Non-canonical Hh signaling cooperates with Wnt to maintain cancer stem cell (CSC) stemness.

**Hippo pathway:** YAP1 and WWTR1 (encoding YAP and TAZ) undergo amplification in approximately 14% of HNSCCs, 16% of lung squamous carcinomas, 17% of cervical squamous carcinomas, and 15% of esophageal squamous carcinomas (from TCGA data across >9,000 tumors). YAP/TAZ induce PD-L1 expression across multiple tumor types. Hippo pathway target genes include *Axin2*, *MYC*, *Sox2*, *CTGF*, *CDK1*, and *Snail*.

**TGF-beta and FGF/FGFR:** TGF-beta drives stable EMT via Snail1/2, ZEB1/2, Twist, and *ids*. SMAD mutations occur in 60% of pancreatic cancer patients. FGFR3 overexpression activates ERK and boosts FGF2 production in HNSCC. FGFR1 increases AKT activation driving gefitinib resistance in NSCLC.

### 2. Dormant cancer cells mimic embryonic diapause

Cancer cells enter a reversible quiescent state — analogous to embryonic diapause — following antitumor treatment. Core drivers of dormancy include *Myc*, *mTOR*, *GPX4*, *Mex3a*, oxidative phosphorylation, and LINE-1 repression. Key dormancy markers: MHC class I downregulation shields cells from T-cell recognition; dormant myeloma cells mimic myeloid cells (CD169+ bone marrow macrophages) to evade immune surveillance. Niche factors maintaining dormancy include CAF-secreted HGF (activates PI3K–AKT), thrombospondin 1 from endothelial cells, and CCL5-recruited TAMs. The shift between chronic and acute hypoxia governs the dormancy-activation transition.

### 3. Oncofetal reprogramming of the TME

A pivotal finding (Sharma et al. 2020, *Cell*, cited as ref. 232) demonstrates onco-fetal reprogramming of endothelial cells to immunosuppressive macrophages in hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) using scRNA-seq across development and disease. The HCC ecosystem displayed characteristics reminiscent of fetal liver development, including re-emergence of fetal-associated endothelial cells (PLVAP/VEGFR2+) and fetal-like FOLR2+ TAMs. This "fetal-like TME" feature has been observed more broadly in cancer cells, establishing a mechanistic link between embryogenesis and oncogenesis.

TAMs exert immunosuppression via cathepsin B/S (PDAC), sustain CSC survival via juxtacrine signaling (breast cancer), and maintain tumor dormancy. MDSCs inhibit T-cell and NK-cell activity and govern premetastatic niche development. Key mediators: CCL5 (macrophage recruitment in breast cancer), GAS6/AXL (MDSC-mediated resistance), KRAS-IRF2-CXCL3 axis (immunotherapy resistance in CRC).

### 4. Overexpression of embryo/fetal ABC transporters

The human placenta expresses ABC transporters as efflux pumps protecting the fetus from maternal xenobiotics. In cancer, the same transporters — MDR1 (ABCB1/P-gp), MRP1 (ABCC1), and BCRP (ABCG2) — are overexpressed to efflux antitumor drugs, driving multidrug resistance. HIF-1 is a master regulator of all three. CAF-derived growth factors (ATF4, MK, TGF-beta1) and cytokines (IL-1beta, IL-6) also upregulate these transporters. N6-methyladenosine (m6A) RNA modification crosstalks with BCRP expression. In AML, coexpression of multiple ABC transporter genes significantly reduces overall survival.

### 5. Therapeutic implications

The review catalogs clinical-stage agents against each pathway:
- Wnt: anti-Frizzled mAbs (vantictumab/OMP-18R5), porcupine antagonists (WNT974/LGK974), beta-catenin inhibitors (CWP232291, LF3, KYA1797K)
- Notch: gamma-secretase inhibitors (PF-03084014, RO4929097), anti-DLL3 ADC (rovalpituzumab tesirine for SCLC)
- Hedgehog: SMO antagonists vismodegib and sonidegib (FDA-approved for BCC), glasdegib (AML)
- Hippo: LATS1/2 and MST1/2 activators; NEDD8-activating enzyme inhibitor pevonedistat (blocks YAP/TAZ)
- ABC transporters: third-generation P-gp inhibitors (tariquidar/XY9576), nanoparticle co-delivery of siRNA + anticancer drugs

## Relevance

### `H3` — This paper is a cornerstone for the oncofetal/developmental-byproduct hypothesis

This review provides the mechanistic foundation for `H3`: that the "neural gene" signal observed in the cbioportal top-mutated-genes list may be a *byproduct of global de-repression of developmental programs* rather than evidence of active neural hijacking (`H1`) or immune modulation (`H2`).

**The core `H3` mechanism illustrated by this paper:**

1. **Global chromatin de-repression during oncofetal reprogramming.** The review emphasizes that cancer cells reactivate "ancient, conserved developmental programs encoded in the genome." Embryonic programs are not tissue-specific at the chromatin level in the same way adult programs are: fetal cells, particularly early in development, express many genes broadly across lineages before tissue-specific silencing occurs. When oncofetal reprogramming de-represses developmental chromatin broadly, neural developmental genes — which are among the most numerous and evolutionarily ancient developmental gene classes in the genome — will inevitably be swept along. Many of the genes in the observed "neural" top-mutated list (NKAIN2, KCNIP4, TAFA2/FAM19A2, RIT2, CALN1, RBFOX1, LSAMP, SGCZ, OPCML) are regulated by developmentally conserved transcription factors (SOX2, which is explicitly named as a Hippo/Hedgehog target; MYCN, a Wnt target; BMI1, a Hedgehog target) — the same TFs that govern early neural lineage specification.

2. **Fetal brain is the largest developmental transcriptome.** The human brain, and particularly the fetal/embryonic brain, has the broadest transcriptome of any organ — expressing the highest fraction of all protein-coding genes of any tissue during development [UNVERIFIED — this claim is supported by BrainSpan and GTEx developmental data but not stated explicitly in this paper]. Oncofetal de-repression therefore preferentially liberates neural developmental genes simply because they constitute such a large fraction of the "developmentally silenced" gene space. Under gene-length confounding (longer genes mutate more), large neural genes like NKAIN2, LSAMP, and RBFOX1 would further be over-represented in any naive mutation-frequency count.

3. **SOX2 as the nexus.** The paper explicitly names SOX2 as a Hippo pathway target gene (Figure 5; also glioma stemness via Hedgehog, Figure 4). SOX2 is a master regulator of both neural stem cell identity and pluripotency. Its reactivation in cancer cells via Hedgehog/Hippo pathways predicts co-expression of its downstream transcriptional targets, many of which are neural developmental genes. Under `H3`, the causal arrow is: oncofetal reprogramming → SOX2/MYCN/BMI1 reactivation → broad neural developmental gene de-repression → apparent "neural gene" expression and/or mutation enrichment.

4. **The fetal-like TME finding (HCC scRNA-seq, ref. 232).** The Sharma 2020 *Cell* study cited here demonstrates that tumors do not merely express isolated fetal antigens — they reconstitute a full fetal-like ecosystem, including cell types (PLVAP/VEGFR2+ endothelial cells, FOLR2+ macrophages) that are characteristic of fetal but not adult liver. This is direct evidence that developmental-stage expression profiles, not tissue-of-origin labels, define oncofetal programs. This supports the `H3` operationalization strategy: use fetal-stage expression as the defining criterion for "neural gene" identity.

**Data-driven operationalization of `H3` — defining "neural genes" from developmental expression:**

This paper's framework directly motivates a label-free, data-driven definition of neural genes for the cbioportal analysis:

- A gene is "developmentally neural" if it shows peak expression in fetal/embryonic brain relative to (a) adult brain, and (b) fetal non-neural tissues, as measured by normalized expression in developmental atlases (BrainSpan, GTEx fetal, Human Cell Atlas fetal).
- This definition is tissue/stage-specific (fetal brain enriched) rather than adult-annotation-based (GO:neural, which collapses adult and developmental programs).
- Genes that are "neural" by adult GO annotation but not specifically fetal-brain enriched would be predicted by `H3` to show weaker association with pan-cancer mutation enrichment.
- Genes that are both adult-neural AND fetal-brain enriched AND also expressed in fetal non-neural tissues (i.e., broad developmental de-repression rather than neural-specific) would support `H3` over `H1`.

**Cross-hypothesis distinctions:**

- `H1` (neural hijacking) predicts: neural gene expression or mutation enrichment in tumors correlates with neural/neuroendocrine tumor subtypes, perineural invasion markers, axon guidance receptor expression, and functional connectivity features. Neural gene signal should be strongest in nervous-system-adjacent cancers.
- `H3` (developmental byproduct) predicts: neural gene mutation enrichment correlates with markers of dedifferentiation and stemness (SOX2, MYCN, BMI1, Wnt/Notch/Hedgehog pathway activity scores), is present broadly across cancer types (not concentrated in neural-adjacent tumors), and the genes involved are specifically enriched in fetal rather than adult neural expression profiles.

### Connections to other hypotheses

- **`H4` (CNS-cancer artifact):** If the neural gene signal is purely `H4`, it would be concentrated in glioma/brain studies. `H3` predicts the signal persists even after excluding brain/CNS cancer studies, driven by broad oncofetal de-repression — this is testable.
- **`H5` (misannotation):** Oncofetal reprogramming provides a biological mechanism by which genes annotated as "neural" in adults could be legitimately expressed/mutated in non-neural tumors, converting what looks like misannotation into a real biological signal (`H3`).

## Limitations

1. **No primary data; no neural gene analysis.** This is a broad review of oncofetal mechanisms with a drug-resistance and therapy focus. It does not analyze neural gene expression or mutation frequencies specifically, so the `H3` connection is an interpretive extrapolation by this project — clearly marked [SPECULATION] where it goes beyond the paper's explicit claims.

2. **Oncofetal antigens named (AFP, GPC3, CEA) are not neural genes.** The classic oncofetal antigens the review focuses on are hepatic/gut developmental markers, not neural. The review does not discuss neural oncofetal programs explicitly. The `H3` extrapolation rests on the general de-repression mechanism, not on a neural-specific oncofetal program documented in the paper.

3. **Pathway-specific focus may underrepresent the breadth of epigenetic reprogramming.** The review organizes evidence by signaling pathway (Wnt, Notch, etc.) rather than by chromatin/epigenetic state, which may understate the extent of genome-wide de-repression that would be needed to explain a broad "neural gene" sweep [SPECULATION].

4. **Publication bias toward druggable pathways.** The clinical-trial tables (Tables 1–3) focus on pathways with existing drug programs; pathways less amenable to pharmacological targeting (e.g., broad epigenetic reprogramming) receive less coverage.

5. **Chinese institution authorship concentration.** Most authors are affiliated with Chinese academic medical centers (West China Hospital/Sichuan University, Zhejiang University, Chongqing Medical University, Hainan Medical University). The review reflects the global literature but may have differential citation coverage.

6. **No quantitative synthesis.** As a narrative review, the paper does not perform meta-analysis or systematic evaluation of effect sizes, so the emphasis on individual studies may reflect selection rather than representative effect magnitudes.

## Follow-up

### Key papers to read for `H3` operationalization

- **Sharma et al. 2020** (*Cell* 183:2377–394, ref. 232 in this paper): Onco-fetal reprogramming of endothelial cells in HCC — the foundational scRNA-seq study showing tumors reconstitute fetal-like cell ecosystems. Critical for `H3`.
- **BrainSpan Atlas** (developmental transcriptomics, brainspan.org): Primary resource for fetal-stage-enriched neural gene definition; maps gene expression across prenatal developmental windows and brain regions.
- **GTEx developmental data / Human Cell Atlas fetal**: Complementary atlases for defining fetal-brain-enriched genes vs. fetal non-neural tissues.
- **The Hallmarks of Cancer review cited as ref. 529 in this paper** ("Hallmarks of Cancer: New Dimensions," *Cancer Discovery* 22:31–46): Introduces "unlocking phenotypic plasticity" as a hallmark, directly relevant to the oncofetal reprogramming concept.
- **Magnon et al. 2023** (`paper:Magnon2023`): Neural addiction of cancer — provides `H1` perspective for contrast with `H3`.
- **Venkatesh et al. 2019** (`paper:Venkatesh2019`): Neural activity drives glioma growth — `H1` mechanism with developmental gene involvement.
- **Keough et al. 2022** (`paper:Keough2022`): Developmental gene programs in cancer — direct bridge paper.

### Testable questions to distinguish `H1` (active neural hijacking) from `H3` (developmental byproduct)

**`Q1` — Cancer-type distribution test.** Under `H1`, neural gene mutation enrichment should be concentrated in tumors with neural/neuroendocrine characteristics or high perineural invasion rates. Under `H3`, enrichment should be pan-cancer and correlate with dedifferentiation markers (stemness scores, SOX2 expression), not with neural proximity. Test: stratify the observed "neural gene" mutation signal by cancer type and correlate with neural infiltration scores vs. stemness/Wnt pathway activity.

**`Q2` — Developmental stage specificity test.** Under `H3`, the enriched "neural genes" should be specifically expressed in fetal/embryonic brain rather than adult brain. Concretely: intersect the top-mutated genes (after gene-length normalization) with BrainSpan fetal-stage-enriched gene sets (prenatal > postnatal expression). A strong overlap would support `H3`; a pattern matching adult neural expression (adult brain > fetal) would weaken it.

**`Q3` — Oncofetal co-expression test.** If `H3` is operative, tumors with high expression of classic oncofetal markers (AFP, GPC3, CEA) or high Wnt/Hedgehog pathway scores should also show higher expression of the candidate "neural genes" (NKAIN2, KCNIP4, RBFOX1, etc.). This is testable in TCGA RNA-seq + mutation data within cBioPortal studies already in the pipeline.

**`Q4` — Gene-length confound decomposition.** Many of the observed "neural genes" are very long (NKAIN2: ~1.5 Mb genomic span; LSAMP: ~1.0 Mb; RBFOX1: ~2.3 Mb). After gene-length normalization (mutations per Mb of coding sequence or per kb of coding exons), does the neural gene signal persist? `H3` predicts it partially but not entirely disappears (because developmental de-repression affects expression, not just coding length). `H5` predicts it disappears nearly entirely.

**`Q5` — Stem cell state correlation.** In datasets with matched expression and mutation data, test whether samples classified as high-stemness (e.g., EREG, ALDH1A1, CD44hi) show higher mutation rates in the candidate neural genes. Under `H3`, stemness correlates with oncofetal reprogramming correlates with neural developmental gene de-repression. Under `H1`, no such correlation is expected independently of tumor histology.

**`Q6` — Neural gene TF binding enrichment.** Test whether the promoters/enhancers of the top-mutated "neural genes" are enriched for binding sites of known oncofetal TFs (SOX2, MYCN, OCT4, NANOG, BMI1) using public ChIP-seq data (ENCODE, ChIP-Atlas). Under `H3`, these TFs should regulate many of the candidate genes; under `H1`, regulation by neural-specific TFs (NEUROD1, ASCL1, BRN2) should dominate.

### Datasets and atlases for developmental-stage-based label-free neural gene definition

- **BrainSpan Atlas of the Developing Human Brain** (brainspan.org): RNA-seq and microarray data across 8–40 pcw prenatal stages and postnatal ages, 16 brain regions. Primary resource for fetal-brain-enriched gene definitions.
- **Human Cell Atlas Fetal** (humancellatlas.org): scRNA-seq of fetal tissues including brain, liver, kidney, lung — enables cross-tissue comparison to identify neural-specific vs. broadly fetal-expressed genes.
- **GTEx v8 developmental data** (gtexportal.org): Though adult-primary, the fetal brain RNA-seq from GTEx provides adult-vs-fetal brain comparison.
- **Allen Human Brain Atlas Developing** (developinghumanbrain.org): Microarray and RNA-seq data at prenatal developmental stages.
- **ENCODE developmental ChIP-seq / ATAC-seq**: Chromatin accessibility at fetal brain stages — defines which "neural genes" are in open vs. closed chromatin during normal fetal development vs. in adult cancer cells.
- **TCGA + cBioPortal (this project)**: Mutation frequency tables already generated can be directly intersected with BrainSpan fetal enrichment scores; this is the most tractable near-term test of `H3` vs. `H1`.
