# Topography of mutational signatures in human cancer

- **Authors:** Burçak Otlu, Marcos Díaz-Gay, Ian Vermes, Erik N. Bergstrom, Maria Zhivagui, Mark Barnes, Ludmil B. Alexandrov
- **Year:** 2023
- **Journal:** Cell Reports, 42, 112930
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.celrep.2023.112930
- **BibTeX key:** Otlu2023
- **Source:** PDF

## Key Contribution

This resource paper provides the most comprehensive evaluation to date of how genome architecture
shapes the accumulation of somatic mutations from distinct mutational processes. The authors
analyze 82,890,857 somatic mutations across 5,120 whole-genome-sequenced tumors from 40 cancer
types, integrating 516 tissue-matched topographical feature datasets from ENCODE (nucleosome
occupancy, 11 histone modifications, CTCF binding, replication timing, transcription/replication
strand asymmetry). They demonstrate that most of the 76 COSMICv3.3 signatures (78 SBS, 11 DBS,
18 ID) are systematically shaped by genomic topography, that signatures with related etiologies
show concordant topographic responses, and that some signatures exhibit periodic or
cancer-type-specific enrichments/depletions near topographic features. All results are
disseminated as an online resource in the COSMIC Signatures database (COSMICv3.3), enabling
per-signature, per-cancer-type exploration of topographic interactions [@Otlu2023].

## Methods

**Data.** 82,890,857 somatic mutations (79,269,539 SBS + 429,179 DBS + 3,192,139 ID) from
5,120 whole-genome-sequenced samples drawn from PCAWG, PCAWG other, and MUTOGRAPHS. Signatures
were identified by de novo extraction using SigProfilerExtractor followed by penalized NNLS
decomposition against COSMICv3.3 reference signatures. Per-sample per-mutation signature
probabilities were computed and used for all downstream analyses (only mutations with probability
≥ 0.50 for a signature were retained for that signature's analysis) [@Otlu2023].

**Topographic features.** 516 tissue-matched ENCODE datasets covering [@Otlu2023]:
- Transcription strand (transcribed vs. untranscribed strand via SigProfilerMatrixGenerator)
- Replication strand (leading vs. lagging, annotated from wavelet-smoothed Repli-seq data)
- Genic/intergenic regions
- Replication timing (10 deciles, Repli-seq data)
- Nucleosome occupancy (MNase-seq; occupancy profile within ±1,000 bp of each mutation)
- CTCF binding sites (ChIP-seq; occupancy within ±1,000 bp)
- 11 histone modifications (ChIP-seq): H2AFZ, H3K4me1, H3K4me2, H3K4me3, H3K9ac, H3K9me3,
  H3K27ac, H3K27me3, H3K36me3, H3K79me2, H4K20me1

**Statistical framework.** All observed mutation patterns compared to simulations (100 runs per
sample) generated by SigProfilerSimulator, preserving trinucleotide context and chromosome-level
mutational burden. Fisher's exact test for strand asymmetries, z-tests for occupancy/abundance
analyses; all FDR-corrected by Benjamini-Hochberg. Analyses run both pan-cancer and per-cancer
type [@Otlu2023].

**Software.** SigProfilerExtractor v1.1.0, SigProfilerMatrixGenerator v1.1.31,
SigProfilerSimulator v1.1.2, SigProfilerClusters v1.0.11, SigProfilerTopography v1.0.70,
SigProfilerTopographyCombined (all publicly available on AlexandrovLab GitHub).

## Key Findings

### Transcription strand asymmetry
- Most signatures attributed to **exogenous mutational processes** showed transcription strand
  bias (enrichment on the transcribed strand), consistent with TC-NER repairing bulky adducts —
  this includes SBS4/DBS2 (tobacco), SBS16 (alcohol), SBS24 (aflatoxin), SBS25/31/35/DBS5
  (chemotherapy), SBS29 (tobacco chewing), SBS32 (azathioprine).
- **UV-attributed SBS7a/b/c/d and DBS1** were the only known exogenous processes to show
  enrichment on the **untranscribed** strand, consistent with UV damage to cytosine.
- Clock-like SBS5 also showed transcription strand bias with enrichment on the transcribed
  strand; mechanism unknown.
- Some cancer-type-specific transcription asymmetries noted (e.g., SBS44 lacked
  transcription asymmetry for C>T/T>A in esophageal SCC).

### Replication strand asymmetry
- Signatures attributed to **aberrant/defective endogenous replication polymerases** showed
  strong replication strand bias: SBS10a/b/DBS3 (POLE exonuclease domain mutations), SBS9
  (POLH / polymerase eta / infidelity), SBS10c (POLD1 deficiency).
- **MMR-deficiency-associated signatures** (SBS6, SBS14, SBS15, SBS20, SBS21, SBS26, SBS44,
  ID1) showed predominant replication strand bias on either leading or lagging strand.
- APOBEC-attributed SBS2 and SBS13 both showed enrichment on the lagging strand in all cancer
  types, consistent with APOBEC3 deamination of ssDNA formed during replication on the lagging
  strand template.

### Strand-coordinated mutagenesis (clustered mutations)
- SBS7a (UV) showed the highest strand-coordinated mutagenesis: up to 40 consecutive mutations
  on the same strand.
- APOBEC-attributed SBS2 and SBS13 also showed strand-coordinated mutagenesis (up to 21
  consecutive mutations).
- Also SBS17b (unknown etiology), SBS10a/b (POLE deficiency), SBS4 (tobacco smoking), SBS26
  (defective MMR), SBS28 (unknown etiology).

### Replication timing
- **Most SBSs enriched in late-replicating regions** (39/40 cancer types for aggregate SBS).
- SBS3 (homologous recombination deficiency), DBS2, and ID1 consistently enriched in
  late-replicating regions.
- At least **seven signatures enriched in early-replicating regions**: ID17 (likely TOP2A
  mutations), SBS11 (temozolomide), SBS16/ID11 (alcohol), SBS6/SBS15 (mismatch repair), SBS84
  (AID / cytidine deaminases).
- **APOBEC SBS13:** in samples with high APOBEC3 burden, SBS13 was enriched in late-replicating
  regions (10/17 cancer types); in samples with low APOBEC3 burden, it showed no such
  enrichment — attributed to DNA translesion polymerase activity in hypermutated samples.
- **ID8** showed cancer-type-specific replication timing associations; in esophageal SCC
  samples harboring *TOP2A* p.K743N, ID8 was enriched in early-replicating regions, linking
  ID8 in this context to a specific TOP2A mutation.
- **SBS28** (unknown etiology) was split into SBS28a (POLE deficiency context, late-replicating,
  enriched at nucleosomes, leading-strand bias, strand-coordinated mutagenesis) and SBS28b
  (lung and stomach, no POLE link) — a topography-driven signature disambiguation.

### Nucleosome occupancy
- **Aggregate SBSs, DBSs, and IDs all depleted near nucleosomes** (compared to simulation)
  across most cancer types.
- **SBS1 (clock-like / spontaneous deamination)** closely mimicked simulated data and showed
  nucleosome signal consistent with simulation — 36/36 cancer types; the exception is its
  cancer-type-specific depletion behavior, which was tissue-dependent.
- **DBS2** was consistently depleted near nucleosomes in 13/13 cancer types.
- Some signatures (SBS5, SBS8, SBS40) were largely unaffected by nucleosome occupancy.
- Signatures SBS17a/b (unknown etiology) showed periodic mutation rate variation around
  nucleosomes, with SBS17a showing similar periodic behavior — suggesting possible reactive
  oxygen species damage to deoxyribonucleotide triphosphate pools.

### CTCF binding
- Some signatures **consistently depleted** at CTCF binding sites pan-cancer (SBS1, SBS9,
  SBS10a/b, SBS15, SBS37, SBS84, SBS85); others **commonly enriched** (SBS3, SBS5,
  SBS7a/b/d, SBS12, SBS17a/b, SBS18, SBS22, SBS40; DBS1; ID5, ID6, ID8, ID9).
- ID signatures were enriched at CTCF sites in the majority of cancer types.
- **SBS15** (microsatellite instability) was strongly depleted at CTCF binding sites.
- SBS17a/b showed striking enrichment at CTCF binding sites in all cancer types — in contrast
  to simulated data, suggesting CTCF binding is mechanistically linked to the process generating
  SBS17a/b.

### Histone modifications
- Most mutational signatures were either unaffected or depleted near histone marks pan-cancer.
- Notable exceptions: APOBEC3-associated SBS2 and SBS13, AID-associated SBS84/85, and
  POLH-attributed SBS9 were generally enriched near most histone modifications.
- SBS1 consistently depleted near H3K9me3 across cancer types, with cancer-type-specific
  enrichment in CNS tumors.
- ID1 enriched near H3K27ac in medulloblastoma and lymphoma, depleted in stomach and prostate.

### Case study: clustered mutations in B-cell malignancies
- In 288 WGS B-cell malignancies, non-clustered, *omikli* (short clustered), and *kataegis*
  (longer hypermutated runs) mutations showed strikingly different topographic profiles.
- Non-clustered mutations showed minor nucleosome periodicity and slight CTCF depletion.
- *Omikli* and *kataegis* were both very strongly depleted at CTCF sites (driven by APOBEC3
  activity) and enriched in early-replicating regions (*kataegis* most strongly).
- Importantly, pan-cancer *omikli* and *kataegis* events were almost exclusively generated by
  APOBEC3 in B-cell malignancies, while AID drove non-clustered mutations.

## Relevance

**Direct relevance to hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and (agnostic covariate↔signature-exposure association):**

The paper provides the authoritative topographic portrait of all COSMICv3.3 signatures, which
is highly relevant to hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and in several ways:

1. **Positive-control grounding.** The paper confirms and extends the canonical
   exposure-to-signature map in a systematic, WGS-powered framework. The transcription strand
   asymmetry results for SBS4 (tobacco/lagging enrichment on transcribed strand in most cancers),
   SBS2/13 (APOBEC/lagging replication strand), UV SBS7 (untranscribed strand enrichment), and
   MMR/MSI signatures (distinctive replication strand biases) all represent validated
   topographic fingerprints of known etiologies. Any agnostic association scan that recovers
   these patterns using orthogonal covariates (e.g., transcription level, replication origin
   proximity) rather than direct spectrum matching would constitute a strong positive control for
   the positive-control arm.

2. **Topographic features as additional covariates.** For hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and, the analysis currently targets
   clinical covariates (age, sex, stage, MSI) and mRNA expression modules. This paper suggests
   that genome-architecture features (replication timing, nucleosome context, CTCF proximity)
   are signature-specific and consistent enough to serve as additional mechanistic covariates
   in the association model — or as mechanistic validators of candidate hits.

3. **Signature disambiguation.** The paper demonstrates that topography can split ambiguous
   signatures (SBS28 → SBS28a/b) and disambiguate underlying processes. In the hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and context,
   this warns that signatures assigned at COSMIC-reference level may conflate distinct processes
   that would separate under more refined decomposition; the association model should be
   interpreted in light of possible heterogeneity within a nominal signature.

4. **APOBEC biology.** The consistent lagging-strand enrichment of SBS2/13
   mechanistically grounds the hypothesis that APOBEC3 expression (particularly APOBEC3A/B)
   should associate with SBS2/13 burden within tissue — the ssDNA replication substrate is
   tissue-invariant. The paper also provides the APOBEC-classification formula
   (log-ratio of SBS2/13 burden to non-APOBEC burden) used to stratify samples, which could be
   directly adopted in hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and.

5. **Clock-like signatures.** SBS1 mimics simulated topographic behavior closely, while
   SBS5 is largely unaffected by nucleosome occupancy, replication timing, and most histone
   marks. The authors note several signatures with unknown etiology show unexpected periodic or
   CTCF-linked patterns (SBS17a/b). These patterns represent candidate mechanistic clues that
   could guide which covariates to prioritize in the hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and discovery scan.

6. **MSI/MMR signatures.** SBS15 strongly depleted at CTCF binding sites; multiple MMR
   signatures show distinct replication strand biases. Since MSI status is a clinical covariate
   already in the pipeline, the hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and positive control could test MMR-loss→SBS6/15/26 recovery
   and compare topographic fingerprints as orthogonal confirmation.

**Cross-study meta-analysis relevance:** The PCAWG-based topographic resource uses the same
set of COSMIC reference signatures targeted by the cBioPortal pipeline. The observation that
signatures of related etiologies cluster topographically provides an independent validation
basis: if the cross-study mutation meta-analysis produces frequency tables for driver genes
enriched in specific cancer types, the co-occurrence with known signature exposures (which have
predictable topographic footprints) can be cross-checked against this resource.

## Limitations

- Topographic ENCODE features were drawn from cell-line and reference-tissue ChIP-seq datasets
  that may not perfectly represent the tumor's own epigenome at the time of mutation
  accumulation. The authors explicitly flag this: the features are proxies and do not account
  for epigenome remodeling during tumor evolution.
- The study analyzes signatures at reference-COSMIC resolution; signatures with mixed or
  overlapping etiologies (e.g., SBS28 before its disambiguation) may produce averaged
  topographic signals that obscure sub-process behavior.
- Cancer types with low mutational burden could not be evaluated for DBS and ID signatures
  (requires ≥ 1,000 mutations unambiguously attributed); these remain undercharacterized.
- The topographic analysis is observational and comparative (real vs. simulated), not causal;
  enrichment patterns inform mechanism but do not prove it.
- Future work needed to assess whether topographic profiles evolve during tumor development
  (clonal vs. subclonal mutation context not separated).

  variable.

  extraction on aggregated cohort; does it surface factors not in COSMIC?).
