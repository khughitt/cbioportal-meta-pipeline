## Key Contribution

This 2025 review from Jin lab (Institute of Cancer Neuroscience, Lanzhou University) synthesizes the emerging field of "brain-body crosstalk" in cancer, arguing that tumors are not localized genetic lesions but systemic hijackers of whole-organism homeostasis. The central thesis is that malignancies co-opt the nervous system's bidirectional brain-body communication channels — specifically the HPA axis, the sympatho-adrenal medullary (SAM) axis, the hypothalamic-sympathetic-adrenal (HSA) axis, the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis, the vagus-gut axis, and the neuro-immune and neuro-metabolic axes — to remodel the tumor microenvironment (TME), evade immune destruction, and promote proliferation, invasion, and metastasis. Reciprocally, the brain-body crosstalk system can mount anti-tumor homeostatic responses that the host organism deploys to resist tumor expansion. The review proposes that this bidirectional interplay constitutes a new conceptual framework for cancer treatment, positioning neuromodulation, circadian rhythm targeting, neural blockade, and comprehensive psycho-exercise interventions as rational therapeutic strategies.

## Methods

This is a narrative review (no original data). The authors synthesize findings from:
- Mouse genetic and pharmacological models (xenograft, syngeneic, transgenic; prostate, breast, pancreatic, colorectal, glioma cancer models)
- In vitro mechanistic studies (cell lines; receptor agonist/antagonist pharmacology)
- Selected clinical trials and observational cohorts (propranolol in breast/ovarian/pancreatic cancer; VNS; chrono-immunotherapy)
- Two summary tables: Table 1 catalogs experimental tools for studying neural-tumor crosstalk (Advillin-Cre transgenic mice; Nav1.8-Cre; Ngf knock-in; 6-OHDA; RTX; capsaicin; botulinum toxin; AAV; optogenetics; chemogenetics; EMF; VNS; Trace-n-seq); Table 2 catalogs 20+ ongoing/completed clinical trials targeting neuro-tumor axes (propranolol, larotrectinib, talampanel, ONC201/DRD2 antagonists, VNS, somatostatin analogs, carvedilol)

No bioinformatics or genomic analysis is performed. No neural-gene definition is provided from data.

## Key Findings

### Six homeostatic perceptual systems that tumors hijack

The authors organize brain-body crosstalk around six supervisory processes:

1. **Interoception** — brain monitoring of internal body state via neural/humoral afferent pathways. Tumors corrupt interoceptive signals (cytokines, metabolites, hormones, tissue architecture) detected by sensory/vagal nerves.
2. **Nociception** — pain sensing via NGF, substance P (SP), CGRP. Breast cancer cells activate sensory neurons, inducing SP release that promotes tumor growth, invasion, and metastasis. Perineural invasion (PNI) in PDAC and prostate cancer hijacks nociceptive nerve pathways.
3. **Neuroception** — nervous system's threat-detection cascade; modulates autonomic output to peripheral organs.
4. **Endocriception** — HPA axis-mediated monitoring/adjustment of circulating hormones (glucocorticoids, ACTH, GnRH, sex steroids).
5. **Metaboception** — POMC/NPY/AgRP hypothalamic neurons regulating energy homeostasis via leptin, ghrelin, insulin; tumors subvert appetite-regulating circuits to drive cachexia.
6. **Immunoception** — brain-immune bidirectional signaling; brainstem (NTS) sets inflammatory set-point via vagal afferent arcs; tumors exploit this to establish immunosuppressive TME.

### Key pro-tumor brain-body axes (tumor exploits the organism)

- **HPA axis:** Tumors stimulate cortisol/glucocorticoid release (via IL-6, TNF-α signaling to hypothalamus → ACTH → adrenal). Glucocorticoids then suppress NF-κB in tumors and immune cells, reducing anti-tumor inflammation. Simultaneously, chronic HPA activation promotes immunosuppression.
- **SAM / sympatho-adrenal axis:** Stress-induced catecholamines (norepinephrine [NE], epinephrine) from sympathetic nerves or adrenal medulla act on β-adrenergic receptors (β-AR, encoded by ADRB2/ADRB3) on tumor cells to promote proliferation, survival (Bcl-2/Bax axis), invasion, and angiogenesis. In PDAC, sympathetic NE drives β-AR → CREB → NF-κB signaling. In prostate cancer, Adrb2/Adrb3 double-knockout substantially suppressed tumor development.
- **Vagus-gut-tumor axis:** The vagus nerve is the central conduit of the gut-brain axis. VNS reduces tumor growth in colorectal mouse models via the cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway (CAP), suppressing TNF-α and IL-6. Gut microbial metabolites (SCFAs, e.g., butyrate) exert anti-tumor effects via epigenetic (HDAC inhibition) and direct apoptotic mechanisms.
- **HPG axis:** Estrogen and androgen receptor signaling (ER, AR) modulated by GnRH/ACTH. GnRH analogs (leuprolide) downregulate AR to induce prostate cancer apoptosis; HPG axis also potentiates CD8+T cell glycolytic/IFN-γ activity via ER-β signaling.
- **HSA (hypothalamic-sympathetic-adrenal) axis:** Links psychosocial stressors to adipose tissue regulation; HSA activation induces white adipose atrophy, BDNF upregulation, leptin suppression — collectively limiting the stromal-cell pool available for tumor recruitment in preclinical liver cancer models.
- **Neuro-endocrine-tumor axis:** Pituitary GH, PRL, TSH, and irisin (from skeletal muscle) all exhibit context-dependent anti- or pro-tumor functions.

### Key anti-tumor brain-body axes (organism fights back)

- **HPA anti-tumor:** Acute glucocorticoid pulses suppress pro-inflammatory NF-κB and IL-6/TNF-α, transiently slowing tumor progression. Binding to glucocorticoid receptors in tumor cells modulates Bcl-2/Bax expression.
- **Acute SAM activation:** Acute (not chronic) epinephrine/NE activates β2-AR/cAMP/PKA → p21 upregulation (G1/S block), NF-κB inhibition, and BAX/BAK-dependent apoptosis in hepatocellular carcinoma. Epinephrine also upregulates NKG2D on NK cells, promoting granzyme B-mediated tumor cytotoxicity.
- **Parasympathetic/vagal:** ACh released by parasympathetic nerves binds α7nAChR on tumor cells (suppressing proliferation via PI3K/Akt/mTOR inhibition) and on immune cells (suppressing TNF-α, IFN-γ, IL-1β; increasing CD8+T cell infiltration).
- **Brain-gut SCFAs:** Butyrate and other SCFAs enhance PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitor efficacy and NK cell activity.

### Named genes (mechanistically invoked)

The following genes are named in mechanistic context (not as somatic mutation data):

- **ADRB2, ADRB3** (β2-, β3-adrenergic receptors): prostate cancer tumor promotion; knockout suppresses xenograft growth
- **CHRNA7** (α7 nAChR): anti-tumor parasympathetic signaling; ACh-mediated suppression of proliferation and pro-inflammatory cytokines
- **NGF** (nerve growth factor): drives perineural invasion; sensory neuron activation in breast cancer; TrkA inhibition target (larotrectinib)
- **BDNF** (brain-derived neurotrophic factor): PDAC/colorectal/prostate cells release BDNF precursors to recruit autonomic innervation; HSA axis upregulates BDNF in hypothalamus
- **VEGF** (vascular endothelial growth factor): HIF-1α-mediated angiogenesis in hypoxic niche; hypoxia upregulates Slit2 to promote axon guidance and tumor innervation
- **HIF-1α** (HIF1A): hypoxic TME sentinel; NE activates HIF-1α via β-AR; neuro-hypoxia positive feedback loop
- **SLIT2**: axon guidance molecule expressed in tumor vasculature; upregulated by hypoxia; drives neural innervation of breast cancer
- **SP (TAC1/substance P)**: neuropeptide released by activated sensory neurons; promotes breast tumor growth, invasion, metastasis
- **CGRP (CALCA)**: nociceptor-released; calcitonin gene-related peptide; promotes oral squamous cell carcinoma growth via autophagy under low-glucose stress
- **NF-κB** (RELA/NFKB1): core inflammatory signaling; SAM axis NE activates; glucocorticoids suppress
- **STAT3**: sustained by IL-6/TNF-α in tumor-promoting inflammation; fuels malignant cell proliferation, angiogenesis, immune evasion
- **ARG1** (arginase 1): upregulated by NE in tumor-associated macrophages (immunosuppression)
- **CCL2/CCL5**: Schwann cell-secreted (CCL2 in PDAC PNI); CD8+T lymphocyte-derived (CCL5 in NF1 LGG); drive monocyte recruitment and tumor growth
- **GDF15**: secreted by tumors, acts on CNS to drive anorexia and cachexia
- **IL-6**: tumor-secreted; signals to brain (HPA activation, neuroinflammation); activates STAT3
- **BCL-2, BAX**: acute SAM-mediated apoptosis regulation in tumor cells
- **POMC, NPY, AgRP**: hypothalamic energy-sensing neurons; tumor-induced metaboception disruption
- **GnRH** (GNRH1): HPG axis; GnRH analogs downregulate AR in prostate cancer
- **MCT4 (SLC16A3)**: monocarboxylate transporter 4; inhibition (syrosingopine) disrupts neuro-metabolic lactate shuttling in glioma
- **PIEZO1**: mechanosensitive ion channel in sensory nerve terminals; ECM stiffening activates; transmits pain signals to CNS
- **TRPV1**: capsaicin receptor in sensory neurons; AAV-mediated knockdown in DRG blocks nociceptive signaling fueling tumor progression
- **AMPA receptors (GRIA1-4)**: synaptic inputs in glioma enhance invasive capacity; calcium-permeable AMPA receptors in neuron-glioma synapses mediate activity-dependent tumor growth; talampanel (AMPAR inhibitor) in GBM clinical trials
- **NMDAR (GRIN2B etc.)**: breast cancer brain metastases co-opt NMDA receptor signaling to enhance growth (NMDAR-KO prolongs brain-metastasis-free survival)
- **NEUROLIGIN-3 (NLGN3)**: shed from neurons upon photostimulation; promotes low-grade glioma growth via IGF-1 signaling
- **IGF-1 (IGF1)**: olfactory sensory neuron-secreted; activity-dependent glioma growth
- **NKG2D (KLRK1)**: NK cell activating receptor; upregulated by epinephrine via β2-AR
- **BMAL1**: circadian clock gene; altered in hepatocellular carcinoma SCN perturbation

### Innervated TME niche: recruited neural progenitors

- Breast and prostate adenocarcinomas recruit doublecortin (DCX)-expressing neural progenitor cells from the subventricular zone (SVZ) of the brain. These migrate through BBB into tumors and differentiate into adrenergic neurons that drive tumor progression.
- PDAC: tumor-secreted factors reprogramme Schwann cells into "tumor-activated" state expressing CCL2, creating microtracks for cancer cell migration along nerve pathways.
- Glioma: functional synapses form between tumor cells and neurons (AMPA/NMDA-dependent); electrochemical amplification loop drives tumor growth.

### Key therapeutic findings (from clinical trials in Table 2)

- **Propranolol (β-blocker):** Reduced breast cancer recurrence risk (correlates with increased immune infiltration, decreased metastasis markers); Phase 1-2 ongoing in pancreatic, ovarian, bladder, melanoma.
- **Larotrectinib (TrkA/NGF inhibitor):** Reduces neural invasion; in Lar@NP-OMVs nanocarrier system reduced PDAC tumor volume 70%, extended median survival 40% vs gemcitabine alone.
- **VNS (vagus nerve stimulation):** Phase 2 RCT showed CAP activation suppresses IL-6, TNF-α; enhances immune checkpoint inhibitor efficacy.
- **ONC201 (DRD2/DRD3 antagonist):** Phase 1/2 trials in H3K27M glioma, neuroendocrine tumors (56% response rate completed trial), DIPG.
- **Talampanel (AMPAR regulator):** Phase 2 GBM — improved overall survival vs historical controls.
- **Rimegepant (CGRP-blocking):** Reduces cancer cell autophagy and enhances anti-angiogenic efficacy (preclinical).
- **Olanzapine (antipsychotic):** Suppresses medial prefrontal cortex-NE-CLOCK neuroendocrine axis; attenuates lung cancer stemness.

## Relevance

**This paper is the primary literature anchor for Hypothesis H1 (top-down neural circuitry hijacking).** It provides the most comprehensive and up-to-date mechanistic framing for why tumors may exploit the nervous system systemically.

**H1 — Hijack top-down neural circuitry (STRONGLY supported):** The paper's entire thesis is that tumors subvert the CNS's homeostatic regulatory circuits (HPA, SAM, HSA, HPG, vagal, neuro-metabolic, neuro-immune) to create a systemic macroenvironment favorable to tumor survival. This is the strongest "top-down circuitry" framing available in the 2025 literature. The key H1 mechanism is: tumor secretes IL-6/GDF15/TNF-α → activates HPA/SAM → releases glucocorticoids/NE → remodels TME and suppresses anti-tumor immunity.

**H2 — Immune modulation (SUPPORTED as a downstream mechanism of H1):** The paper explicitly shows that neural circuits modulate immune function — NE remodels TAMs (ARG1 upregulation, IL-10 upregulation, TNF-α/IL-12 downregulation), suppresses NK cytotoxicity (perforin/granzyme B reduction), and impairs CD8+T function. Vagal/parasympathetic ACh conversely enhances anti-tumor immunity. H2 is thus mechanistically downstream of H1 in this framework: the primary signal is neural (top-down), and immune modulation is one major effector arm.

**H3 — Aberrant developmental/oncofetal expression (PARTIALLY supported):** The paper's innervated-niche section describes brain-derived neural progenitor recruitment to tumors, and glioma cell co-option of developmental synaptic mechanisms (neuroligin-3, AMPA receptors). This suggests that neural developmental gene expression programs can be re-activated in the tumor context. However, the paper does not directly address H3 for the specific neural genes observed in the cBioPortal top-mutated gene list.

**H4 — CNS/brain cancer artifact (PARTIALLY addressed):** The paper focuses heavily on brain tumors (glioma, GBM, brain metastases) as paradigmatic cases of neural-tumor communication but also extensively discusses peripheral tumors (PDAC, prostate, breast, colorectal). The CNS-tumor interactions described (AMPA/NMDA synapses, gliomagenesis, neurotrophic factor circuits) are mechanistically distinct from peripheral tumor neural signaling. The presence of brain-tumor studies in cBioPortal datasets could contribute elevated neural gene mutation frequencies if brain cancer studies disproportionately represent the corpus — H4 remains plausible and is not ruled out by this paper.

**H5 — Misannotation (not addressed):** The paper does not discuss gene-length confounding, misannotation, or bioinformatic artifacts in mutation frequency tables.

**Critical interpretive point for the neural-gene mutation frequency observation:** This paper's framework is almost entirely about *systemic signaling by neural circuits acting on tumors*, not about *somatic mutation of neural genes within tumors*. The mechanisms described (catecholamines, acetylcholine, neuropeptides, HPA hormones, vagal signals) operate through protein-ligand interactions, not genomic alteration. The paper does not propose, and would not predict, that the neurally-relevant genes (NKAIN2, KCNIP4, TAFA2, RIT2, CALN1, RBFOX1, LSAMP, SGCZ, OPCML) would be highly somatically mutated within tumors. Those genes would be relevant in H1 terms only if their somatic loss in tumor cells impaired a suppressive neural signal, or if they are large-exon genes (gene-length confounding, H5). The H1 hijacking reviewed here is mediated by signaling axes that do not require tumor-cell mutation of neural genes; the tumor exploits pre-existing brain-body signaling through secreted factors and nerve infiltration.

**No data-driven definition of "neural genes" is provided.** The review classifies genes by functional role (neurotransmitter receptor, neuropeptide, synaptic molecule) drawn from neuroscience literature, not from a systematic transcriptomic or genomic analysis of gene expression in tumors vs normal brain.

## Limitations

- **Narrative review with strong citation selectivity.** With >266 references, the authors can selectively emphasize supportive studies; contradictory evidence (e.g., cases where sympathetic activation inhibits tumor growth) is present but receives less emphasis than the hijacking framework.
- **Animal model heavy.** The majority of mechanistic claims come from mouse models (xenograft, syngeneic, transgenic). Translation to human cancer biology is inferred, not systematically validated.
- **Conflation of CNS tumors with peripheral tumors.** Glioma neural signaling (AMPA/NMDA synapses, neural progenitor recruitment) and peripheral tumor neural infiltration (PNI in PDAC, adrenergic signaling in prostate) are mechanistically distinct phenomena treated within one framework.
- **Correlation vs causation in axis labeling.** The "axes" (HPA, SAM, HSA, etc.) are defined by anatomical pathways, but the direction of causality (tumor → brain vs brain → tumor) is variable across cited studies and is not always resolved.
- **No omics data, no neural-gene definition.** The review does not analyze transcriptomic or genomic data to define which genes constitute "neural" identity in the tumor context. The gene list is derived from neuroscience literature, not data-driven clustering.
- **No consideration of gene-length bias.** The large-exon neural genes (RBFOX1: ~2.2 Mb genomic span; NKAIN2, KCNIP4, LSAMP: all >300 kb) that dominate our top-mutated gene list are not discussed. The paper would not predict or explain why these specific genes appear at the top of a somatic mutation frequency ranking.
- **Chronotherapy and psychotherapy data are largely preclinical or early-phase.** Table 2 shows most neuro-targeted trials are ongoing or reported as "not reported."
- **Potential institutional bias.** Authors are from Institute of Cancer Neuroscience, Lanzhou University — a center with strong commitment to the brain-body crosstalk paradigm; this may inflate the framing of neural effects as central to tumor biology relative to other perspectives.

## Model / Tool Availability

No software tool, model, or dataset is released with this review. Table 1 catalogs publicly available experimental tools for neural-tumor research:

- **Genetic mouse models:** Advillin-Cre, Nav1.8-Cre, Ngf knock-in (ROSA26-inserted), TrkAF592A/TrkBF616A knock-in, TrkbCreERT2 knock-in
- **Chemical tools:** 6-OHDA (sympathectomy), RTX (permanent sensory denervation), capsaicin (temporary TRPV1 ablation), botulinum toxin (cholinergic denervation)
- **Viral/genetic:** AAV (retrograde neural tracing + gene delivery), optogenetic ion channels (ChR2-based), chemogenetic receptors (DREADDs)
- **Physical modalities:** Electromagnetic fields (EMF/TTF), vagus nerve stimulation (VNS)
- **Circuit tracing:** Trace-n-seq (retrograde axonal tracing + single-cell transcriptomics; Lanzhou group)
- **Nanocarrier:** Lar@NP-OMVs (neuron-binding peptide NP41 + TrkA inhibitor larotrectinib + bacterial outer membrane vesicles; preclinical PDAC)
- **Funding:** High-Level Talent Introduction Funds, First Hospital of Lanzhou University

**Testable questions for the cbioportal project:**

1. **Are the top-mutated neural genes (NKAIN2, KCNIP4, RBFOX1, LSAMP, SGCZ, OPCML) large-exon genes?** If yes, gene-length normalization (by coding sequence length or exon count) should dramatically reduce their apparent mutation frequency — H5 (misannotation/length confound) is the null. Check against UniProt protein lengths already in `data/uniprotkb_hsapiens_protein_lengths.tsv.gz`.

2. **Do the top-mutated neural genes correlate with CNS/brain cancer study enrichment?** If GBM, LGG, or brain-metastasis-enriched studies are over-represented in the cBioPortal corpus, the H4 artifact hypothesis predicts the neural gene signal should diminish when those studies are excluded. Test by computing gene-mutation frequency with vs without brain cancer studies.

3. **Are ADRB2/ADRB3 somatically mutated at elevated frequency in any cancer type?** Wang 2025 highlights ADRB2/ADRB3 as mechanistically central to pro-tumor sympathetic signaling. If these genes appear at high somatic mutation frequency, it could support H1 (tumors mutating signaling axes to constitutively activate them) rather than H5.

4. **Does CHRNA7 (α7nAChR) mutation correlate with reduced immune infiltration proxies?** Given its anti-tumor role, somatic CHRNA7 loss could be a mechanism of immune evasion. Testable using TMB or mutation burden as a surrogate.

5. **Can a label-free "neural gene" set be defined from gene ontology / pathway databases (GO:0007268 synaptic signaling, GO:0045202 synapse, GO:0019228 neuronal action potential)?** Cross-reference against the full pipeline gene-frequency table to determine whether neural GO terms are systematically enriched in top-mutated genes independently of gene length.

**Datasets/tools for label-free neural-gene definition (not in this paper but implied by it):**
- Gene Ontology (GO) biological process / cellular component terms (GO:0007268, GO:0007399, GO:0045202)
- COMPARTMENTS database (Jensen Lab) for subcellular localization evidence scores
- Allen Brain Atlas expression data — genes with high brain-specific expression
- Human Protein Atlas "tissue specificity" annotations (Brain category)
- Reactome pathways: "Neuronal system", "Transmission across chemical synapses", "Axon guidance"
- None of these provide a somatic-mutation-context definition; a data-driven definition requires comparing tumor vs matched normal transcriptomes to identify neural program re-activation (H3) — outside this paper's scope
