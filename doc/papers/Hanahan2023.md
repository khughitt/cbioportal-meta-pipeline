# Cancer hallmarks intersect with neuroscience in the tumor microenvironment

- **Year:** 2023
- **Journal:** Cancer Cell
- **Volume/Issue/Pages:** 41(3):573-580

## Key Contribution

This Cancer Cell perspective, co-authored by the originator of the hallmarks of cancer framework (Hanahan) and the leading cancer neuroscience lab (Monje), makes a formal case for integrating the nervous system into the hallmarks conceptualization. The central argument is twofold: (1) tumor innervation — neurons and their axonal projections — constitutes a functionally important, hallmark-inducing constituent of the TME across multiple cancer types; and (2) co-opted neuronal signaling circuits aberrantly active in cancer cells themselves constitute a distinctive new "enabling characteristic" of cancer (analogous to genome instability and tumor-promoting inflammation), one that is broad enough to span cancers of the brain, skin, head and neck, breast, lung, stomach, colon, pancreas, and prostate. By mapping neuroscience-cancer connections onto six of the ten canonical hallmark parameters, the paper provides the canonical reference framing for why "neural signaling" belongs in the cancer hallmarks schema, explicitly positioning it as a candidate emerging hallmark/enabling characteristic. This is the most important single citation for placing the cancer neuroscience phenomenon in the context of a universal cancer biology framework, making it directly relevant for narrative framing in any cross-cancer-type study.

## Methods

This is a theoretical perspective / narrative review — no original experimental data are presented. The authors survey and synthesize published evidence from preclinical models (GEMMs, optogenetics, xenografts, cell co-culture) and human clinical correlates (expression data, retrospective survival analyses, histopathology). The paper is organized by hallmark capability and draws on approximately 39 primary references. Two conceptual figures are presented: Figure 1 maps cell types in the TME (including neurons) to four cancer hallmarks; Figure 2 maps specific neuronal signaling pathways co-opted in cancer cells; Figure 3 (the hallmarks "wheel") highlights the six hallmarks with demonstrated neural connections.

## Key Findings

### Hallmark 1 — Sustaining proliferative signaling

**In CNS tumors:** Glutamatergic neuronal activity drives glioma (GBM, DIPG, optic pathway glioma) proliferation via paracrine mitogens including BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) and NLGN3 (neuroligin-3). NLGN3 is shed from neurons and OPCs in an activity-regulated fashion via ADAM10 sheddase, activating PI3K-mTOR and other oncogenic pathways in glioma cells; NLGN3 also establishes an autocrine loop in glioma cells themselves. Beyond gliomas, NLGN3 autocrine signaling promotes neuroblastoma proliferation via PI3K-mTOR. Functional glutamatergic AMPA receptor (calcium-permeable, GluA2-containing) synapses form between neurons and glioma cells (both pediatric and adult), producing depolarizing currents that promote proliferation. A second synapse type — GABAergic interneurons to diffuse midline glioma GABAA receptors — also produces depolarizing currents in glioma cells because of elevated intracellular chloride.

**In peripheral cancers:** NF1-mutant neurofibromas — arising from Schwann cells — are promoted by adjacent sensory nerves via activity-regulated secretion of COL1A2 (a type I collagen chain) as a paracrine mitogen. Parasympathetic cholinergic nerves drive gastric cancer proliferation via WNT (and YAP) activation. In PDAC, stress-induced norepinephrine stimulates ADRB2 on cancer cells, which upregulates NGF and BDNF, creating a feedforward adrenergic-neurotrophin loop accelerating tumorigenesis (confirmed by beta-blocker association with modestly improved human PDAC survival). In gastric cancer, acetylcholine (ACh) via CHRM3 similarly induces NGF, which amplifies tumor progression autocrine/paracrine via TRK receptors. These parallel PDAC and gastric models are treated as a class: neurotransmitter (norepinephrine or ACh) → receptor (ADRB2 or CHRM3) → NGF/BDNF upregulation → autocrine TRK proliferative signaling + paracrine hyper-innervation → feedforward loop.

### Hallmark 2 — Resisting cell death

Sympathetic denervation of the prostate (chemical or surgical) or genetic ablation of Adrb2 and Adrb3 (beta-2 and beta-3 adrenergic receptors) markedly increased apoptosis and impaired prostate cancer engraftment and progression in xenograft and GEMM models. Deletion of both ADRB2 and ADRB3 together caused profound inhibition of tumor engraftment. Dark-rearing of NF1-associated optic pathway glioma GEMMs (reducing optic nerve activity) completely prevented tumor formation; introducing dark-rearing after tumor onset caused tumor regression. NLGN3 ablation phenocopies dark-rearing on tumor initiation. Both results are interpreted as neural activity conveying the cell-death resistance hallmark.

### Hallmark 3 — Activating invasion and metastasis

**Parasympathetic (prostate):** Cholinergic innervation promotes prostate cancer invasion and lymph node metastasis via CHRM1 (muscarinic receptor-1) expressed on prostatic stromal cells. Cholinergic blockers reduced lymph node dissemination.

**Perineural invasion (PDAC):** PDAC cells reprogram proximal Schwann cells to a "tumor-activated" state via a c-Jun/AP-1 transcriptional program analogous to nerve repair. These reprogrammed Schwann cells: (a) assemble into microchannel tracks that guide cancer cell migration, and (b) secrete CCL2, which recruits inflammatory monocytes that differentiate into macrophages expressing cathepsin B, further stimulating perineural invasion.

**Synaptic-driven invasion (glioblastoma):** AMPAR-mediated glutamatergic synaptic input to margin glioma cells drives their invasiveness via intracellular calcium signaling and CREB. Calcium chelators or CREB inhibition blocked invasion. Live imaging revealed invasive cells transitioning between a migratory and a stationary proliferative state.

**NMDAR signaling:** In pancreatic tumors (both neuroendocrine and ductal), cancer cells upregulate glutamate transporters to autocrinally activate NMDAR, driving both proliferation and invasion. In triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) brain metastases, paracrine glutamate from neuronal synapses (pseudo-tripartite synaptic association) activates NMDAR in breast cancer cells, promoting brain colonization.

**Glutamate-NMDAR-FMRP axis:** NMDAR signaling in cancer cells activates FMRP (fragile X mental retardation protein, encoded by FMR1), an RNA-binding protein governing translation of hundreds of genes. FMRP was initially identified as a driver of invasion and metastasis; it is broadly overexpressed in human solid tumors, including those without NMDAR activity (suggesting additional upstream activators).

### Hallmark 4 — Avoiding immune destruction

**GABA autocrine loop:** Cancer cells express GAD1/GAD2 (glutamic acid decarboxylases) and GABABR; GABA levels in tumors are inversely correlated with prognosis. GABA→GABABR→GSK-3β suppression→β-catenin stabilization represses CCL4/CCL5 expression, reducing T cell and CD103+ dendritic cell recruitment. Pharmacological or genetic inhibition of GAD1 or GABABR restored immunostimulatory CCL4/5 and improved tumor immunity in mouse models.

**FMRP immunosuppression:** Beyond invasion, FMRP more recently emerged as a master regulator of the immunosuppressive TME: FMRP stimulates cytokine secretion that expands immunoinhibitory Tregs, and programs immunosuppressive macrophages. FMRP knockout/knockdown (Fmr1 gene) in cancer cells converted the TME from immunosuppressive to immunostimulatory, enabling productive T cell killing. D.H. is a co-inventor on a patent for FMRP as a cancer immunosuppressor.

### Hallmark 5 — Tumor-promoting inflammation

**Midkine–CD8+–CCL5 axis in NF1-LGGs:** Neurons in NF1-associated low-grade glioma TME secrete midkine, which recruits and activates CD8+ T cells. These T cells then secrete CCL4, which induces CCL5 expression in microglia/myeloid cells. CCL5 triggers cancer cell proliferation and suppresses apoptosis. Crucially, the CD8+ T cells are maintained in a state that is non-cytolytic — they are a form of tumor-promoting inflammation that paradoxically conveys sustained proliferative signaling and cell-death resistance rather than anti-tumor immunity. This is described as an "unusual form" of tumor-promoting inflammation that simultaneously conveys immune evasion.

### Hallmark 6 — Inducing/accessing vasculature (angiogenesis)

Noradrenaline (norepinephrine) signaling via ADRB2 expressed specifically on endothelial cells (not pericytes or myeloid cells — confirmed by tissue-specific knockouts) triggers an angiogenic switch in prostate cancer. The mechanism operates through endothelial cell metabolism: ADRB2 activation instructs aerobic glycolysis supporting the angiogenic phenotype; ADRB2 absence switches endothelial cells to oxidative phosphorylation, which suppresses angiogenesis. This links the nervous system to both the angiogenesis hallmark and cellular metabolism deregulation.

### The proposed enabling characteristic

The paper's central conceptual contribution is the explicit proposal that **co-opted neuronal signaling circuits in cancer cells** — activated by non-mutational epigenetic reprogramming and in some cases somatic mutation — constitute a distinctive new **enabling characteristic**, distinct from but partially overlapping with genome instability and tumor-promoting inflammation. The paper argues (Figure 3) that six of ten hallmark parameters have demonstrated neural connections and that "tumor innervation as a common and functionally impactful constituent of the TME" should be added alongside cancer cells' co-optation of neuronal regulatory circuits as a unified argument for hallmark-enabling neural connectivity. The conclusion explicitly draws the analogy to how "deregulating cellular metabolism" and "avoiding immune destruction" graduated from "emerging" to "core" hallmarks; by this logic, cancer neuroscience is positioned as the next candidate for formal incorporation.

### Named genes / proteins

**Paracrine neural factors and receptors:** NLGN3, BDNF, NGF, IGF-1, midkine, COL1A2, ADRB2, ADRB3, CHRM1, CHRM3.

**Neurotransmitters and their receptors:** Glutamate → AMPAR (GluA2 subunit), NMDAR, GABABR, GABAAR; GABA → GABABR, GABAAR; norepinephrine → ADRB2/ADRB3; acetylcholine → CHRM1/CHRM3; GABA synthesis: GAD1, GAD2.

**Downstream oncogenic effectors:** PI3K-mTOR (downstream of NLGN3/AMPAR), GSK-3β, β-catenin (WNT), YAP, TrkA/TrkB (NGF/BDNF receptors), CREB (glioma invasion), FMR1/FMRP.

**Chemokines and immune mediators:** CCL2, CCL4, CCL5; cathepsin B.

**Tumor-suppressor/oncogene context:** NF1 (neurofibromatosis 1; loss-of-function mutations drive neural-activity-dependent glioma), MYC (Myc-driven prostate cancer GEMM).

**Structural neural markers (contextual):** c-Jun/AP-1 (Schwann cell reprogramming).

## Relevance

**H1 (tumors hijack top-down neural circuitry): FOUNDATIONAL SUPPORT — this paper is the canonical reference for H1 in the hallmarks framework.** The paper's entire first section ("Impact of neurons and innervation on the acquisition of hallmark capabilities") is the formal argument for H1. It demonstrates that tumors co-opt activity-regulated neural mechanisms — the same ones governing normal OPC proliferation, synaptic plasticity, and tissue homeostasis — to acquire proliferative, survival, and invasive capabilities. The direction of causation is bidirectional: neurons drive hallmark acquisition, while tumors reciprocally remodel neural circuits ("cancer-induced neural remodeling" in Figure 1). H1 is not merely supported by cited studies — it is the organizing thesis.

**H2 (via immune modulation): STRONGLY SUPPORTED as a mechanism, and partially overlapping with H1.** The GABA→GABABR→β-catenin→CCL4/5-repression axis and the FMRP→immunosuppressive Treg/macrophage axis both show co-opted neuronal molecules driving immune evasion intrinsically in cancer cells. The midkine→CD8+→CCL5 axis shows neural secretion from the TME engineering a paradoxical pro-tumorigenic inflammatory state. H2 is presented as a downstream consequence of H1 mechanisms (neural co-option shapes the immune microenvironment), not as an independent competing hypothesis.

**H3 (aberrant oncofetal/developmental expression): INDIRECTLY SUPPORTED for CNS tumors, not discussed for peripheral cancers.** The paper notes that NLGN3, BDNF, AMPAR-mediated synaptic integration, and GABAergic signaling are mechanisms that cancer cells co-opt from normal neural development (OPC proliferation regulation, myelination, synaptic plasticity). The phrase "co-option of neural mechanisms that parallel their roles in healthy tissues" (Introduction) is the H3 framing applied to all cancers, not only brain tumors. However, the paper does not explicitly invoke oncofetal transcriptomic programs or compare tumor gene expression to fetal neural atlases.

**H4 (artifact of brain/CNS cancer cohort composition): PARTIALLY ADDRESSED — the paper weakens H4 as the sole explanation.** The paper explicitly covers cancers of the brain, skin (basal cell, melanoma), head and neck, breast, lung, stomach, colon, pancreas, and prostate (Conclusion). The neural mechanisms described for prostate (ADRB2/ADRB3, CHRM1), PDAC (NGF, BDNF, ADRB2, NMDAR), gastric (CHRM3, NGF, WNT), and breast (NMDAR in brain metastases) cancers do not require CNS tumor presence in the cohort. This means that if the pipeline's top-mutated "neural genes" list includes ADRB2, CHRM1/3, TRKA/B (NTRK1/2), or NGF, their elevation could reflect genuine biology in epithelial cancers. However, the paper provides no genomic/mutational data and cannot rule out that glioma/CNS studies drive the specific gene list observed (the paper is mechanistic, not epidemiological).

**H5 (misannotation/non-neural functions): WEAKENED for the specific genes named.** The paper demonstrates bona fide functional roles in cancer for NLGN3, BDNF, NGF, ADRB2, CHRM1/3, GAD1, GABABR, FMR1/FMRP, and NMDAR subunits — through their canonical neural molecular identities, not via misannotation. However, for GABA and serotonin (mentioned in passing), the paper acknowledges they operate through non-neural cell types in the TME (B cells, immune cells) — supporting the view that some "neural" molecules have broader signaling roles. For the specific genes observed in the pipeline's top-mutated list (NKAIN2, KCNIP4, TAFA2/FAM19A2, RIT2, CALN1, RBFOX1, LSAMP, SGCZ, OPCML), the paper does not directly discuss any of these. Their absence from this review — which is comprehensive — is notable: they are not established cancer-neuroscience mediators in the Hanahan/Monje framework.

**Neural signaling as an emerging hallmark/enabling characteristic — CRITICAL FOR PROJECT NARRATIVE:**

The paper explicitly and formally proposes "co-opted neuronal signaling circuits in cancer cells" as **a new enabling characteristic** of cancer, analogous to genome instability and tumor-promoting inflammation (Figure 2 caption; Conclusion). The Conclusion paragraph states: "it seems reasonable to postulate that co-opted neuronal signaling circuits in cancer cells constitute a distinctive and instrumental regulatory mechanism." Figure 3 shows six of ten hallmarks highlighted as having neural connections, while the Introduction notes that "tumor innervation as a common and functionally impactful constituent of the TME" should be recognized as broadly applicable across cancer types. This framing — cancer neuroscience as an emerging enabling characteristic — is the most useful narrative handle for the project: the project's cross-study neural-gene enrichment question maps directly onto asking whether the somatic mutation landscape reflects selection on genes in this newly proposed enabling characteristic class.

**Data-driven label-free neural-gene definition: NOT PROVIDED.** The paper does not offer a computational resource, gene list, expression atlas, or scoring method for defining "neural genes" from data alone. All gene identifications come from hypothesis-driven experiments and curated neural function annotations. The paper does gesture toward the logic of a data-driven approach (noting that NLGN3, BDNF, AMPAR, and GABAergic pathway components are co-opted from normal neural programs), which implies that a reference transcriptome of normal neurons/OPCs could serve as a label-free anchor — but this is not developed.

## Limitations

- The perspective is explicitly a selective narrative review, not a systematic meta-analysis. Gene and hallmark examples are chosen to illustrate the argument rather than to be comprehensive or unbiased; negative evidence (cancer types or genes where neural co-option was tested and found absent) is not discussed.
- No somatic mutation data, no genomic epidemiology. The paper cannot explain why neural genes would appear at elevated SOMATIC MUTATION frequency in a pan-cancer mutation database. All mechanisms described involve expression-level, signaling, or electrophysiological phenomena in host nervous system or co-opted expression programs — not somatic mutation selection. This is the single most important limitation for the pipeline's interpretation question.
- The "enabling characteristic" framing is explicitly a postulate ("it seems reasonable to postulate"), not a formally validated addition to the hallmarks schema. The Conclusion notes that the bar for inclusion in the core hallmarks is "appreciable generality" — the cited evidence is provocative and multi-cancer but not yet comprehensively validated across all cancer types.
- The FMRP/FMR1 section carries a conflict of interest: D.H. is co-inventor on an FMRP cancer patent and founder of a company (Opna Bio SA) exploiting it; the FMRP immunosuppression findings (ref 33) are from D.H.'s own laboratory. The paper is transparent about this (Declaration of Interests), but the emphasis on FMRP as a central integrating mechanism warrants independent confirmation.
- Hallmarks 4 (deregulating cellular metabolism) and 5 (enabling replicative immortality) lack established neural connections per Figure 3; the paper acknowledges these gaps and calls for future investigation.
- Cancer types not prominently discussed: leukemias/lymphomas, renal, bladder, ovarian — limiting generalizability to those cancer types that may be in the cBioPortal pipeline.
- The paper largely aggregates findings from mouse models with limited or no human prospective validation for most specific mechanisms.

## Model / Tool Availability

No software, datasets, computational models, or resources are released with this perspective. The paper cites the NLGN3-targeting clinical trial (NCT04295759, early-phase, referenced via citing studies). Opna Bio SA (D.H.'s company) is developing FMRP as a therapeutic target but no resource is available. The paper is open-access (CC BY-NC-ND) via Cancer Cell.

## Follow-up

- **Canonical framing for project narrative:** Cite Hanahan2023 as the primary reference when framing the project's neural-gene enrichment finding as potentially reflecting "co-opted neuronal signaling circuits" (the paper's proposed enabling characteristic). This elevates the scientific context of the observation from a methodological artifact (H4/H5) to a potentially biology-grounded pattern (H1/H2).

- **Critical pipeline test — mutation vs. expression separation:** Because the paper explicitly states that neural co-option is "evidently activated by non-mutational epigenetic reprogramming," the project should test whether genes implicated in this paper (NLGN3, BDNF, NGF, ADRB2, CHRM3, GAD1, FMR1, GRIN2A) appear elevated in EXPRESSION data (if available) versus somatic MUTATION frequency. A dissociation (high expression, not high mutation) would be consistent with the paper's mechanism; high mutation frequency would require separate explanation.

- **FMR1/FMRP check:** FMR1 is described as broadly overexpressed across human solid tumors without requiring NMDAR activity. Check its somatic mutation frequency in the pipeline's cross-cancer gene × cancer matrix. If FMR1 appears at high mutation frequency, this may reflect loss-of-function selection against FMRP's growth-restricting immune function; if not, FMRP's role in cancer is purely expression-level, consistent with the paper.

- **Data-driven neural-gene definition anchor:** The paper's mechanistic catalog (NLGN3, BDNF, NGF, ADRB2, CHRM1/3, GAD1/2, GABABR, NMDAR, AMPAR, FMRP, midkine, COL1A2) can serve as a positive-control gene set for any label-free approach. If a computational method (e.g., brain-specificity score from GTEx, OPC/neuron single-cell enrichment from Allen Brain Atlas) correctly ranks these genes as "neural," it validates the method. The genes in the pipeline's top-mutated list (NKAIN2, KCNIP4, RBFOX1, etc.) are ABSENT from this canonical catalog — an important negative to document explicitly.

- **H4 test strategy (cancer-type stratification):** The paper covers nine cancer type categories with documented neural mechanisms. Stratify the pipeline's top-mutated neural-gene list by cancer type and test whether enrichment (a) is driven by CNS/glioma studies alone (pure H4), (b) persists in prostate/pancreatic/gastric studies (supported by the paper's peripheral cancer evidence), or (c) appears in cancer types the paper does not cover (would suggest H5 or uncharacterized biology).

- **GRIN2A/GRIN2B (NMDAR subunits):** These are both neural function genes and established cancer genes (GRIN2A somatic mutations are recurrent in melanoma). The paper's NMDAR mechanism in PDAC and breast metastases adds biological plausibility to their appearance in cross-cancer mutation tables. Check pipeline output for GRIN2 family mutation frequency distribution across cancer types.

- **NF1 as the bridge gene:** NF1 loss is the clearest example where a canonical somatic driver mutation (established in Bailey 2018, flagged by the pipeline) also operates through a neural-activity-dependent mechanism. NF1-mutant tumors are enriched in CNS/neurofibromatosis contexts. Testing whether NF1 mutation frequency in non-CNS cancers co-occurs with elevated expression of neural pathway genes would directly connect the pipeline's mutation data to the paper's mechanistic framework.
