# The neuro-immune axis in cancer: Relevance of the peripheral nervous system to the disease

- **Authors:** Nina Cortese, Alessandra Rigamonti, Alberto Mantovani, Federica Marchesi
- **Year:** 2020
- **Journal:** Immunology Letters, Vol. 227, pp. 60–65
- **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.imlet.2020.07.010
- **BibTeX key:** Cortese2020
- **Source:** PDF

## Key Contribution

A focused narrative review that synthesizes evidence for bidirectional crosstalk between the peripheral nervous system (PNS) and immune cells in the tumor microenvironment (TME). The paper argues that nerves are a bona fide functional component of the TME and that PNS–immune interactions shape tumor initiation, growth, immune evasion, and metastatic spread via three overlapping mechanisms: (1) neural remodeling/plasticity within tumors, (2) autonomic neural regulation of tumor-infiltrating leukocytes, and (3) perineural invasion (PNI) as an active, immune-mediated dissemination route. The review is positioned as a call to characterize specific mediators and pathways so that the neuro-immune axis can be targeted therapeutically.

## Methods

Narrative review; no primary data generated. The paper draws on published preclinical models (mouse denervation experiments, genetic sympathetic/parasympathetic manipulation), human histopathology (pancreatic, prostate, head-and-neck, colorectal, breast, gastric cancers), and in vitro cell-migration assays. No systematic literature search protocol is described; study selection is the authors' synthesis of what they consider the most compelling lines of evidence available as of mid-2020.

## Key Findings

### 1. Shared receptors and ligands underpin neuro-immune crosstalk

- Immune cells and sensory neurons co-express pattern-recognition receptors (TLRs, FPRs), damage-associated molecular pattern (DAMP) receptors (TLR4/RAGE for HMGB1; P2X3 on nociceptors, P2X7 on macrophages for ATP), and nociceptive ion channels (TRPV1 on T cells, TRPV2 on macrophages).
- Immune cells carry a full functional cholinergic apparatus: muscarinic (mAChRs) and nicotinic (nAChRs, especially alpha-7/CHRNA7) receptors, choline acetyltransferase (ChAT), and acetylcholinesterase (AChE). TLR agonists induce ChAT in macrophages and DCs; T-cell activation upregulates ChAT and AChE.
- Most immune cells express adrenergic receptors; beta-2 adrenergic (ADRB2) signaling suppresses NF-kB and pro-inflammatory cytokines while increasing IL-10 and TGF-beta.
- Key shared neuropeptides: substance P, CGRP (calcitonin gene-related peptide), VIP (vasoactive intestinal peptide). Their effects on immune cells are context-, receptor-, and tissue-dependent.
- Neurotrophins (NGF, BDNF, GDNF, neurturin, artemin) are produced by immune and stromal cells and act on both neural and cancer cells.

### 2. Neural remodeling in tumors is progressive and pro-tumorigenic

- Nerve density increases progressively during normal-to-preneoplastic-to-cancerous tissue transition in prostate and other cancers.
- Tumor cells reactivate developmental/regenerative neural programs driven by neurotrophins (NGF, BDNF) and their receptors (TrkA/NTRK1, TrkB/NTRK2, RET). Elevated neurotrophin expression is linked to poor outcomes across cancer types.
- Adrenergic nerves activate an angio-metabolic switch in prostate cancer via noradrenaline signaling through ADRA (alpha-adrenergic) and ADRB (beta-adrenergic) receptors.
- Denervation (surgical or chemical) inhibits cancer initiation and progression in prostate, breast, and squamous cell carcinoma; autonomic nerve neoformation promotes prostate tumor dissemination.
- Exception: vagotomy accelerates pancreatic cancer progression by increasing inflammation and tumor-associated macrophage (TAM) recruitment — parasympathetic and sympathetic arms have opposing TME effects.

### 3. Autonomic regulation of tumor-infiltrating leukocytes

- **Sympathetic (adrenergic) effects — generally pro-tumorigenic:**
  - Catecholamines stimulate macrophage infiltration, angiogenesis, and metastasis via ADRB2 signaling.
  - ADRB2 signaling on CD8+ T cells impairs effector function by reducing glucose uptake and glycolytic activity.
  - Sympathetic innervation in breast cancer upregulates PD-1 on CD4+ and CD8+ T cells and FOXP3 on CD4+ T cells; denervation or pharmacological beta-blockade restores IFN-gamma production.
  - ADRB2 on myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) controls their frequency, survival, and expression of arginase-I and PD-L1.
  - Beta-adrenergic signaling on TAMs induces a pro-metastatic gene signature ("metastatic switch") in breast cancer.
  - Neuropeptide Y (NPY), released by prostate cancer cells under sympathetic stimulation, has chemotactic effects on macrophages.
- **Parasympathetic (cholinergic) effects — generally anti-tumorigenic:**
  - Vagus nerve stimulation suppresses splenic/hepatic TNF-alpha, IL-1-beta, and IL-6 production via CHRNA7 (alpha-7 nicotinic acetylcholine receptor) on macrophages; HMGB1 secretion is also inhibited.
  - The anti-inflammatory reflex does not require direct vagal innervation of the spleen; memory CD4+ T cells in the spleen respond to noradrenaline from sympathetic fibers via ADRB2 and produce acetylcholine.
  - Cholinergic signaling (muscarinic receptor agonism) restrained pancreatic tumorigenesis by suppressing cancer stem cells, reducing TAM infiltration, and decreasing TNF-alpha production.
  - Adrenergic control of lymphatic vessel tone impedes T-cell egress from lymph nodes (via ADRB2), reducing tumor lymphocyte infiltration.

### 4. Perineural invasion (PNI) as an active, immune-assisted metastatic route

- PNI is observed in pancreatic, head-and-neck, prostate, colorectal, and gastric cancers and correlates with poor clinical outcome.
- Cancer cells actively migrate along axons; PNI resembles nerve regeneration, involving Schwann cells (glial cells that respond to nerve damage) and stellate cells.
- Key molecular mediators of PNI:
  - Neurotrophic factors: NGF, GDNF, neurturin, artemin
  - Chemokines: CXCL13, CX3CL1 (fractalkine), CCL2
  - Neurotransmitters: catecholamines, acetylcholine, neuropeptides
  - Adhesion molecule: L1CAM (L1 cell adhesion molecule)
- The CCL2/CCR2 axis is central: CCL2 is expressed by macrophages, Schwann cells, neurons, and fibroblasts within the perineurium; in vitro CCL2 induces prostate cancer neural invasion; in vivo CCR2 knockout or CSF-1 inhibition reduces PNI.
- Endoneurial macrophages support PNI by secreting GDNF, activating the RET tyrosine kinase receptor on cancer cells.
- Schwann cell-derived CCL2 recruits monocytes; macrophages promote cancer cell neural invasion via cathepsin B secretion.
- CX3CR1/CX3CL1 axis: CX3CR1 expression on pancreatic tumor cells mediates neural tropism and is associated with increased local recurrence.
- NGF expression in pancreatic cancer correlates with PNI frequency and pain severity.
- PNI elicits a perineural inflammatory infiltrate of macrophages, mast cells, and T lymphocytes.

## Relevance

### `H1` (Neural circuitry hijack — top-down)

Partial but indirect support. The paper does not address CNS or top-down circuitry. All reviewed mechanisms are peripheral (PNS). Argument: the top-mutated neural genes in somatic data likely reflect PNS-expressed genes acting in the TME rather than CNS-circuit hijacking per se. However, the review does not provide gene-level expression data that would confirm or deny this.

### `H2` (Immune modulation — via neuro-immune axis) — PRIMARY RELEVANCE

Strong, direct support. This paper is the canonical mechanistic substrate for the `H2` local note label. Key causal chains documented:
- PNS regulates TAM polarity, MDSC immunosuppression, CD8+ T-cell exhaustion, NK-cell activity, and lymphocyte trafficking — all through secreted neuro-immune mediators whose encoding genes include CHRNA7, ADRB2, CCL2, CCR2, CX3CR1, CX3CL1, CXCL13, NGF, GDNF, BDNF, NTRK1, NTRK2, RET, NPY, HMGB1, TLR4, TRPV1, TRPV2.
- The sympathetic/parasympathetic opposition (pro- vs anti-tumorigenic) implies that the net somatic mutation signal in neural-expressed genes reflects a balance of opposing forces modulated by tumor type and stage.
- Mechanistic basis for why neural genes would show elevated somatic mutation frequency via the `H2` local note label: genes that are functionally important at the neuro-immune interface may be under selective pressure in the tumor, leading to recurrent mutation patterns independently of their genomic length.

### `H3` (Aberrant developmental/oncofetal expression)

Moderate support. The review explicitly states that tumor cells "reactivate neural programs resembling those that regulate development" (nerve-mediated developmental and regenerative pathways, including neurotrophins/neurotrophin receptors). This provides biological grounding for the `H3` local note label: neural developmental genes expressed in tumors may be mutated or expressed aberrantly, creating an oncofetal transcriptional state that confounds "neural gene" annotation.

### `H4` (Brain/CNS-cancer artifact)

Not addressed. The paper focuses exclusively on PNS; CNS-specific cancers (glioma, etc.) are not discussed. This paper does not resolve the `H4` artifact hypothesis but implicitly supports the view that neural gene involvement in solid non-CNS tumors is a genuine biological phenomenon, not purely artifactual.

### `H5` (Misannotation)

Not addressed. The paper does not discuss gene annotation methodologies or the distinction between PNS-expressed and CNS-expressed gene products.

### Data-driven neural-gene definition

The paper provides NO computational or omics-based method for defining neural genes from data. All gene assignments are literature-curated. However, it implicitly suggests a functional gene set: genes mediating PNS-immune crosstalk (CHRNA7, ADRB2, CCL2, CCR2, CX3CR1, CX3CL1, NGF, GDNF, NTRK1/TrkA, NTRK2/TrkB, RET, NPY, HMGB1, TRPV1, TRPV2, MrgprB2, substance-P pathway genes, CGRP pathway genes, VIP, ChAT/CHAT, AChE/ACHE). These represent a curated neuro-immune gene set orthogonal to pure annotation-based lists. To define neural genes purely from data, one would need: (1) cross-tissue single-cell RNA-seq to identify genes with expression enriched in peripheral neurons vs all other cell types; (2) protein-protein interaction network analysis seeded from the above mediators.

## Limitations

- Narrative review with no systematic search protocol or PRISMA-equivalent; citation selection reflects author perspective, not unbiased literature coverage.
- Most cited experimental evidence comes from prostate, pancreatic, and breast cancer; generalization to other cancer types (including the neural-gene-enriched cancer types in the user's dataset) is assumed rather than demonstrated.
- The review predates major single-cell resolution studies of PNS-immune interactions in tumors; some claims about cell-type specificity of receptor expression may be revised by subsequent scRNA-seq data.
- Causal directionality of many claimed associations is unclear: the review frequently describes correlational evidence (e.g., nerve density increasing with cancer progression) without establishing whether neural changes drive or follow tumor progression.
- The paper does not distinguish between somatic mutation of neural/neuro-immune genes and transcriptional dysregulation; the project's hypothesis space requires this distinction.
- No quantitative data on gene-level mutation frequencies, expression levels, or effect sizes; claims cannot be directly validated against cBioPortal pipeline outputs.
- Page discrepancy: the article states journal volume 227 (2020), pages 60–65 on the PDF; user's task brief cited pages 38–47 [UNVERIFIED — may reflect a different edition or preprint pagination].

## Follow-up

- **Testable `Q1`:** Do genes encoding PNS-immune interface proteins (CHRNA7, ADRB2, CCL2, CCR2, CX3CR1, CX3CL1, NGF, GDNF, NTRK1, NTRK2, RET) show elevated somatic mutation frequencies in the cBioPortal pipeline outputs, and are these elevated after correcting for gene length and replication timing?
- **Testable `Q2`:** Is the neural gene mutation signal in the pipeline concentrated in cancer types where PNI is clinically prominent (pancreatic, prostate, head-and-neck, colorectal) rather than in CNS-origin cancers? This would distinguish `H2` (PNS-immune) from `H4` (CNS artifact).
- **Testable `Q3`:** Do the user's top-ranked neural genes (NKAIN2, KCNIP4, TAFA2/FAM19A2, RIT2, CALN1, RBFOX1, LSAMP, SGCZ, OPCML) encode proteins known to be expressed in PNS neurons or immune cells (not just brain)? Cross-referencing against GTEx peripheral-nerve expression or Human Protein Atlas PNS annotations would distinguish PNS-relevant from CNS-only neural genes.
- **Testable `Q4`:** Does the beta-adrenergic / PD-1 axis documented here (sympathetic innervation upregulates PD-1 on T cells) predict co-occurrence of ADRB2 mutations with immune checkpoint gene alterations in the pipeline's cross-study data?
- **Datasets/tools for label-free neural-gene definition from omics:**
  - GTEx v10 (nerve tibial tissue expression) — genes with tissue-enriched expression in peripheral nerve vs all tissues define a PNS-expression signature
  - Human Protein Atlas tissue specificity scores for peripheral neurons
  - PanglaoDB / CellMarker 2.0 single-cell marker databases for neuron subtypes (sensory, autonomic, enteric)
  - STRING protein interaction network seeded with known PNS-immune mediators listed above, then expanded by neighborhood enrichment
  - CellChat or NicheNet applied to published PNS-containing tumor scRNA-seq datasets to identify ligand-receptor pairs actually active at the neuro-immune interface
