# Diffsig: Associating Risk Factors With Mutational Signatures

- **Authors:** Ji-Eun Park, Markia A. Smith, Sarah C. Van Alsten, Andrea Walens, Di Wu, Katherine A. Hoadley, Melissa A. Troester, Michael I. Love
- **Year:** 2023
- **Journal:** bioRxiv (preprint; posted February 10, 2023)
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.02.09.527740
- **BibTeX key:** Park2023
- **Source:** PDF

## Key Contribution

Diffsig is a Bayesian Dirichlet-multinomial hierarchical model and R package for estimating the association of arbitrary covariates (binary, categorical, or continuous "risk factors") with pre-defined mutational signature exposures. It addresses a fundamental limitation of standard approaches — naive point-estimate-based tests (e.g. Wilcoxon on NMF contributions) ignore the large sampling variance in signature attribution when mutation counts are low — by modelling the latent compositional contribution of each signature as a Dirichlet-distributed random variable, with association coefficients (β on the softmax scale) as the estimand. The model was validated on simulated data and applied to TCGA breast cancer (N=907 samples with HRD scores and PAM50 molecular subtypes), successfully recovering expected associations: HRD score ↔ SBS3 and HER2-enriched subtype ↔ APOBEC signatures SBS2/SBS13 [@Park2023].

## Methods

**Model architecture.** Diffsig is a two-level Bayesian hierarchical model:

1. Association layer: β ~ N(0, σ_β = 0.5) coefficient matrix linking M risk factor covariates to K pre-defined signatures; α = Xβ gives per-sample log-scale signature affinities.
2. Composition layer: per-sample signature contributions φ_n ~ Dirichlet(softmax(α_n) · τ), where τ ~ lognormal(0, 2) is a precision parameter capturing residual overdispersion among same-covariate samples.
3. Observation layer: observed 96-context mutation counts Y_n ~ Multinomial(C · φ_n), where C is the fixed K × 96 reference signature matrix (e.g. COSMIC).

The prior on β is zero-centered normal with fixed σ_β = 0.5, calibrated from TCGA breast cancer posterior estimates. Multiple risk factors can be included simultaneously for unbiased (confounding-adjusted) estimation. Posterior inference uses Stan via rstan (4 chains, 4 cores); convergence assessed via Gelman-Rubin R̂ < 1.05 [@Park2023].

**Signatures used.** Pre-defined sets selected from COSMIC based on prior literature; for breast cancer: SBS1, 2, 3, 5, 13; for liver cancer (simulations): SBS1, 4, 5, 12, 29. De-novo extraction is explicitly excluded — the analyst supplies the reference matrix.

**Data.** TCGA breast cancer MC3 MAF (hg19; SNVs only; germline and adjacent-normal chromosomes removed); hypermutated samples (mutation counts 563–5037) excluded; final analysis set N = 907 samples with both HRD score and PAM50 subtype available. PAM50 categories: basal-like (n=161), HER2-enriched (n=74), luminal A (n=487), luminal B (n=193); normal-like excluded. HRD scores derived from combined LOH, large-scale transitions, and telomeric allelic imbalance (NtAI) [@Park2023].

**Simulations.** 300 total simulations; N ∈ {50, 100, 150, 200, 1000}; three risk-factor types (binary, categorical 3-level, continuous); 20 replications per condition; breast- and liver-cancer COSMIC signatures. Confounding scenario tested with two correlated continuous covariates (σ_12 = 0.7) where only one is included in the model [@Park2023].

**Comparison.** Benchmarked against HiLDA (Yang et al. 2019), a related Bayesian LDA model that discovers de-novo signatures and tests group differences (binary only). Diffsig and HiLDA applied to the HER2-enriched vs. basal-like subset (N=241) and outputs qualitatively aligned after mapping HiLDA de-novo signatures to COSMIC [@Park2023].

## Key Findings

1. **Simulation accuracy.** Point estimates have low RMSE and 80% credible intervals achieve near-nominal coverage (>0.99) across all risk-factor types and sample sizes as small as N=50. RMSE and CI width decrease monotonically with N. At N=50, median RMSE ≤ 0.1.

2. **Confounding simulation.** Including both correlated covariates yields substantially lower RMSE and better CI coverage than including only one — demonstrating that omitting a confounding covariate biases estimates, and that Diffsig handles multi-covariate designs gracefully.

3. **Sampling variability demonstration.** Down-sampling a single TCGA basal-like sample from 428 to ~43 mutations (10% of counts, 100 draws) shows that naive NNLS-based relative contributions for SBS13 range from 0.2 to >0.65, motivating the model's heteroskedastic treatment of contribution uncertainty.

4. **TCGA breast cancer — HRD.** β for HRD score is clearly elevated for SBS3 relative to the other four signatures, consistent with the established SBS3 ↔ homologous-recombination-deficiency link. All R̂ < 1.05 (≈1.00). Total computation ~12 hours on 931 samples with 4 cores.

5. **TCGA breast cancer — PAM50 subtype.** HER2-enriched tumors have higher estimated association with APOBEC signatures SBS2 and SBS13 relative to basal-like, consistent with Roberts et al. (2013) and Pitt et al. (2018). Both binary and continuous (centroid-correlation) versions of the HER2 covariate yield nearly identical associations; binary formulation is faster (6.65 vs. 242 sample-minutes).

6. **HiLDA comparison.** Both models agree on the direction and rank of HER2 vs. basal-like signature associations. Diffsig is slightly faster (58 vs. 78 min on 241 samples) and directly reports associations against COSMIC signatures rather than requiring manual signature alignment.

## Relevance

Diffsig is a near-direct implementation of the association layer that **hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and** (agnostic covariate↔signature-exposure association) proposes to build. Its architecture maps precisely onto that design:

- **Positive-control recovery.** Diffsig's TCGA breast cancer results are a published positive control: it recovers HRD→SBS3 and PAM50-HER2→APOBEC (SBS2/13) without being told these links. This validates the positive-control strategy of the project hypothesis — if Diffsig (or a reimplementation) is applied to the cBioPortal/MC3 multi-study corpus, recovery of UV→SBS7, smoking→SBS4, APOBEC3-expression→SBS2/13, and MMR-loss/MSI→SBS6/15/26 within the relevant cancer types is the positive-control gate.

- **Multi-covariate, confounding-aware design.** The simulation results directly support the hypothesis's requirement to model multiple covariates simultaneously (clinical + molecular features, including expression modules). The confounding scenario shows that omitting a correlated covariate biases estimates — operationally relevant for the cBioPortal pipeline where many covariates are collinear.

- **Heteroskedastic treatment of low-mutation samples.** The project's cBioPortal cohort contains many panel-sequenced studies with low mutation counts per sample — exactly the regime where the Dirichlet-multinomial hierarchy adds the most value over naive NNLS. This is aligned with the pipeline's hypermutator annotation (which separates low-burden from high-burden samples) and the concern in question:0018-can-mutational-signature-decomposition-be-added-downstream-of-the-cross about panel adequacy for per-sample signature assignment.

- **Pre-defined signature set.** Diffsig operates on a pre-supplied reference matrix (e.g. COSMIC SBS), consistent with the hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and plan to use restricted SigProfiler assignment as the upstream step, not a new de-novo factorization.

- **Limitation for the project hypothesis.** Diffsig's β prior (σ = 0.5, fixed from TCGA breast cancer) is calibrated to a single cancer type and sequencing modality (WES). Applying it to a heterogeneous multi-study corpus spanning WES, WGS, and targeted panels may require re-calibration or a fully hierarchical prior over σ_β. Computation at 1,000 samples already takes ~5–6 hours; a cross-study corpus of tens of thousands of samples would require either approximate inference or batched within-study execution.

## Limitations

1. **Single cancer type.** Applied only to TCGA breast cancer; generalization to the multi-cancer, multi-study heterogeneity of cBioPortal is untested.
2. **Fixed σ_β prior.** The β ~ N(0, 0.5) prior was calibrated to one cancer type and one sequencing modality; this may over-regularize or under-regularize in other settings.
3. **Computational cost.** Runtime scales poorly: ~5–6 hours for N=1,000 samples; unsuitable without batching or approximate inference for large corpora.
4. **Binary covariate only for HiLDA comparison.** HiLDA handles only two-group comparisons; Diffsig's claimed advantage for multi-class and continuous covariates was demonstrated only in simulation, not on a real multi-class dataset beyond the 4-level PAM50 simulation.
5. **Pre-defined signatures required.** If the "true" active signatures in a new cohort differ from COSMIC, the model will misattribute mutations. The method is strictly an association model, not a discovery model.
6. **No matched normal.** Analysis used tumour-only mutation calls; germline contamination or clonal haematopoiesis signal is uncontrolled — relevant for the cBioPortal pipeline's CH-contamination concern (topic:clonal-hematopoiesis-contamination).
7. **Preprint status.** This is a bioRxiv preprint (February 2023); peer-reviewed publication status should be rechecked before treating the method as final.
