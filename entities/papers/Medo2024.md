# A comprehensive comparison of tools for fitting mutational signatures

- **Authors:** Matúš Medo, Charlotte K. Y. Ng, Michaela Medová
- **Year:** 2024
- **Journal:** Nature Communications
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-024-53711-6
- **BibTeX key:** Medo2024
- **Source:** PDF

## Key Contribution

This paper provides the first large-scale, systematic benchmarking of twelve signature *fitting*
tools — tools that decompose a tumour's mutation catalogue into known COSMIC SBS signatures
rather than extracting signatures de novo. Using synthetic mutational catalogs with empirically
driven signature weights from eight cancer types and real PCAWG WGS data, the authors show that
no single tool dominates all regimes: SigProfilerSingleSample excels at low mutational burden
(≤1,000 mutations), while SigProfilerAssignment and MuSiCal are best at high burden (≥2,000
mutations). Critically, the paper shows that ad hoc restriction of the reference catalog is
often harmful, and that out-of-reference signatures pose the most severe and underappreciated
challenge across all tools.

## Methods

**Benchmarking framework — SigFitTest (github.com/8medom/SigFitTest):**

- Twelve tools evaluated: deconstructSigs, mmsig, MuSiCal, MutationalPatterns, sigfit,
  sigLASSO, sigminer, signature.tools.lib, SigProfilerAssignment, SigProfilerSingleSample,
  SigsPack, YAPSA (Table 1). Languages span R and Python.
- **Synthetic cohorts — single-signature:** 100 samples, each with exactly one active
  signature; mutation counts 100 / 1,000 / 10,000. Reference: all 67 COSMICv3 signatures.
- **Synthetic cohorts — heterogeneous:** signature weights from empirical COSMIC tissue
  contribution data for eight cancer types (Liver-HCC, Stomach-AdenoCA, Head-SCC,
  ColoRect-AdenoCA, Lung-AdenoCA, Skin-Melanoma, Lymph-BNHL, CNS-GBM). Cohorts of 100
  samples, mutation counts 100 / 2,000 / 50,000.
- **Real data:** 146 PCAWG WGS samples with ≥50,000 mutations; ground truth derived by
  consensus of the three best-performing tools (SigProfilerAssignment, sigLASSO, MuSiCal)
  where all three agree.
- **Metrics:** fitting error (mean absolute difference of true vs estimated relative weights,
  Eq. 3), precision, sensitivity, F1 score; false positive weight (total weight on inactive
  signatures).
- **Reference catalog sensitivity test:** COSMICv2 vs COSMICv3 comparison; two-step
  ad-hoc restriction procedure (fit full catalog, then restrict to signatures appearing above
  threshold w₀ across ≥5 samples) assessed against simply using the complete COSMICv3.
- **Out-of-reference catalog experiment:** synthetic catalogs generated with 12 signatures
  absent from COSMICv3.3.1 that were added in v3.3.1 (SBS10c, SBS10d, SBS86–95). Weight
  of out-of-reference signatures varied from 0 to 0.4 (each of two out-of-ref signatures
  assigned 0.5x to 0.3x of the contaminating fraction).
- **Downstream analysis use-case:** synthetic CNS-GBM cohorts with artificially introduced
  SBS40 activity differences between even/odd-numbered samples; four tools compared on
  statistical power to detect the introduced group difference.

**Evaluation metrics:**

The exponentiated Shannon index was used to quantify signature "flatness" (effective number
of active contexts); this correlates strongly (Spearman's rho 0.78–0.84) with per-tool fitting
error across signatures.

## Key Findings

**Tool performance — heterogeneous cohorts:**

- At 100 mutations/sample: **SigProfilerSingleSample** achieves the lowest fitting error
  across all eight cancer types on average.
- At 2,000 mutations/sample: SigProfilerSingleSample remains competitive; **mmsig** is
  best for some cancer types; **SigProfilerAssignment** and **MuSiCal** emerge as top
  performers at 50,000 mutations.
- sigLASSO, signature.tools.lib, and sigfit are among the best for low false positives when
  mutation counts are small, though they are not top overall.
- Performance varies substantially by cancer type (Supplementary Fig. 11) — no tool is
  uniformly best.
- Running time spans four orders of magnitude: SigsPack (fastest) to mmsig (slowest); even
  the slowest tools are not a practical bottleneck for typical cohorts.

**Reference catalog restriction:**

- Expanding from COSMICv2 (67 signatures) to COSMICv3 (78 signatures) increases fitting
  error for most tools due to higher overfitting risk.
- SigProfilerSingleSample, SigProfilerAssignment, mmsig, MuSiCal, and sigLASSO are robust
  to the larger reference at high mutation counts.
- Ad hoc restriction of the reference catalog to "likely active" signatures is **beneficial
  only for ~100 mutations/sample** in combination with SigProfilerSingleSample or
  SigProfilerAssignment. For ≥2,000 mutations, using the full COSMICv3 catalog with a
  well-performing tool is preferable.

**Out-of-reference signatures — the dominant challenge:**

- When active signatures are missing from the reference catalog, **all tools fail**: fitting
  error increases substantially and precision/sensitivity both drop (Fig. 4e).
- Out-of-reference weight of 20% causes precision losses of 20–40% across tools; sensitivity
  also drops markedly.
- Three tools — deconstructSigs, signature.tools.lib, and sigfit — are less prone to
  "redistributing" missing-signature mutations to other COSMIC signatures, but they are not
  top performers in other settings.
- The authors argue that out-of-reference signal is a bigger practical problem than
  overfitting, particularly at high mutational burden.

**Downstream analysis (statistical power to detect signature differences):**

- At 10,000 mutations, all four tested tools (MutationalPatterns, SigProfilerSingleSample,
  SigProfilerAssignment, signature.tools.lib) reliably detect an introduced SBS40 group
  difference in CNS-GBM synthetic cohorts.
- At 1,000 mutations, **SigProfilerSingleSample alone maintains >90% success rate**; the
  others succeed in fewer cohorts, especially for small cohort sizes.
- At 100 mutations, only SigProfilerSingleSample retains statistical power; all others
  largely fail irrespective of cohort size.

**Two-step extraction+fitting pipeline:**

- First extracting signatures de novo (using SignatureAnalyzer v0.0.7) and then refitting
  to the extracted signatures can improve results for small mutation counts (m=100) when
  combined with SigProfilerSingleSample or SigProfilerAssignment.
- For larger mutation counts (m=2,000 or 50,000), this two-step approach is not superior
  to direct fitting with the full COSMICv3 catalog.

**Reconstruction accuracy score as quality filter:**

- SigProfilerSingleSample's sample reconstruction similarity score (commonly used to filter
  low-quality samples) is strongly influenced by the number of mutations, not only by
  fitting accuracy. Its absolute value is a poor indicator of fit quality and should not be
  used as a universal threshold.

## Relevance

This paper is directly relevant to hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and (agnostic covariate–signature-exposure
association; positive-control recovery of UV/smoking/APOBEC/MMR).

**h08a (positive-control recovery):** The pipeline currently uses
`run_restricted_sigprofiler_assignment.py`, which implements a restricted-catalog approach.
This paper's finding that ad hoc catalog restriction is beneficial **only** at low mutation
counts (~100 mutations/sample) and harmful at high burden is an important calibration signal:
the restricted assignment step is appropriate for panel-sequenced studies (low mutation
counts) but the full COSMICv3 catalog should be preferred for WGS/WES-level samples such as
TCGA MC3, where per-sample mutation counts are typically >1,000. For hypothesis:0007's positive-control
arms (UV→SBS7, smoking→SBS4, APOBEC3-expr→SBS2/13), sample mutation count determines which
tool to deploy:

- Panel (< ~500 mutations): **SigProfilerSingleSample** with the full COSMICv3.
- WES (500–5,000 mutations): **SigProfilerSingleSample** or **SigProfilerAssignment**.
- WGS / MC3 (>5,000 mutations): **SigProfilerAssignment** or **MuSiCal**.

**Out-of-reference problem:** In a cross-study meta-analysis aggregating cBioPortal cohorts
with heterogeneous sequencing panels, some active signatures may not be in the COSMICv3
reference used at fitting time (either rare cancer-specific processes or recently added
signatures). This paper shows this will inflate fitting error and reduce precision across all
tools — a systematic bias that should be flagged when reporting h08b discovery hits (any
novel covariate→signature association may partly reflect mis-attribution of out-of-reference
signal).

**SigFitTest simulation framework** (github.com/8medom/SigFitTest) is publicly available
and could be adapted to calibrate the choice of fitting tool for specific cBioPortal study
cohorts before running the hypothesis:0007 association scan.

## Limitations

- Evaluation restricted to SBS (single base substitution) signatures only; doublet base
  substitutions, indels, copy number, and structural variation signatures not assessed.
- Only COSMICv3.0 signatures used as reference (version 3.3.4 as of October 2023 is more
  recent); the authors note that absolute weight differences between COSMICv3.0 and v3.4
  are below 10⁻⁷ for profiles already in v3.0.
- Synthetic cohort sizes fixed at 100 samples; behavior in larger cohorts (e.g. thousands
  of samples) is not directly evaluated.
- "Ground truth" for real PCAWG samples is itself an estimate (consensus of the three
  best-performing tools), introducing circularity for those tools.
- Out-of-reference signature simulation uses only 12 specific recently added COSMIC
  signatures; the effect of other types of missing signatures may differ.
- Confidence interval methods (bootstrapping, sigfit intervals) are not consistently applied
  across all tools in the evaluation; tools that do compute CIs may gain from CI-based
  filtering.
- Tool versions evaluated may not reflect current releases (e.g., SigProfilerAssignment
  v0.0.27, SigProfilerSingleSample v1.9.0 at time of submission).

## Follow-up

- **Calibrate tool choice for cBioPortal panel vs WES vs WGS studies** before running hypothesis:0007
  positive-control arm — split by estimated per-sample mutation count using the
  SigProfilerSingleSample (low) / SigProfilerAssignment (high) recommendation.
- **Investigate MuSiCal** for WGS-scale fitting (currently not in the pipeline); its
  full pipeline (de novo + matching + refit) may be valuable for MC3 samples.
- **Assess out-of-reference risk** for each cBioPortal study before aggregating: if a
  study's cancer type has known active signatures beyond COSMICv3, the mis-attribution bias
  described here will inflate false-positive hypothesis:0007 discovery hits.
- Consider running the **two-step extraction+refitting** approach (SignatureAnalyzer →
  COSMICv3 matching → restricted refit) for panel cohorts with ~100 mutations/sample, as
  this can outperform direct fitting at that burden.
