# mmsig: a fitting approach to accurately identify somatic mutational signatures in hematological malignancies

- **Authors:** Even H. Rustad, Ferran Nadeu, Nicos Angelopoulos, Bachisio Ziccheddu, Niccolo Bolli, Xose S. Puente, Elias Campo, Ola Landgren, Francesco Maura
- **Year:** 2021
- **Journal:** Communications Biology
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s42003-021-01938-0
- **BibTeX key:** Rustad2021
- **Source:** PDF

## Key Contribution

The paper introduces `mmsig`, an R package for mutational signature fitting in hematological cancers that uses a dynamic, cosine-similarity-based error-suppression procedure instead of hard percentage thresholds. This approach achieves higher specificity — particularly for low-abundance, distinctive signatures like APOBEC (SBS2/SBS13) — while maintaining reconstruction accuracy, avoiding the false-positive problem that plagues threshold-based tools like `deconstructSigs` when applied to COSMIC v3.1. The paper also establishes that choice of reference version (COSMIC v2 vs. v3.1) has a major impact on estimated signature contributions, with v3.1 providing sharper, better-separated profiles.

## Methods

**Algorithm.** `mmsig` performs expectation-maximization fitting of COSMIC reference signatures to individual tumor mutational profiles (96 trinucleotide SBS classes). Error correction is iterative and cosine-similarity-driven: at each iteration, the least-contributing signature is removed and the sample is re-reconstructed; if removing that signature reduces cosine similarity by >1%, it is retained. SBS1 and SBS5 (ubiquitous clock-like signatures) are forced into every profile regardless of contribution. Confidence intervals are generated by resampling 1,000 times from the multinomial distribution of the observed profile. Transcriptional strand bias is assessed (Poisson test, p < 0.05) as an orthogonal specificity check for signatures associated with transcription-coupled repair.

**Data.** Three publicly available WGS cohorts:
- 82 multiple myeloma (MM) patients (EGAD00001003309, 7 removed for missing treatment data)
- 142 chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL) patients (EGAS00000000092)
- 47 de novo AML + 2 therapy-related AML (phs000178.v2.p1 / EGAD00001003309)

Both COSMIC v2 and v3.1 references were used, along with disease-specific additions (SBS-MM1 for melphalan, SBS-HSPC for hematopoietic stem cells).

**Benchmarks.** `mmsig` was compared against `deconstructSigs` and `mutationalPatterns` using (a) the 82 MM WGS samples with and without standard threshold filters; (b) simulated MM genomes with known signature admixtures at dilutions from 1–20%; (c) random downsampling to 100–5,000 mutations; (d) exome vs. WGS subsets.

## Key Findings

1. **Reconstruction accuracy equivalence.** Without filtering, all three tools (mmsig, deconstructSigs, mutationalPatterns) achieve virtually identical cosine similarity distributions (median >0.998 on MM). Differences emerge only after error-correction/filtering.

2. **Specificity for clinically relevant signatures.** After applying recommended filters, `mmsig` achieved 100% specificity for SBS-MM1 (melphalan mutagenesis) — i.e., zero false positives in MM patients not exposed to melphalan — while retaining true positives for SBS1 and SBS2. By contrast, `deconstructSigs` with its hard 6% threshold introduced false negatives for SBS1 and SBS2, and `mutationalPatterns` retained false positives for SBS-MM1.

3. **APOBEC detection at low abundance.** `mmsig` reliably detects SBS2 (APOBEC) with 100% sensitivity at 4% contribution in simulated MM genomes. SBS-MM1, which has a less distinct profile, requires ~12% contribution for >95% sensitivity.

4. **COSMIC version matters enormously.** Signature contribution estimates differed significantly (Wilcoxon, p<0.001 for all signatures tested) between COSMIC v2 and v3.1. In v2, SBS1 was inflated (includes a flat background now partitioned to SBS5), and APOBEC signatures SBS2 and SBS13 overlapped substantially; v3.1 separates them cleanly (C>T vs. C>G). Algorithms showed similar performance on v2; `mmsig` showed superior performance on v3.1.

5. **CLL biology: nc-AID via SBS9 and IGHV status.** In 142 CLL patients, `mmsig` reliably detected SBS9 (non-canonical AID activity) specifically in IGHV-mutated patients. One unmutated CLL patient (CLL1078) with SBS9 evidence had 30% of IGHV bases mutated via Sanger sequencing — the classic marker of nc-AID — suggesting IGHV status alone may not perfectly stratify nc-AID exposure. `deconstructSigs` showed lower sensitivity for SBS9.

6. **AML: platinum chemotherapy signature SBS35.** `mmsig` identified SBS35 in both platinum-exposed AML patients with high confidence (non-zero 95% CI + characteristic transcriptional strand bias), while its false positive rate in 47 de novo AML cases was 21% — versus 70% for `deconstructSigs`. Transcriptional strand bias verification was decisive: one de novo AML case with a positive SBS35 point estimate lacked the expected strand bias, suggesting a false positive correctly flagged.

7. **Low mutation-count performance.** With ≥500 mutations (standard for WGS), all signatures are identifiable with high accuracy. Below 250 mutations, variability increases; flat signatures (SBS5) are most affected. With sets as small as 100 mutations, `mmsig` using a non-zero point-estimate criterion maintained sensitivity for distinctive signatures (SBS2, SBS9) at the cost of specificity; requiring non-zero 95% CI restored ~100% specificity but reduced sensitivity substantially at low counts.

8. **WES applicability (partial).** Going from WGS to WES reduces mean mutations from ~5,437 to ~245 per sample in MM and ~2,391 to ~108 per sample in CLL. WES-based fitting of SBS-MM1 performed comparably to downsampled WGS, but SBS9 (nc-AID) in CLL WES showed markedly lower concordance with WGS — expected because nc-AID activity is concentrated in non-coding regions.

## Relevance

**Direct relevance to h08 (agnostic covariate–signature association; positive-control recovery).** This paper is a primary methodological reference for signature fitting in hematological cancer contexts:

- **H08a positive control arm — APOBEC recovery.** `mmsig` demonstrates that APOBEC signatures (SBS2/SBS13) are recoverable at low admixture (~4% contribution threshold) with high sensitivity and specificity using COSMIC v3.1. The paper also shows that APOBEC expression/activity (IGHV-mutated CLL → nc-AID/SBS9; MM plasma cells → APOBEC deaminase/SBS2) produces biologically coherent exposures — exactly the positive controls h08 requires. The mmsig paper is thus a worked example of what a "passing" APOBEC recovery looks like.

- **Signature fitting tool for the pipeline.** If the cbioportal pipeline moves toward per-sample signature exposure estimation (q018), `mmsig` is an actionable candidate for heme malignancy substudies. Its 95% CI construction and transcriptional strand bias check provide the uncertainty quantification that the h08 association layer needs to avoid false-positive exposures as outcomes.

- **Reference-version lesson.** The dramatic sensitivity of estimated exposures to COSMIC v2 vs. v3.1 is a calibration warning for the cross-study meta-analysis: studies whose signature decompositions were run on v2 cannot be directly compared to v3.1-based decompositions. If pooling signature exposures across cBioPortal studies, the reference version must be harmonized.

- **Low-count caveat for panel sequencing.** Targeted capture panels yield too few mutations for reliable per-sample fitting (mean ~20 mutations for MM, ~1 for CLL in this paper). This directly informs q018's feasibility verdict: panel-sequenced studies from cBioPortal cannot contribute per-sample signature exposures and should enter the h08 analysis only as cohort-pooled or restricted-assignment inputs.

- **Chemotherapy artifact signatures.** The treatment of SBS-MM1 (melphalan) and SBS35 (platinum) as clinically annotated confounders rather than disease-process signatures maps directly to h08's reverse-causation guard (R2 in the hypothesis). Knowing which signatures track treatment rather than biology prevents misattributing a clinical covariate association.

## Limitations

- **Hematological-only validation.** `mmsig` is explicitly validated on MM, CLL, and AML. Generalization to carcinomas with very different signature landscapes (e.g., UV-driven melanoma, smoking-driven LUAD) is not demonstrated. The error-suppression heuristic may not be well-calibrated for tissues with dominant signatures that are "flat."

- **WGS required for most signatures.** The paper confirms that exome and panel sequencing provide insufficient mutation counts for reliable per-sample fitting in heme malignancies, limiting clinical applicability and confirming q018's feasibility concern.

- **Tool forces SBS1/SBS5 in all samples.** This design choice means mmsig cannot detect samples where one of these ubiquitous signatures is genuinely absent (e.g., certain pediatric tumors). The forcing also slightly inflates SBS1/SBS5 estimates and may suppress other signatures.

- **Reference panel selection is prior-knowledge-dependent.** The user must pre-select which COSMIC signatures to include for a given cancer type. `mmsig` does not automatically discover novel signatures (de novo extraction is not performed); it relies on externally derived reference catalogs.

- **Sensitivity-specificity trade-off remains unresolved for ambiguous signatures.** Signatures with overlapping profiles (SBS5/SBS8, SBS5/SBS8/SBS9) remain difficult even with mmsig; the paper acknowledges this is an inherent feature of the shared signature space.

## Model / Tool Availability

- **R package:** `mmsig` — open-source, available on GitHub: https://github.com/evenrus/mmsig
- **License:** Not explicitly stated in paper; GitHub repository should be consulted
- **Hardware:** Standard desktop R environment; fitting is fast (short run-times cited as an advantage)

## Follow-up

- **q018 feasibility:** This paper's WES/panel results directly inform whether cBioPortal panel studies can contribute per-sample signature exposures to the h08 association layer — the verdict is likely no for most panel studies.
- **Pipeline signature fitting:** Consider whether mmsig or SigProfilerAssignment is preferred for a heme-focused substudy; mmsig's 95% CI and transcriptional strand bias check are clinically attractive.
- **COSMIC version audit:** Confirm which COSMIC version is used in any cBioPortal study meta-data; cross-version pooling of signature exposures is unreliable.
