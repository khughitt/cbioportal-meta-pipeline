# Mutation signatures reveal clonal evolution

- **DOI:** 10.1182/blood.2022019510

## Key Contribution

This clonal-evolution mutational-signature note links topic:tumor-mutational-burden.

This is a 2-page editorial comment in *Blood* written by Lee and van Galen at Brigham and Women's Hospital, commenting on Diamond et al. (Blood 2023;141:2359-2371). It synthesises the key findings of Diamond et al.'s study of therapy-related myeloid neoplasms (tMNs) using chemotherapy-specific mutational signatures (SBS31/SBS35 for platinum, SBS-MM1 for melphalan) as temporal barcodes to reconstruct two distinct evolutionary trajectories from primary malignancy to secondary haematopoietic neoplasm. The central argument is that chemotherapy selects pre-existing clonal haematopoiesis (CH) clones rather than directly inducing driver mutations, and that this selection can be tracked — and timed — using characteristic mutational signatures.

## Methods

The commentary summarises Diamond et al.'s methodology and places it in biological context:

- **Cohort (primary study):** Whole-genome sequencing (WGS) on 40 tMNs spanning solid tumours, multiple myeloma (MM), and non-Hodgkin lymphoma (NHL); supplemented with TCGA and Beat AML public datasets.
- **Mutational signature quantification:** Single-base substitution (SBS) signatures assigned, including SBS1 (age-related), SBS-HSC (haematopoietic stem cell background), SBS31 and SBS35 (platinum-based chemotherapy), and SBS-MM1 (melphalan).
- **Temporal barcoding:** Chemotherapy-specific signatures embedded within chromosomal gains used to determine whether drug exposure preceded or followed large copy-number events.
- **Clonal haematopoiesis integration:** Intersection of tMN WGS data with CH mutation calls from a large cohort of platinum-exposed patients [@Bolton2020] to estimate direct mutagenic contribution of chemotherapy to driver genes.
- **Apheresis/pre-treatment samples:** Targeted sequencing of pre-melphalan blood or apheresis products in 11 patients to test the "escape via apheresis" model.

## Key Findings

1. **Chemotherapy selects rather than induces driver mutations.** Intersecting post-platinum tMN WGS data with CH mutations from platinum-exposed patients, Diamond et al. found only a minor platinum contribution to nonsynonymous mutations in canonical driver genes. Chemotherapy creates a selective environment permissive to expansion of mutated precursors, but does not commonly introduce the drivers themselves.

2. **Two evolutionary modes identified:**
   - **Mode 1 (signature-positive):** Chemotherapy selects a CH clone harbouring a pre-existing driver (notably TP53 mutations are enriched); subsequent acquisition of complex genomics (CNAs, structural variants, chromoplexy, chromothripsis). All tMNs exposed to platinum harboured the platinum SBS signature. SMARCA4 amplification found in 5/8 chromothriptic tMNs; overexpression supports proliferative advantage.
   - **Mode 2 (escape via apheresis):** Pre-existing CH clone avoids chemotherapy exposure by being physically removed during apheresis and reinfused via autologous stem cell transplantation (ASCT). These tMNs lack a chemotherapy signature and genetically resemble de novo AML. Supported by: (a) latency between chemo and tMN did not correlate with signature penetrance; (b) sequential platinum-then-melphalan/ASCT patients harboured only platinum signature; (c) post-melphalan/ASCT B-ALL lacked melphalan signature; (d) preleukemic mutations in 8/11 apheresis samples, including TP53-mutated CH in one apheresis product.

3. **Temporal barcoding via mutational signatures.** In all 8 tMN cases with chemotherapy signatures amenable to this approach, chemotherapy-related SBS signatures were found within chromosomal gains, confirming that drug exposure preceded large CNAs. Extended to 2 post-platinum MM cases using age-associated SBS5 to time chromosomal gains — multi-chromosomal gains inferred to have emerged in the second decade of life, with the most recent common ancestor pre-dating both the primary tumour and platinum exposure.

4. **41% penetrance of melphalan signature.** Only 41% of melphalan-exposed tMNs harboured the SBS-MM1 signature, consistent with subclonal dynamics, variable mutagenicity, or the apheresis escape route being common in this setting.

5. **TP53 loss as a chemo-resistance mechanism.** Significant enrichment of TP53 mutations in signature-positive tMNs relative to those without chemotherapy signatures, suggesting TP53 loss protects CH clonal precursors from chemotherapy-induced killing and enables their expansion.

## Relevance

**hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and (agnostic covariate–signature-exposure association; positive-control recovery of known signatures).** This paper is directly relevant to hypothesis:0007 in two ways:

- **Positive-control signature recovery:** The Diamond et al. work, as summarised here, demonstrates that SBS31/SBS35 (platinum) and SBS-MM1 (melphalan) can be cleanly recovered in tMN WGS data and used as reliable markers of prior drug exposure. In a cross-study meta-analysis context (hypothesis:0007), these chemotherapy-related signatures represent strong positive controls for drug-exposure covariates: if a study includes patients with prior platinum or alkylating-agent therapy, that exposure should manifest as an elevated SBS31/SBS35 or SBS-MM1 burden and co-vary with tMN/AML diagnoses.

- **Confounding interpretation:** The paper highlights that chemotherapy signatures can be present at variable penetrance (100% for platinum tMNs; 41% for melphalan tMNs), which means absence of a signature does not exclude prior drug exposure. This asymmetry is important when using signature exposures as covariates: hypothesis:0007 should account for clonal escape routes (such as apheresis) that decouple drug exposure from signature presence.

**Clonal haematopoiesis (CH) contamination (hypothesis:0001-non-tumor-signal-contamination, topic:clonal-hematopoiesis-contamination).** The paper reinforces that TP53-mutated CH clones in blood are a pre-existing substrate for tMN evolution. This has direct relevance to the pipeline's `ch_priority_gene` flagging (DNMT3A, TET2, TP53, ASXL1, PPM1D, CHEK2, PRPF8) and the `annotate_ch.py` step: TP53 mutations in blood-derived samples may represent pre-leukemic CH rather than tumour-intrinsic driver events.

**Cross-study mutation meta-analysis.** The distinction between Mode 1 (chemotherapy-selected, signature-positive, complex CNAs, TP53-enriched) and Mode 2 (apheresis-escaped, signature-negative, de-novo-AML-like) creates natural confounders for cross-study aggregation of haematological malignancies: studies with high ASCT rates will differ systematically from those without, independent of tumour biology. Stratifying on treatment history or study-level treatment context is important when pooling AML/MDS studies in cBioPortal.

## Limitations

- This is a 2-page editorial comment, not a primary research article. Quantitative claims and methodological details are summaries of Diamond et al. and should be verified against the primary paper.
- The primary study cohort is relatively small (40 tMN WGS samples); the temporal-barcoding analysis was performed on a subset (8 cases with chemotherapy signatures).
- The apheresis-escape model is described as "difficult to prove directly" even by the authors; the four lines of supporting evidence are circumstantial (absence of signature, timing, targeted sequencing of a small subset).
- The commentary does not distinguish between SBS31 and SBS35 in terms of relative signal strength or clinical utility, nor does it address the SBS-MM1 signature's biological mechanism in detail.
- Generalisation beyond myeloid neoplasms following autologous transplant contexts is not discussed.

## Model / Tool Availability

No tool or model released by this commentary. The primary study (Diamond et al. 2023) presumably released analytical code and processed WGS data, but this is not discussed in the commentary itself.

## Follow-up

- **Diamond et al. 2023** (Blood 141:2359-2371) — the primary research article this commentary discusses; should be read for quantitative details, full cohort description, and complete signature analyses.
- **Bolton et al. 2020** (Nat Genet 52:1219-1226) — cancer therapy shapes CH fitness landscape; directly cited for the platinum-exposed CH cohort used in Diamond et al.'s analysis.
- **Maura et al. 2019** (Nat Commun 10:2969) — practical guide for mutational signature analysis in haematological malignancies; provides methodological context for SBS signature decomposition in this setting.
- **Rustad et al. 2020** (Nat Commun 11:1917) — timing initiation of multiple myeloma; used for temporal barcoding extension to post-platinum MM cases.
- Questions raised for hypothesis:0007: (1) Should studies enriched for tMN/AML post-ASCT be flagged in the cross-study meta-analysis as potentially signature-confounded? (2) Are SBS31/SBS35/SBS-MM1 among the "known-signature positive controls" expected to be recoverable in the cBioPortal haematological cohorts?
