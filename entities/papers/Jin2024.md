# Accurate and sensitive mutational signature analysis with MuSiCal

- **Authors:** Hu Jin, Doga C. Gulhan, Benedikt Geiger, Daniel Ben-Isvy, David Geng, Viktor Ljungstrom, Peter J. Park
- **Year:** 2024
- **Journal:** Nature Genetics, Vol. 56, pp. 541-552
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41588-024-01659-0
- **BibTeX key:** Jin2024
- **Source:** PDF

## Key Contribution

MuSiCal (Mutational Signature Calculator) is a rigorous analytical framework that addresses two fundamental problems in the standard mutational signature analysis workflow: (1) the non-uniqueness of NMF solutions biases de novo signature discovery, especially for flat signatures; and (2) standard NNLS refitting systematically over-assigns signatures, introducing spurious assignments that can mislead clinical interpretation. MuSiCal introduces minimum-volume NMF (mvNMF) for discovery and likelihood-based sparse NNLS for refitting, plus a data-driven in silico validation module for parameter optimization. Applied to >2,700 PCAWG cancer genomes, MuSiCal delivers an improved catalog of both SBS and ID signatures, discovering nine new indel signatures not present in the current COSMIC catalog.

## Methods

**Algorithm architecture (four modules):**

1. **Preprocessing** — cohort stratification and outlier removal. Samples with MMRD (mismatch repair deficiency) and/or POLE-exo domain mutations are removed and analyzed separately in a tissue-independent manner before main signature discovery.

2. **De novo discovery via mvNMF** — minimum-volume NMF adds a regularization term that penalizes the volume of the convex cone formed by discovered signatures, promoting the unique minimum-volume solution among all NMF-equivalent solutions. Theoretically, mvNMF recovers the true underlying signatures under mild assumptions satisfied by tumor somatic mutations, whereas standard NMF requires much stronger (often unrealistic) assumptions. A parameter-free method infers the number of signatures from the data itself.

3. **Matching and refitting via likelihood-based sparse NNLS** — an observed mutation spectrum is decomposed into existing signatures using multinomial likelihood to assess signature presence. Rather than a fixed percentage threshold (the standard NNLS approach), MuSiCal iteratively adds/subtracts signatures based on their impact on the multinomial log-likelihood, applied bidirectionally. This correctly handles flat signatures (which need many mutations to achieve statistical significance) differently from sparse signatures (which can be statistically supported with few mutations in a small number of trinucleotide channels).

4. **In silico validation / parameter optimization** — a data-driven framework that simulates synthetic datasets from the inferred signature assignments and compares de novo discoveries from simulated vs. real data. The pair of likelihood thresholds that minimizes cosine distance between W\_data and W\_simul (both transformed from simulation and from data) selects the sparsest valid parameter set.

**Benchmarking:** Simulation studies using tumor-type-specific SBS signatures and Dirichlet-distributed exposures from 25 PCAWG tumor types (10 independent simulations each). Metrics include area under the precision-recall curve (auPRC) for de novo discovery, and cosine distances between assignments from simulated vs. real data for refitting quality. Comparisons against SigProfilerExtractor, SignatureAnalyzer, signature.tools.lib, SigneR, thresholded NNLS, sigLASSO, and the Decomposition module of SigProfilerExtractor.

**PCAWG reanalysis:** >2,700 cancer genomes reanalyzed for both SBS and ID signatures. Tumor-type-specific de novo discovery performed after stratification and outlier removal. An updated catalog of ID and SBS signatures was constructed.

## Key Findings

**De novo discovery (mvNMF vs. NMF):**
- mvNMF reduces cosine error by 67-98% on average across different signatures within the same tumor type compared to NMF.
- The improvement is especially notable for flat signatures (e.g., SBS3), which standard NMF systematically distorts: NMF misidentifies SBS3 as a composite involving the false SBS39.
- MuSiCal achieves auPRC of 0.929 vs. 0.893 for SigProfilerExtractor across 25 PCAWG tumor types.

**Refitting (likelihood-based sparse NNLS vs. thresholded NNLS):**
- auPRC 0.872 vs. 0.824 for thresholded NNLS; also outperforms sigLASSO and SigProfilerExtractor Decomposition.
- At any fixed threshold, thresholded NNLS either assigns too many false signatures (threshold too low) or misses true ones (threshold too high); the likelihood-based approach identifies the correct active signature set without requiring threshold tuning.

**PCAWG reanalysis — ID signature catalog:**
- Of 18 ID signatures in the current COSMIC catalog, 16 are re-discovered with modified spectra; ID15 and ID16 are not found (likely artifactual).
- Nine new ID signatures (ID19-26 plus ID11b) are discovered. ID11b (cosine similarity 0.94 to COSMIC ID11) is dominated by T insertions and distinguished from ID11. ID19-26 have cosine similarity <0.8 to any COSMIC signature.
- An updated ID catalog is proposed: replace COSMIC ID1-14, 17, and 18 with MuSiCal-derived versions; remove ID15 and ID16; add ID11b and ID19-26.

**PCAWG reanalysis — SBS signatures:**
- Most SBS signatures discovered match the current COSMIC catalog (SBS1-60 and SBS84-94).
- Six new SBS signatures (SBS95-100) are identified, mainly from MMRD tumors.
- SBS40 — a flat signature annotated as having unknown etiology, correlated with patient age in some tumor types — is found to be over-assigned by PCAWG (nearly universally assigned regardless of tumor type). MuSiCal restricts SBS40 assignment to kidney and bladder cancers, where ID5 correlation supports a mononucleotide repeat insertion mechanism. The MuSiCal-derived SBS40 has higher T>N mutation proportion (57%) than COSMIC SBS40 (41%).

**SBS-ID associations inform etiologies:**
- A Pearson correlation analysis between SBS and ID signature exposures (per-sample, MMRD and POLE samples analyzed separately) reveals several novel associations:
  - ID11b correlates with SBS4 and SBS92 (tobacco smoking signatures) and ID3 (a known tobacco signature), suggesting ID11b represents another component of tobacco smoking-related mutagenesis.
  - ID14 (1-bp C insertions at nonrepetitive regions; 1-bp T insertions at long mononucleotide T repeats) correlates with SBS10 and SBS28, suggesting POLE-exo association.
  - ID26 (1-bp T deletions at 4- and 5-bp mononucleotide T repeats) correlates with SBS14 and IS15, suggesting MMRD origin.

**In silico validation (glioblastoma case study):**
- For PCAWG glioblastoma (n=39), MuSiCal assigns eight COSMIC signatures (SBS1, 2, 5, 8, 12, 13, 30, 31), whereas PCAWG assigns four (SBS1, 5, 30, 40). In silico validation shows MuSiCal assignments have higher consistency between simulation and data (smaller cosine distances), confirming improved accuracy. PCAWG's broad SBS40 assignment is flagged as artifactual over-assignment.

## Relevance

**Direct relevance to h08 (agnostic covariate-signature association):**

MuSiCal is directly relevant to h08 in several ways:

1. **Signature assignment quality is a prerequisite for h08a positive-control recovery.** h08a requires recovering UV→SBS7, smoking→SBS4, APOBEC3-expr→SBS2/13, and MMR-loss→SBS6/15/26 from agnostic covariate association. If the input signature exposures are corrupted by systematic over-assignment (the SBS40 problem documented here), the association scan will produce spurious hits or miss true signal. MuSiCal's refitting provides the cleanest available per-sample signature exposures.

2. **The SBS40 over-assignment problem is a cautionary tale for h08.** MuSiCal shows that PCAWG assigns SBS40 ubiquitously across tumor types via NNLS over-assignment. Any covariate↔SBS40 association discovered in h08 using PCAWG-assigned exposures would be artifactual. Using MuSiCal exposures (SBS40 restricted to kidney/bladder) dramatically changes the interpretability of this signature.

3. **The flat-signature distortion from standard NMF is relevant to h08b novelty claims.** Many signatures with unknown etiology (including SBS40 and clock-like signatures) are flat. Standard NMF systematically distorts flat signature recovery. Novel h08b hits on flat-signature exposures must account for whether the assigned exposures are reliable.

4. **The SBS-ID co-occurrence approach is a template for the agnostic association layer in h08.** Jin et al. demonstrate that pairwise correlation of signature exposures across samples reveals etiological relationships (tobacco signature co-occurrence, POLE association). h08 generalizes this to include non-signature covariates (clinical fields, expression modules).

5. **The ID signature catalog update matters for the cBioPortal cross-study aggregation.** The project ingests cBioPortal MAFs that contain indel calls. If downstream signature decomposition is added (per q018/q019), the updated ID catalog with nine new signatures will affect the decomposition results for indel-rich tumor types.

6. **Positive-control signatures are well-grounded here.** MuSiCal confirms the spectra and prevalence of SBS4 (smoking, lung), SBS7a/b/c/d (UV, skin melanoma), SBS2/13 (APOBEC, widespread), and SBS6/15/26 (MMR, MSI-H) — the exact signatures targeted by h08a. The simulation benchmarks for skin melanoma (Fig. 3a) also validate MuSiCal's precision/recall (F1=0.96) on the UV signatures.

**Cross-study meta-analysis relevance:**
The cBioPortal pipeline aggregates across heterogeneous studies using heterogeneous sequencing panels. The MuSiCal preprocessing module (stratification + outlier removal; MMRD/POLE separation) is directly relevant to the pipeline's existing t083 label canonicalization and the planned hypermutator annotation pipeline (TMB-based, POLE hotspot detection). MuSiCal's recommendation to analyze MMRD and POLE samples tissue-independently aligns with the pipeline's `matched_normal_studies` + CH-aware annotation design.

## Limitations

- MuSiCal operates on the standard 96-channel SBS and 83-channel ID definitions. These lack more detailed context information (e.g., extended 192-channel or transcriptional-strand context), so some identified signatures may correspond to multiple mutational processes — leading to interpretation ambiguity.
- Structural variant (SV) and copy number signatures are outside scope; MuSiCal does not address these modalities.
- The method requires whole-genome or at least whole-exome data for per-sample signature discovery; panel-based sequencing is not addressed.
- The global SBS catalog update is not performed because no substantial global difference between COSMIC and MuSiCal SBS spectra was observed (except SBS40). Only the six new SBS95-100 signatures are added to the existing COSMIC catalog.
- The in silico validation framework requires an initial assignment to simulate from; a poor initial assignment could lead the optimization to an incorrect optimum.
- Joint discovery of different signature types (SBS + ID together) is not supported; MuSiCal analyzes them separately.
