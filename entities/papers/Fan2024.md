# Neuroscience in peripheral cancers: tumors hijacking nerves and neuroimmune crosstalk

- **Authors:** Hua-Yang Fan, Xin-Hua Liang, Ya-Ling Tang
- **Year:** 2024
- **Journal:** MedComm, 5(11):e784
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1002/mco2.784
- **BibTeX key:** Fan2024
- **Source:** PDF

## Key Contribution

This is a comprehensive narrative review — described by the authors as the first comprehensive review of peripheral nerve–tumor–immune crosstalk — that synthesises the bidirectional relationship between the peripheral nervous system (PNS) and non-CNS (peripheral) solid tumors. The central thesis is that tumors both respond to and actively remodel neural architecture: cancer cells hijack neural signaling to promote proliferation, invasion, angiogenesis, and immune evasion, while simultaneously secreting neurotrophic factors (NGF, GDNF, BDNF) and axon-guidance molecules (semaphorins) to reshape nerve fiber identity and density. The review maps this across the autonomic nervous system (ANS), sensory nerves, and the enteric nervous system (ENS), characterizes the triangular nerve–tumor–immune crosstalk in which immune cells both express neural receptors and modify nerve function, and surveys current clinical translation (beta-blockers, anti-NGF antibodies, vagotomy, cholinergic agonists) with an active clinical-trials table.

This peripheral-cancer framing complements paper:Mancusi2023, paper:Venkatesh2019, paper:Magnon2023, paper:Mravec2008, paper:Keough2022, paper:Hanahan2023, paper:Kizil2024, and paper:Xiong2023.

## Methods

This is a narrative (non-systematic) review. No primary data were generated; no meta-analysis, statistical pooling, or PRISMA protocol is described. Evidence is drawn from mouse model denervation experiments (surgical sympathectomy, chemical sympathectomy with 6-OHDA, vagotomy, capsaicin ablation), in vitro cancer cell–neuron co-culture systems, patient-derived xenograft models, clinical observational and retrospective cohort studies, and early-phase clinical trials. Cancer types covered systematically include pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC), prostate cancer, gastric cancer, colorectal cancer (CRC), breast cancer, melanoma, oral squamous cell carcinoma (OSCC), head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC), ovarian cancer, hepatocellular carcinoma, and nasopharyngeal carcinoma. The review organises evidence around nerve type (sympathetic, parasympathetic, sensory/nociceptive, enteric, motor) and biological process (initiation, progression/metastasis, perineural invasion, tumor-induced nerve remodeling/neurogenesis, neuroimmune crosstalk). ## Key Findings

**1. Nerve-type-specific and cancer-type-specific effects are the rule, not the exception.**
Sympathetic (adrenergic) nerves are broadly tumor-promoting across prostate, breast, gastric, ovarian, and skin cancers — sympathectomy suppresses tumor growth in multiple models. Parasympathetic (cholinergic) nerves are context-dependent: pro-tumorigenic in prostate cancer (M1/M4 muscarinic receptors promote growth and chemoresistance) but anti-tumorigenic in PDAC (vagotomy increases pancreatic tumor incidence in mouse models by removing a cholinergic brake via the M1 receptor). Sensory nerves are similarly context-dependent: in breast cancer they act parasympathetically and their ablation via capsaicin increases metastasis; in OSCC/PDAC they promote tumor growth and their denervation suppresses it. The enteric nervous system (ENS) is strongly pro-tumorigenic in CRC and PDAC.

**2. Named neurotrophic factors and receptors driving each cancer type.**
- NGF (nerve growth factor) / TRKA receptor: prostate cancer neurogenesis; OSCC nociceptive secretion of CGRP/SP; breast cancer nerve-fiber recruitment. Anti-NGF antibody tanezumab is in clinical trials (NCT02609828 for bone metastases).
- GDNF (glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor) / GFRα / RET: PDAC Schwann cell paracrine signaling promoting tumor migration toward neurons; ENS tropism in CRC and PDAC.
- BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) / TRKB: axonogenesis across multiple cancers; cancer cell secretion attracting nerves.
- Semaphorin 3A (SEMA3A): PDAC cancer cell-derived, reprograms sensory nerves to sympathetic adrenergic phenotype.
- CHRNA5 (nicotinic acetylcholine receptor α5): knockdown promotes cell-cycle arrest in breast cancer.
- Adrb2 (β2-adrenergic receptor / ADRB2): knockdown reduces vascular density, inhibits prostate cancer progression; promotes VEGF/VEGFC-driven lymphangiogenesis in breast cancer; mediates MACCI upregulation in gastric cancer under stress.
- CGRP and substance P (SP): nociceptive neuropeptides elevated in OSCC histologic samples; amplify angiogenesis, proliferation, metastasis via multiple downstream effectors.
- DCLK1 (doublecortin-like kinase 1): tuft-cell marker in gastric cancer; DCLK1+ cells and nerves are the ACh source in mouse gastric mucosa, a self-reinforcing loop promoting tumor formation.
- TRPV1 / TRPA1: sensory neuron ion channels; enhanced expression drives precancerous pancreatic inflammation; ablation of sensory neurons in pancreatitis mouse model eliminated inflammation and reduced cancer progression.
- MMP-2 / MMP-9: elevated by norepinephrine-induced STAT3 phosphorylation in ovarian cancer cells; also elevated in nasopharyngeal carcinoma.
- p53 loss in OSCC: drives nerve reprogramming to adrenergic phenotype via exosomal miRNA.

**3. Perineural invasion (PNI) is a histopathological hallmark with defined molecular mediators.**
PNI incidence: ~98% in pancreatic cancer, ~80% in HNSCC, ~75% in prostate cancer, ~33% in CRC. Schwann cells interact with cancer cells via NCAM1, MAG, L1CAM (on Schwann cells) and E-cadherin, MUC1, L1CAM (on cancer cells); GDNF / RET and CX3CL1 / CX3CR1 are key paracrine axes. Schwann cells form tumor-activated Schwann cell tracks (TASTs) that create invasion pathways. Neural progenitor cells recruited from brain can cross blood–brain barrier, infiltrate peripheral tumors (prostate, breast), and differentiate into adrenergic neurons.

**4. Tumor-induced neurogenesis (axonogenesis) is a hallmark of aggressive tumors.**
Tumor tissues have higher nerve fiber density than surrounding normal tissue. Cancer cells secrete NGF, GDNF, BDNF plus extracellular vesicles (EVs) carrying miRNAs that reprogram axon identity. In PDAC, loss of p53 drives nerve reprogramming via exosomal miRNAs. Elevated NGF precursors in prostate cancer associate with adrenergic nerve infiltration. This process is therapeutically targetable: anti-NGF antibodies, TRKA inhibitors, and NGF-targeted siRNA via nanoparticles reduce tumor growth in animal models.

**5. Neuroimmune crosstalk forms a three-way circuit: nerves ↔ tumor ↔ immune cells.**
- T cells: express α- and β-ARs; chronic β2-AR signaling suppresses CD8+ T-cell cytotoxicity, promotes apoptosis; β1-AR blockade enhances CD8+ T-cell function and synergizes with ICIs. Acute stress boosts IFN production in CD4+ T cells, but chronic stress drives immune exhaustion.
- MDSCs: express β2-ARs; adrenergic signaling enhances their immunosuppressive function; α2-AR further reinforces suppression. β2-AR knockdown in MDSCs reverses immunosuppression.
- TAMs: recruited to perineural invasion sites via CCL2 from Schwann cells/axons; vagotomy M1-polarizes TAMs; GABA signaling (from CD8+ T cells or cancer cells) promotes M2 polarization and immunosuppression. In breast and pancreatic cancers, TAM density increase is nerve-dependent.
- NK cells: stress and adrenergic signaling impair NK cytotoxicity.
- GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid): produced by cancer cells and CD8+ T cells; activates GABA-BR on macrophages, suppressing CCL4/CCL5, reducing T-cell and dendritic-cell infiltration in colon cancer.

**6. Clinical translation: beta-blockers are the leading candidate class.**
Multiple trials are underway: propranolol and carvedilol (non-selective β-AR blockers) across prostate, breast, hepatocellular, melanoma, soft tissue sarcoma, and combined pancreatic/hepatic/biliary cancers, often in combination with ICI or chemotherapy. Bethanechol (cholinergic agonist) is in trial for PDAC. Botulinum toxin A (autonomic denervation) in prostate cancer (NCT01520441). Nine patients with metastatic melanoma treated with propranolol + pembrolizumab achieved 78% objective response rate with elevated IFN-γ and reduced IL-6. β1-AR blocker nebivolol inhibits oxidative phosphorylation in cancer cells and reduces tumor angiogenesis.

## Relevance

**`H4` (brain/CNS-cancer artifact) — directly refuted as the sole explanation.** This review is devoted entirely to peripheral, non-CNS cancers (pancreatic, prostate, gastric, breast, oral/HNSCC, colorectal, ovarian, skin). The extensive evidence for nerve dependence in these cancers — sympathectomy experiments, vagotomy, PNI epidemiology, ENS co-culture data — establishes beyond reasonable doubt that neural genes and neural signaling are biologically active in peripheral tumors, not merely contaminating signal from adjacent CNS tissue. `H4` may still explain a subset of the gene-length confound in brain-adjacent or CNS cancers, but it cannot explain the pattern in pancreatic or prostate cancer contexts.

**`H1` (hijack top-down neural circuitry) — strongly supported, but redefined as PNS-mediated.** The "hijacking" concept articulated by the review is precisely the `H1` local note label, reframed at the PNS level: tumors actively recruit, remodel, and co-opt peripheral nerve fibers to sustain growth. The bidirectional loop (tumor secretes NGF → recruits sympathetic/sensory nerves → nerves release norepinephrine/neuropeptides → tumor growth and immune suppression → more NGF) is a peripheral analogue of top-down circuit capture.

**`H2` (via immune modulation) — confirmed as a major mechanistic arm.** The neuroimmune crosstalk section (Section 4) documents that adrenergic signaling suppresses CD8+ T cells, expands MDSCs, M2-polarizes TAMs, and impairs NK cells — all in peripheral tumors. This is `H2` operating via the PNS rather than CNS. The combination of β-AR blockers with ICIs is already in clinical trials.

**`H3` (aberrant developmental/oncofetal expression) — partially supported.** NGF, GDNF, BDNF, TRKA, TRKB, and axon-guidance molecules (semaphorins, L1CAM, NCAM1) are developmental neurogenesis genes whose re-expression by cancer cells drives tumor innervation. Neural progenitor cells recruited from the brain and differentiating into adrenergic neurons in peripheral tumors (prostate, breast) is a striking oncofetal parallel.

**`H5` (misannotation) — not addressed.** The review does not engage with the possibility that cBioPortal mutation frequencies for neural genes could reflect sequencing or annotation artefact.

**Data-driven neural-gene definition — partial contribution.** The review does not provide a computational gene list, but it supplies a curated receptor/ligand set (NGF, GDNF, BDNF, TRKA/TRKB/GFRα, ADRB2, CHRNA5, DCLK1, SEMA3A, CGRP/CALCRL, SP/TAC1, TRPV1, TRPA1, CX3CL1/CX3CR1, NCAM1, L1CAM, RET, VEGF/VEGFC, MMP2/MMP9, COX2) that constitutes a biological positive-control set for "neural signaling in solid tumors." Genes encoding these ligands and receptors are candidates for a label-free neural gene list derived from pathway/interaction databases rather than brain-region co-expression.

## Limitations

- Narrative (non-systematic) review without stated inclusion/exclusion criteria or quality assessment of cited studies; selection of evidence may reflect author perspective (oral/maxillofacial surgery group, Sichuan University).
- Effect directions for nerve type × cancer type are drawn from heterogeneous mouse models; species differences in neural anatomy and tumor microenvironment biology limit direct human translation.
- The review acknowledges that motor nerve involvement in tumor biology is largely unexplored and that research on neutrophils and B cells in tumor–nerve interactions is limited.
- Most mechanistic evidence comes from a small number of cancer types (PDAC, prostate, gastric); extrapolation to all peripheral cancers is speculative.
- Clinical trial evidence is preliminary; no phase III results are included in the reviewed literature. The 78% ORR in nine melanoma patients on propranolol + pembrolizumab is a small case series [UNVERIFIED as to whether this refers to a formal trial report or preliminary data].
- The review does not address gene-length bias, somatic mutation frequency analysis, or computational approaches — the link to the project's mutation-pattern question requires inference rather than direct evidence from this paper.
- The paper does not define a curated "neural gene" list computationally; the receptor/ligand catalogue is assembled qualitatively.

## Follow-up

- **`H4` falsification.** This paper provides sufficient evidence that neural signaling is functionally active in peripheral (non-CNS) cancers. The next step for the project is to ask whether the top-mutated neural genes (NKAIN2, KCNIP4, TAFA2/FAM19A2, RIT2, CALN1, RBFOX1, LSAMP, SGCZ, OPCML) overlap with the receptor/ligand/axon-guidance gene sets catalogued here. If they do not, a separate explanation (gene length, `H5` misannotation, or developmental re-expression of brain-lineage genes in neural-crest-derived cancers) must be invoked.
- **Label-free neural gene set construction.** The receptor/ligand gene catalogue in this review (ADRB2, ADRB1, CHRNA5, CHRNB2, GDNF, GFRA1, RET, NGF, TRKA/NTRK1, BDNF, TRKB/NTRK2, SEMA3A, L1CAM, NCAM1, CX3CL1, CX3CR1, CALCRL, TAC1, TRPV1, TRPA1, DCLK1, VEGF/VEGFA, VEGFC, MMP2, MMP9, PTGS2/COX2, MACCI/MARCKS) can seed a programmatic pull from STRING, Reactome "Neuronal System," or Human Protein Atlas "brain-expressed" gene sets to generate a data-driven positive-control list independent of gene-name searches.
- **Cancer-type stratification prediction.** Per-nerve-type dependence (sympathetic pro-tumorigenic in prostate/breast/gastric, cholinergic anti-tumorigenic in PDAC, sensory context-dependent) predicts that neural-gene mutation signals should differ across cancer type in systematic ways. This is testable against the pipeline's `gene_cancer_study_ratio_annotated.feather` output — does ADRB2 or NGF mutation frequency vary across cancer types in the direction the review predicts?
- **Covariate for hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and.** Stress hormone levels (norepinephrine, cortisol) are not typically in cBioPortal clinical metadata, but beta-blocker prescription status or depression/anxiety clinical annotations could serve as proxy covariates for β-AR–pathway activity. Worth checking whether cBioPortal studies include treatment history fields for beta-blockers.
- **PNI as a cancer-type weight.** The PNI incidence figures (98% PDAC, 80% HNSCC, 75% prostate, 33% CRC) provide a biologically principled weighting scheme for cancer types in a neural-gene signal-to-noise analysis: higher PNI rate → stronger prior that neural mutations are functional rather than passenger.
- **DCLK1 and LSAMP in cBioPortal.** DCLK1 (a tuft-cell/neurogenesis kinase) and LSAMP (limbic system-associated membrane protein, a neural adhesion molecule) appear in both the top-mutated-gene list from the project and in the molecular biology covered here. Their canonical neural function + this review's evidence for aberrant re-expression in GI tumors (DCLK1) and potential neural-crest relevance (LSAMP) warrants checking their mutation frequency specifically in PDAC and CRC studies in the pipeline.
