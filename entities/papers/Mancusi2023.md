# The neuroscience of cancer

- **Authors:** Rebecca Mancusi, Michelle Monje
- **Year:** 2023
- **Journal:** Nature
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-023-05968-y
- **BibTeX key:** Mancusi2023
- **Source:** PDF

## Key Contribution

This flagship Nature review synthesizes the emerging field of "cancer neuroscience" — the bidirectional interplay between the nervous system and tumors across initiation, progression, and metastasis. The central argument is that the nervous system is not merely a bystander in the tumor microenvironment but an active regulator of cancer biology at both local (paracrine and synaptic) and systemic (autonomic and neuroendocrine) levels. Tumors in turn remodel and hijack neural circuits and neural developmental programs. The review integrates evidence from CNS gliomas, peripheral cancers (prostate, gastric, pancreatic, breast, melanoma, basal cell carcinoma), and brain metastases, organizing the mechanisms into three conceptual tiers: (1) direct neuron-to-cancer-cell synaptic and paracrine signaling, (2) neural regulation of the tumor immune microenvironment, and (3) systemic nervous system effects. This constitutes the most comprehensive synthesis of the field available and defines its conceptual vocabulary.

## Methods

This is a narrative review (no original experimental data). The authors synthesize published preclinical (mouse genetic models, optogenetics, xenografts, co-culture) and clinical (histopathology, electrocorticography, retrospective survival analyses) evidence. Figures were created with BioRender.com. The review is organized by mechanism and tumor type.

## Key Findings

### CNS glioma — neuron-to-glioma synaptic integration

1. **NLGN3 (neuroligin-3) is a critical paracrine mediator.** Activity-regulated shedding of NLGN3 from OPCs (via ADAM10 sheddase) drives high-grade and low-grade glioma proliferation in multiple preclinical models. NLGN3 mRNA and protein levels inversely correlate with survival in human glioblastoma. Therapeutic blocking of NLGN3 shedding is in early-phase clinical trial (NCT04295759). NLGN3 upregulates synapse-associated genes in glioma cells and activates PI3K-mTOR, SRC, and RAS oncogenic pathways.

2. **BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) is a second key paracrine factor.** Activity-regulated BDNF secretion from neurons promotes glioma proliferation (both high-grade and low-grade) and reinforces glutamatergic neuron-to-glioma synaptic currents.

3. **IGF-1 (insulin-like growth factor-1)** was identified as the key activity-regulated paracrine factor in olfactory bulb high-grade glioma.

4. **Neuron-to-glioma synapses form via AMPA receptors.** A subset (5-10%) of glioma cells form functional glutamatergic AMPA receptor-mediated synapses with neurons (confirmed by electron microscopy, electrophysiology, calcium imaging). The synaptically integrated subpopulation corresponds to the most malignant cells in pediatric high-grade glioma (resembling OPCs) and in adult glioblastoma (resembling neuronal precursor cells, NPCs).

5. **A second glioma cell subpopulation is driven by potassium-evoked depolarizing currents** (not direct synapses). These cells form a gap junction-coupled network, amplifying and synchronizing calcium transients across the tumor. Hub cells drive autonomous oscillatory calcium transients via KCa3.1 (calcium-activated potassium channel). The gap junction-coupled network integrates synaptically and electrically into the brain.

6. **Glypican-3 (GPC3) and TSP-1 (thrombospondin-1)** are glioma-secreted synaptogenic factors that promote neuronal hyperexcitability, drive glioma-associated seizures, and remodel functional neural circuits including language circuitry in glioblastoma patients.

7. **Brain metastases (breast-to-brain, B2BMs)** express NMDA receptors (NMDAR), localize at glutamatergic synapses forming pseudo-tripartite synapses, and use neuronal glutamate to enhance their metastatic growth. NMDAR knockdown substantially reduces B2BM colonization in mouse models.

### Peripheral nervous system — cancer-type-specific autonomic and sensory regulation

8. **Prostate cancer — adrenergic/cholinergic duality.** Ablating adrenergic (sympathetic) nerves prevents prostate xenograft growth and lymph node dissemination; parasympathetic (muscarinic, CHRM1) signaling promotes dissemination and metastasis. Doublecortin-positive NPCs migrate from the brain to the prostate cancer niche and generate new adrenergic neurons that promote cancer initiation. Beta-2 adrenergic signaling promotes angiogenesis.

9. **Gastric cancer — cholinergic promotion via vagus nerve.** Acetylcholine from parasympathetic tuft cells signals to muscarinic receptors on gastric cancer cells to drive proliferation and NGF secretion, creating a feed-forward loop. Vagotomy reduces gastric tumor burden. Wnt and Notch signaling are activated downstream of cholinergic input.

10. **Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) — bidirectional adrenergic/cholinergic.** Adrenergic/sympathetic signaling (noradrenaline, catecholamines) and NGF promote PDAC growth; cholinergic (parasympathetic) signaling suppresses it. TRPV1 and TRPA1 channels in sensory neurons are upregulated in early PDAC; capsaicin-induced sensory neuron ablation abrogated CNS inflammation and reduced PDAC initiation. The peripheral inflammatory signals are transmitted to the CNS via afferent nerves.

11. **NGF (nerve growth factor)** is a recurring pro-tumorigenic neurotrophic factor across multiple cancer types (prostate, gastric, pancreatic, breast) and correlates with perineural invasion and prognosis.

12. **Basal cell carcinoma — Hedgehog (Hh) pathway via mechanosensory nerves.** Touch dome epithelial stem cells are innervated by mechanosensory nerves; Hh ligand from these nerves drives BCC oncogenesis. Denervation strongly abrogates BCC growth.

13. **Melanoma — CGRP (calcitonin gene-related peptide) promotes T cell exhaustion.** Nociceptor neurons release CGRP in the melanoma TME (stimulated by SLPI from cancer cells), which acts on T lymphocytes to promote exhaustion, limiting anti-cancer immunity.

14. **Breast cancer — sympathetic vs. parasympathetic opposition.** Sympathetic innervation promotes primary breast tumor growth and metastasis through noradrenaline; parasympathetic and sensory nerves suppress growth and reduce PD-1/PD-L1 expression, pointing to nerve-type-specific opposite effects.

### Neuro-immune mechanisms

15. **GABA** from B cells, macrophages, and tumor cells suppresses cytotoxic T cell responses across multiple cancers (colon adenocarcinoma via GABA-B receptor on T cells; colon adenocarcinoma via immune-suppressive macrophage phenotype). B cell-specific GABA deletion via Gad67 knockout significantly reduced colon adenocarcinoma growth.

16. **Serotonin** from platelets promotes PD-L1 upregulation on pancreatic and gastric cancer cells via TGM2-mediated histone serotonylation, suppressing CD8+ T cell infiltration.

17. **Noradrenaline** from sympathetic nerves or circulating catecholamines upregulates myeloid-derived suppressor cell states, PD-1, and drives T cell exhaustion. Adrenergic signaling limits immune cell trafficking via effects on vasculature.

18. **Vagus nerve** has systemic immunomodulatory roles via efferent cholinergic pathways; brain regions encode and retrieve specific immune responses via cortical-autonomic circuits.

The review explicitly names (mechanistically important in their contexts): **NLGN3**, **BDNF**, **NGF**, **IGF-1**, **AMPAR** (AMPA receptors), **NMDAR** (NMDA receptors), **KCa3.1**, **GPC3** (glypican-3), **TSP-1** (thrombospondin-1), **ADAM10**, **CGRP** (calcitonin gene-related peptide), **GABA / GAD67 / GABA-B / GABA-A receptors**, **serotonin / TGM2**, **TRPV1**, **TRPA1**, **NGF / TrkB / TrkA**, **NF1** (neurofibromatosis 1), **PIK3CA**, **Wnt / Notch**, **CHRM1** (muscarinic acetylcholine receptor 1), **CHRM3**, **beta-2 adrenergic receptor (ADRB2)**, **PD-1 / PD-L1**, **CCL4 / CCL5**, **SLPI**, **CSF-1R**, **doublecortin (DCX)**.

## Relevance

**H1 (tumors hijack top-down neural circuitry): STRONGLY SUPPORTED.** The review's core thesis is that tumors hijack activity-regulated developmental and circuit-maintenance mechanisms — the same ones that regulate OPC proliferation, myelination, and circuit plasticity. Gliomas integrate into neural circuits via AMPAR-mediated synapses and gap junctions. Brain metastases co-opt NMDAR glutamate signaling. This is precisely H1: tumors exploit neural circuitry to obtain growth signals.

**H2 (neural signal via immune-system modulation): STRONGLY SUPPORTED.** The review devotes an entire major section to neuro-immune cross-talk. Adrenergic signaling shapes myeloid and T cell states; GABA and serotonin modulate T cell cytotoxicity and macrophage polarization; CGRP drives T cell exhaustion in melanoma; vagus nerve regulates systemic immune trafficking. H2 and H1 are presented as complementary rather than competing mechanisms.

**H3 (neural signal as oncofetal / developmental deregulation): SUPPORTED AS A MECHANISM FOR CNS TUMORS, NOT PERIPHERAL.** The review explicitly frames glioma as hijacking "activity-regulated mechanisms of myelin plasticity" that govern normal OPC development. Glioma cells that are most synaptically integrated most resemble OPCs or NPCs (single-cell transcriptomics). However, for peripheral cancers the paper does not invoke aberrant developmental gene expression; instead it describes bona fide innervation of adult tissues. So H3 is supported specifically for the brain-tumor subset of the pipeline's neural gene enrichment.

**H4 (artifact from brain/CNS cancers in cohort): PARTIALLY ADDRESSED.** The review demonstrates that CNS cancers (gliomas) have the richest and best-characterized neuron-cancer interactions. If cBioPortal cohorts include substantial glioblastoma/glioma representation, this could inflate neural-gene mutation frequencies. However, the review also documents equally mechanistically important neural-gene roles in prostate, gastric, pancreatic, and breast cancers — so H4 does not fully explain the signal; peripheral cancers are genuinely neural-gene-enriched in function.

**H5 (genes are misannotated / have non-neural functions): WEAKENED for several specific genes.** The paper shows that NLGN3, BDNF, AMPAR subunits, KCa3.1, glypican-3, and TSP-1 are functionally active in cancer via their neural roles, not via misannotation. However the review also shows some "neural" molecules (GABA, serotonin) acting through non-neural cell types (B cells, platelets) in the tumor context — suggesting that for these molecules, the "neural gene" label partially reflects their broader signaling roles.

**Data-driven label-free neural-gene definition:** The paper does NOT provide a data-driven, label-free approach. All gene/pathway identifications are based on mechanistic hypothesis-driven experiments and curated neural function annotations. The paper does cite single-cell transcriptomic signatures (glioma cells most resembling OPCs or NPCs are most synaptically integrated) which offers a potential handle: one could use sc-RNAseq cell-type similarity scores to OPC/NPC reference atlases as a label-free proxy for "neural program activation." No expression atlas or computational resource is provided for direct reuse.

## Limitations

- The review is largely built on preclinical mouse models (optogenetics, genetic ablation, xenografts); human clinical validation for most mechanisms remains limited to histopathological correlates (perineural invasion, nerve density) and retrospective survival associations rather than prospective causal evidence.
- The review does not discuss somatic mutation patterns in neural genes — its evidence base is expression, electrophysiology, and pharmacological perturbation. It cannot directly explain why neural genes are in the top-mutated list of cBioPortal studies (the project's core question), since high mutation frequency ≠ causal neural-pathway engagement.
- Neural circuit mechanisms are circuit- and neuron-subtype-specific (e.g., IGF-1 is specific to mitral/tufted cells in the olfactory bulb). The review acknowledges this specificity is a challenge for generalizing across cancer types and CNS regions.
- The apparent contradiction between pro-tumorigenic sympathetic and anti-tumorigenic parasympathetic innervation (and vice versa in different cancers, e.g. gastric vs. pancreatic for cholinergic) highlights that "the nervous system" is not a monolithic pro- or anti-tumor entity; the pipeline would need cancer-type-specific models.
- No discussion of cancer-type cohort composition biases in genomic databases, which is the pipeline's key methodological concern (H4).
- No genomic/mutational data resources are provided; the paper is exclusively mechanistic.

## Model / Tool Availability

No software tools, datasets, or computational resources are released. The review cites numerous published preclinical models (mouse genetic models with NF1 mutation, IDH-wild-type, optogenetic constructs). The clinical trial targeting NLGN3 is NCT04295759 (early-phase). No expression atlas or single-cell reference is published in association with this review.

## Follow-up

- **For the pipeline:** Test whether the neural-gene enrichment in top-mutated-genes lists persists after stratifying by cancer type and removing CNS/glioma studies. If the signal collapses, H4 is the primary explanation. If it persists in epithelial cancers (prostate, gastric, pancreatic), it may reflect genuine somatic selection on neural-signaling pathway genes (NGF, NTRK genes, adrenergic receptors, neuroligin family).
- **NLGN3 specifically:** Check the somatic mutation frequency of NLGN3 in the pipeline's cross-study gene x cancer matrix. The paper establishes NLGN3 is upregulated at the expression/shedding level in glioma — is there evidence of somatic gain-of-function mutation, or is NLGN3 in the top-mutated list because of its large gene length (length-confounding, a known concern in the project)?
- **Neural gene definition via OPC/NPC transcriptomic identity:** Use Allen Brain Atlas or Human Cell Atlas OPC/NPC gene signatures as a label-free reference to define "neural genes" agnostically, then test whether the project's top-mutated genes are enriched for this signature beyond what length confounding explains.
- **NGF / TrkB / TrkA / NTRK genes:** These are both neural-function genes and established oncogenes (NTRK fusions are FDA-approved targets). Their presence in mutation frequency tables is biologically interpretable via the cancer neuroscience framework; annotate with NTRK fusion status in pipeline outputs.
- **GPC3 (glypican-3):** Cross-check against the project's top-mutated gene list. GPC3 is a large gene and a known hepatocellular carcinoma marker; it appears in this review as a glioma synaptogenic factor — a dual-function gene relevant to both H3 (oncofetal expression) and H1 (neural circuit hijacking).
- **Neuro-immune link (H2):** The paper's mechanistic framework predicts that in studies with available immune cell deconvolution data, neural-gene mutation status (e.g., ADRB2, CHRM1, NF1) should correlate with immune microenvironment composition. This is a testable covariate association in the pipeline's h08 framework.
