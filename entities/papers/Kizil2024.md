# Neural control of tumor immunity

- **Authors:** Burak Kizil, Francesco De Virgiliis, Christoph Scheiermann
- **Year:** 2024
- **Journal:** The FEBS Journal, Vol. 291, pp. 4670–4679
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1111/febs.17280
- **BibTeX key:** Kizil2024
- **Source:** PDF

## Key Contribution

This viewpoint/review synthesizes evidence that tumors are actively innervated by peripheral nerves — sensory, parasympathetic, and sympathetic — and that this innervation exerts bidirectional, context-dependent control over the tumor immune response. The paper's central argument is that nerve-derived neurotransmitters and neuropeptides (noradrenaline, acetylcholine, substance P, CGRP) act directly on receptors expressed by tumor-infiltrating immune cells (T cells, MDSCs, NK cells, DCs, macrophages), thereby modulating immune surveillance and checkpoint expression. Crucially, the direction of effect differs by nerve type and tumor context: sensory innervation generally suppresses anti-tumor immunity via CGRP; sympathetic innervation promotes T cell exhaustion and MDSC immunosuppression; parasympathetic/vagal activation tends to enhance anti-tumor immunity via acetylcholine. The paper positions pharmacological targeting of this neuro-immune axis — particularly beta-adrenergic blockade — as a clinically tractable adjunct to immune checkpoint inhibition.

## Methods

This is a narrative viewpoint/review article. No primary data are generated. The authors:

- Comprehensively survey experimental studies using genetic ablation of specific nerve populations (TRPV1+, Chrm1-Cre, Adrb1/2 knockout mice), chemical denervation (resiniferatoxin, 6-OHDA), and pharmacological manipulation (beta-blockers, muscarinic agonists/antagonists, vagotomy, electroacupuncture of the vagus nerve).
- Compile evidence across cancer model systems (melanoma B16F10, mammary carcinoma 4T1, pancreatic adenocarcinoma 6606PDA, lung adenocarcinoma Lewis lung, prostate cancer Hi-Myc, colorectal CT26/MC38, oral squamous cell carcinoma) and a limited set of clinical/human studies.
- Organize findings by nerve type (sensory, parasympathetic/PSNS, sympathetic/SNS) and immune effector cell type (CD8+ T cells, CD4+ T cells, Tregs, MDSCs, macrophages, NK cells, DCs, B cells).
- Discuss the therapeutic potential of beta-adrenergic blockade in combination with immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) based on clinical trial observations.

## Key Findings

1. **Sensory nerves exert predominantly immunosuppressive effects via CGRP, but pro-inflammatory effects via substance P.** Sensory neurons release calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP; encoded by CALCA) and substance P (SubP; encoded by TAC1). CGRP inhibits CD8+ cytotoxic T cell function — CGRP-deficient (Calca−/−) mice and CGRP-deficient oral cancer lines show increased CD4+ T, cytotoxic CD8+ T, and NK cell infiltration. Chemical/surgical ablation of TRPV1+ sensory neurons (resiniferatoxin) increases leukocyte recruitment and decreases immunosuppressive cells including Tregs and MDSCs in melanoma. In contrast, substance P (SP) administration delays tumor growth and increases NK/T cell activity, and SP stimulates synthesis of pro-inflammatory cytokines. Sensory nerves also impede tertiary lymphoid structure (TLS) formation and reduce T cell clonality in tumors (Vats et al. 2022, ref [32]).

2. **Parasympathetic (vagal) signaling acts primarily via acetylcholine and nicotinic/muscarinic receptors to enhance anti-tumor immunity.** Vagal activation (electroacupuncture, electrical stimulation) enhances CD8+ T and NK cell numbers and function in mammary carcinoma and pancreatic models. The anti-inflammatory reflex mediated by the vagus nerve suppresses MDSCs — vagal stimulation induces TFF2 secretion by memory CD4+ T cells, which inhibits MDSCs in colorectal cancer. Muscarinic acetylcholine receptor M1 (CHRM1) activation reduces pancreatic tumor incidence. Key receptors: nicotinic acetylcholine receptor α7 (α7nAChR), CHRM1.

3. **Sympathetic nervous system (SNS) signaling via beta-adrenergic receptors promotes tumor progression and immune evasion.** Noradrenaline (NA) released locally by sympathetic nerves preferentially binds α-adrenergic receptors (ADRA2, encoded by ADRA2A/B) and β-adrenergic receptors (ADRB1, ADRB2, ADRB3). ADRB2 is the most expressed and studied receptor in tumor immune cells. Key effects:
   - **T cell exhaustion:** Beta-1 adrenergic receptor (ADRB1) activation links sympathetic signaling to CD8+ T cell exhaustion in pancreatic, colon, and melanoma models (Globig et al. 2023, ref [47]). Adrenergic signaling increases exhausted CD8+ T cell proportions.
   - **MDSC expansion and immunosuppression:** ADRB2 signaling regulates MDSC metabolic pathways and drives expression of immunosuppressive molecules arginase-1 and PD-L1 (Mohammadpour et al. 2019, 2021, refs [51,52]). Beta-adrenergic signaling increases MDSC numbers from both murine and human peripheral blood cells.
   - **NK cell suppression:** Beta-adrenergic signaling decreases NK cytotoxicity (e.g., mammary carcinoma MADB106 model).
   - **Dendritic cell alteration:** Stress-induced adrenergic signaling alters DC subsets and CD8+ T cell activation in mammary carcinoma 4T1.
   - **PD-1/PD-L1 upregulation:** Sympathetic/parasympathetic nerve density positively correlates with PD-1 and PD-L1 expression in breast cancer; genetic denervation reduces PD-1 in tumor-infiltrating CD4+ and CD8+ T cells.

4. **Alpha-2 adrenergic receptor (ADRA2) activation has a distinct pro-immune effect via macrophage activation.** In colorectal cancer models (CT26, MC38, multiple others), α2-ADR agonists activate macrophages which in turn stimulate CD4+ and CD8+ T cells for anti-tumor immunity (Naulaerts et al. 2023, ref [49]).

5. **Beta-adrenergic blockade is the most clinically advanced therapeutic modality.** Beta-blockers (propranolol and others) have shown effectiveness in improving treatment outcomes across multiple cancer types (melanoma, prostate, breast, pancreatic, ovarian, non-small cell lung, colorectal) in clinical studies, especially combined with neoadjuvant PD-1 checkpoint inhibition or NSAIDs. A phase II randomized trial (Hiller et al. 2020) showed preoperative propranolol reduces biomarkers of metastasis in breast cancer.

6. **Stress and the SNS create a pro-tumorigenic TME via MDSC accumulation.** Mice under chronic stress show accelerated tumor growth, increased CD11b+ myeloid and plasmacytoid DCs, and elevated MDSCs. Psychological stress in breast cancer patients correlates with elevated MDSC levels and elevated postsurgical stress correlates with cancer recurrence.

7. **Both autonomic divisions modulate immune checkpoint molecule expression.** PD-1 on T cells and PD-L1 on tumor/stromal cells are regulated by nerve-derived signals: sympathetic-parasympathetic nerve density correlates with PD-1/PD-L1 expression in breast cancer tumors, and pharmacological or genetic manipulation of adrenergic/cholinergic signaling modulates checkpoint expression. This positions neuro-immune crosstalk as a mechanistic route to ICB resistance or sensitization.

## Relevance

**Direct support for paper-internal `H2` (neural gene enrichment via immune modulation).** This paper provides the mechanistic backbone for the paper-internal `H2`: neural genes are enriched in pan-cancer mutation data not because they drive tumor biology through neural circuitry per se, but because they encode receptors and signaling proteins that immune cells in the TME use to receive and process neuro-immune signals. Specifically:

- Genes encoding adrenergic receptors (ADRB1, ADRB2, ADRB3, ADRA2A/ADRA2B), cholinergic receptors (CHRM1, α7nAChR), and neuropeptide signaling components (CALCA/CGRP, TAC1/SubP) are expressed on immune cells infiltrating tumors — not only on neurons. If these immune-expressed neural-signaling genes are frequently mutated in cancer, their frequency could reflect immunoediting pressure rather than a neural-circuit function.

- The paper does not directly address somatic mutation patterns in these genes. Whether the user's top-mutated neural gene list (NKAIN2, KCNIP4, TAFA2/FAM19A2, RIT2, CALN1, RBFOX1, LSAMP, SGCZ, OPCML) overlaps with neuro-immune interface genes is not resolved here. These specific genes are not named in this review. Most of the user's top-mutated list are CNS-enriched structural/developmental genes rather than the peripheral-receptor genes (ADRB2, CHRM1, CALCA) emphasized here, suggesting the overlap between the neuro-immune axis covered by Kizil2024 and the user's observed top-mutated neural genes is partial at best.

**`H1` (hijacking neural circuitry):** Partially supported. The review documents that tumors actively promote neoneurogenesis (nerve infiltration) and perineural invasion, suggesting cancer cells do co-opt neural infrastructure. However, the mechanisms described are largely paracrine (neurotransmitter→immune receptor) rather than cancer cells acquiring neural identity.

**`H2` (neural-gene enrichment via immune modulation):** Strongly supported as a mechanistic framework. The neuro-immune receptors (ADRB2, CHRM1, CALCA, TAC1) are both neuronally derived and immune-cell expressed, creating a conceptual bridge between "neural" gene expression and immune regulation in tumors.

**`H3` (oncofetal/developmental expression):** Not addressed by this paper.

**`H4` (brain/CNS-cancer artifact):** The paper focuses on peripheral nerves (PNS) in solid tumors — not CNS cancers. However, the genes discussed (ADRB2, ADRA2) are expressed broadly, not exclusively in CNS, weakening `H4` for the neuro-immune axis specifically.

**`H5` (misannotation):** Not addressed.

**Data-driven neural-gene definition:** Kizil2024 implicitly defines a functional category of "neuro-immune interface genes" — genes whose protein products mediate communication between neurons and immune cells. These include: ADRB1, ADRB2, ADRB3, ADRA2A, ADRA2B, CHRM1, CALCA (CGRP), TAC1 (substance P), TRPV1, BDNF (mentioned in SNS-driven intratumoral innervation via BDNF induction). This is a mechanism-defined set, not an expression-cluster-defined set. A label-free data-driven definition (e.g., from single-cell RNA-seq co-expression of immune and neural gene programs) would require different resources than this review provides.

## Limitations

- This is a narrative viewpoint, not a systematic review or meta-analysis. Study selection is not described, and effect sizes are not synthesized quantitatively. Evidence quality varies considerably across cited studies.
- Most mechanistic data derive from mouse tumor models (syngeneic transplant: B16F10, 4T1, MC38, 6606PDA); generalizability to human tumor biology is assumed but not directly demonstrated for many claims.
- The direction of effect of sympathetic and parasympathetic innervation is highly context-dependent (cancer type, tumor stage, cell type) and often contradictory across studies. The review acknowledges this but does not provide a unifying quantitative framework.
- The "neuro-immune interface genes" implicated (ADRB2, CHRM1, CALCA, TAC1) are not systematically linked to somatic mutation data; whether these genes are preferentially mutated in cancer, and whether such mutations alter immune cell function, is not addressed.
- The user's top-mutated neural genes (NKAIN2, KCNIP4, TAFA2/FAM19A2, RIT2, CALN1, RBFOX1, LSAMP, SGCZ, OPCML) are not mentioned in this paper. These appear to be predominantly CNS-structural/developmental genes, not the peripheral autonomic/sensory receptor genes this review focuses on. The mechanistic link between this review and the user's specific gene list therefore remains [SPECULATION] without further analysis.
- Gene-length confounding is not discussed; large genes in the user's top-mutated list could appear simply due to mutation target size, not immune-mediated selection.
- Clinical evidence for beta-blockers is largely observational/retrospective; the one cited RCT (Hiller 2020, phase II) used biomarker endpoints, not survival.
