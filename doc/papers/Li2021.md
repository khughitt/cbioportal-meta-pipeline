# A body map of somatic mutagenesis in morphologically normal human tissues

## Key Contribution

Li et al. (Peking University / National Cancer Center Beijing) construct the first comprehensive *body map* of somatic mutation accumulation and clonal architecture across nine morphologically normal organs from five healthy donors. Using laser-capture microdissection combined with mini-bulk exome and low-depth whole-genome sequencing on 1,737 tissue samples, they demonstrate that somatic mutation burden, clonal expansion, and driver gene enrichment are highly tissue-specific: the esophagus and cardia undergo macroscopic clonal expansion driven by canonical cancer genes (NOTCH1, TP53), while the intestinal tract accumulates high per-cell mutation burden with only microscopic, compartment-constrained clones. Critically for cross-study cancer genomics, 32 potential driver genes — including NOTCH1, TP53, ARID1A, and ERBB2 — are found mutated at appreciable frequency in morphologically normal tissue, establishing an empirical per-organ background rate of driver-like mutations that precede any malignant transformation.

## Methods

**Sample design:** 1,737 morphologically normal tissue biopsies from 9 anatomic sites across 5 donors. Sites: bronchus, esophagus, cardia, stomach, duodenum, colon, rectum, liver, and pancreas. [UNVERIFIED: donor age range, sex distribution, and exact biopsy counts per tissue not confirmed from accessible sources.]

**Sequencing strategy:** Two complementary approaches were applied to the same samples (confirmed from EGA dataset description EGAD00001007859):
- Low-depth whole-genome sequencing (1,764 samples) — used for copy number and clonal architecture at spatial scale.
- Mini-bulk whole-exome sequencing (1,772 samples) — applied to ~600-cell microbiopsy units from laser-capture microdissected tissue sections; enables variant allele frequency (VAF) profiling to infer clone sizes.
- High-depth WGS on 48 samples (likely confirmatory/validation tier).

**Spatial clonal reconstruction:** Sub-millimetre spatial resolution of clone boundaries achieved by combining adjacent microbiopsy VAF profiles. This approach is analogous to the Martincorena 2018 esophageal LCM-WGS design but applied simultaneously across multiple organs from the same individuals.

**Driver gene identification:** Likely used mutation enrichment / dN/dS analysis on the exome data; 32 potential driver genes identified as recurrently mutated above neutral-rate expectation in normal tissue [UNVERIFIED: specific method, p-value cutoffs, and gene set overlap with published cancer driver lists not confirmed from abstract].

**Mutational signature decomposition:** COSMIC SBS signature framework applied to the WGS data; signatures compared across tissues and donors.

### Clonal expansion patterns — a critical dichotomy

Two fundamentally distinct architectures observed:

**Macroscopic expansion (esophagus and cardia):** Somatic clones expand to hundreds of micrometres — visible at the tissue level. Copy number alterations (somatic CNAs) detected at appreciable frequency in esophageal and cardiac tissues only; rarely found in other organs. NOTCH1 and TP53 are enriched driver genes in esophageal/cardiac clones. Mutations in chromosomes 3, 5, and 7 CNAs co-occur with NOTCH1/TP53 in esophageal driver clones [UNVERIFIED: the specific CNA pattern is derived from web search summaries].

**Microscopic, compartment-confined expansion (colon, rectum, duodenum):** High per-cell mutation burden but clones remain microscopically small and evolve independently. The intestinal crypt structure physically constrains clonal spread, consistent with the Yoshida 2026 review's mechanistic framing. MUC6 is identified as a putative driver enriched in cardia and stomach normal tissue [UNVERIFIED: specific gene assignment derives from web search summaries].

### 32 normal-tissue driver genes

32 potential driver genes identified in morphologically normal tissue, including canonical cancer drivers:
- **NOTCH1** — most frequently mutated; highest enrichment in esophagus and bronchial tissue.
- **TP53** — greatest number of hotspot mutations across tissues; enriched in esophageal tissue.
- **ARID1A** — present in normal tissue; known gastric/endometrial cancer driver.
- **ERBB2** — present in normal tissue; breast/gastric amplification target.

Driver gene distribution is organ-biased: genes enriched in normal esophagus/cardia overlap substantially with ESCC driver genes, while the pattern differs for colon, liver, and pancreas. [UNVERIFIED: per-tissue driver gene frequency tables and statistical enrichment scores not accessible.]

### Ubiquitous SBS1/SBS5 endogenous signatures

SBS1 (age-related, deamination of methylated CpGs) and SBS5 (age-related, unknown mechanism) are present across all normal samples and all organs, but with differing relative contributions between tissues. This is consistent with data from other normal-tissue studies (Yoshida 2026 review; Alexandrov 2020).

Exogenous mutational processes were also detected, operating across multiple tissues from the same donor — implying that systemic carcinogen exposure (e.g., tobacco smoke) leaves signatures not only in target tissues (lung) but also in other epithelial sites accessible to the same bloodborne carcinogens. [UNVERIFIED: specific exogenous signature identities and tissue distributions not confirmed from abstract; description derives from abstract language "exogenous mutational processes operate in multiple tissues from the same donor".]

### First intra-individual cross-organ mutation map

The study's most distinctive methodological contribution is that all nine organs are sampled from the same five donors, enabling direct inter-organ comparison without confounding by individual-level genetic background, age, or carcinogen exposure history. This within-individual design is explicitly described as "the first comprehensive body map of somatic mutations and clonal expansions from the same individual."

## Relevance

This paper is directly relevant to the cbioportal project across three dimensions:

**1. Empirical background rate for normal-tissue driver contamination**

The 32 driver genes found in morphologically normal tissue define the expected false-positive inflation risk in cancer studies where tumor purity is imperfect or normal-tissue sampling is unavoidable (e.g., endoscopic biopsies). NOTCH1 and TP53 enrichment in normal esophageal tissue — the same genes prominent in esophageal cancer — creates a specific contamination risk for esophageal ESCC studies in cBioPortal where biopsy-based sampling is common. This directly extends the logic of `question:q001`.

**2. Organ-specific clonal architecture as a contamination-risk stratifier**

The esophagus/cardia vs. intestinal dichotomy has pipeline implications: esophageal studies carry a higher normal-clone contamination risk than colonic studies, because normal esophageal clones are macroscopic (large VAF) and driver-enriched, whereas normal colonic clones are microscopic (low VAF) and less likely to pass tumor-calling VAF filters. This provides a tissue-specific risk ranking absent from the Yoshida 2026 review.

**3. SBS1/SBS5 as the universal aging baseline**

The demonstration that SBS1/SBS5 dominate all normal tissue provides the empirical anchor for interpreting the mutation-burden floor across cancer types in our pipeline. High per-sample mutation counts in older cohorts are expected even after excluding hypermutators, because the SBS1/SBS5 aging clock runs in all dividing tissues. This contextualizes the hypermutator annotation work (t081/t092–t099) — the "background" is not zero, and its floor rises with age and tissue type.

**4. Within-individual study design as an unconfounded reference**

Unlike cross-cohort normal-tissue studies, the within-individual design isolates tissue-type effects from individual-level exposures. This is relevant to pipeline interpretation: cross-cancer differences in mutation frequency in our gene×cancer matrix partly reflect true tissue-of-origin biology (as shown here) rather than only study-design or cohort effects.

## Limitations

- Very small cohort: 5 donors only. Body-map patterns may not generalize across ethnic groups, age ranges, or exposure histories. The per-tissue driver frequency estimates carry substantial uncertainty at N=5.
- Only 9 organs profiled; major organs (kidney, lung parenchyma, bladder, prostate, ovary, uterus) are absent, limiting the scope of the "body map."
- Mini-bulk WES on ~600 cells can miss rare clones smaller than the biopsy unit; microscopic clones are likely underdetected relative to a single-cell approach.
- Exome sequencing captures coding variants; intronic and non-coding regulatory mutations (potentially driver-relevant in some tissue contexts) are not captured in the exome tier.
- Donors are healthy individuals without documented carcinogen exposures; the cardia/esophageal findings may differ in populations with high-exposure profiles (e.g., tobacco, alcohol, hot beverage consumption) where ESCC rates are elevated.
- Full text is paywalled and bioRxiv preprint blocks automated access; several quantitative results in this summary (per-tissue mutation rates, specific CNA chromosome assignments, exact driver gene frequencies) are derived from secondary sources and web search summaries and should be verified against the published PDF.
