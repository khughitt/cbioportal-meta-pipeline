# Cancer-nervous system crosstalk: from biological mechanism to therapeutic opportunities

- **Authors:** Sirui Huang, Jing Zhu, Linglu Yu, Yan Huang, Yue Hu (Sirui Huang and Jing Zhu contributed equally)
- **Year:** 2025
- **Journal:** Molecular Cancer
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1186/s12943-025-02336-4
- **BibTeX key:** Huang2025a
- **Source:** PDF

## Key Contribution

A comprehensive 29-page narrative review synthesizing the bidirectional crosstalk between the nervous system and cancer tumors, organized around three interaction tiers: (1) direct neural effects on tumor cells via neurotransmitters, neurotrophic factors, and neurogenic chemokines; (2) tumor effects on neural function (axonogenesis, neuropathic pain, neuroplasticity); and (3) neuro-immune-cancer triangulation in which the nervous system modulates immune cells in the TME, indirectly shaping tumor fate. The review explicitly extends the Hanahan "hallmarks of cancer" framework by arguing that neural regulation plays a role in at least five of the eight hallmarks and two enabling characteristics. It catalogs the principal signaling pathways, receptor families, and therapeutic candidates (pharmacological and non-pharmacological) in a series of summary tables (Tables 1–4), making it a useful reference map for the nascent cancer neurobiology field.

This note belongs to the same cancer-neuroscience review family as paper:Mancusi2023, paper:Keough2022, paper:Magnon2023, paper:Hanahan2023, paper:Venkatesh2019, paper:Hwang2025a, paper:Huang2023a, paper:Mravec2008, and paper:Cortese2020.

## Methods

Narrative review; no new experimental data or computational analysis. The review synthesizes findings from:

- Primary literature on animal models of neural ablation, denervation, optogenetic stimulation, and pharmacological receptor blockade across gastric, colorectal, pancreatic, prostate, breast, lung, and renal cancers.
- In vitro mechanistic studies of neurotransmitter receptor signaling in cancer cell lines.
- Clinical/translational studies and retrospective analyses of neural-targeted agents (beta-blockers, Trk inhibitors, botulinum toxin).
- Table 1: neuronal activity mechanisms across cancer types by organ system (digestive, respiratory, urinary, endocrine systems); Table 2: cancer-nervous system signaling pathway compendium keyed on neurosecretory factor → receptor → downstream pathway → tumor outcome; Table 3: immune cell types regulated by the nervous system within the TME; Table 4: drug pipeline targeting cancer-nervous system interactions.

## Key Findings

### Neurotransmitter and neurotrophic factor signaling on tumor cells

- **Glutamate/NMDAR/AMPAR axis:** Glutamate released by neurons activates NMDAR and AMPAR on tumor cells. NMDAR triggers CaMKII/MAPK/CREB → METTL3 → HK2 (m6A-modified mRNA, glycolysis enhancement, PNI promotion in PDAC). AMPAR activates MAPK/K-Ras (pancreatic invasion and migration).
- **BDNF → TrkB:** Promotes ERK/MAPK, PI3K/AKT/mTOR, PLC-γ/DAG, and JAK/STAT pathways on tumor cells — proliferation, anti-apoptosis, angiogenesis, invasion. p75NTR (low-affinity receptor for BDNF) instead activates NF-κB/MMP9 and AKT/ERK, promoting metastasis.
- **NGF → TrkA:** Activates MAPK/ERK, HIF-1α, EphA2/SRC. Aberrantly expressed in many tumor types and serves as a marker of tumor progression. Stimulates perineural infiltration and axonogenesis.
- **Norepinephrine → ADRB2:** Upregulates hTERT expression via ADRB2/p21-activated kinases (PAK)/Src/HIF-1α/c-MYC cascade, promoting EMT and ovarian cancer invasion. Also activates VEGF-mediated angiogenesis, programs macrophages (TAMs) toward M2 immunosuppressive phenotype.
- **ADRB3 → Epac/JNK/BDNF/mTOR:** ADRB3 in tumor cells induces BDNF secretion into TME and stimulates ribosome biogenesis, cell cycle entry (G0–G1 progression), relevant in lung cancer.
- **Acetylcholine → mAChRs (CHRM1/CHRM3/CHRM4/CHRNA5):** Tumor-derived acetylcholine drives autocrine/paracrine EGFR transactivation via M3R (gastric cancer); CHRM1 activation suppresses EGFR/MAPK/PI3K/AKT (anti-tumor in PDAC); CHRM4 promotes neuroendocrine differentiation (prostate cancer); CHRNA5 triggers CaMKII/GSK-3β/β-catenin (ICC, drug resistance).
- **GABA → GABA_B receptors:** Acetylcholine (PD-L1 upregulation, immune escape) counterbalanced by GABA (GSK-3β suppression, β-catenin enhancement → tumor proliferation and CD8+ T cell exclusion).

### Tumor effects on neural function

- **Axonogenesis:** Tumor-secreted NGF and BDNF promote de novo nerve fiber sprouting and innervation of the tumor (axonogenesis). Semaphorin 4F (S4F) expressed on prostate tumor cells induces neurite sprouting in co-cultured neurons. Ephrin-B1 from tumor-derived exosomes stimulates PC12 neurite outgrowth (Eph/Ephrin signaling). Netrin-1 (highly expressed in aggressive cancers) binds DCC receptors on neurons, activating FAK/Rac1/Cdc42 → CGRP+ sensory nerve fiber elongation, nociceptive innervation.
- **Cancer-induced neuropathic pain:** Tumor-released NGF activates TrkA → RAS/MAPK/ERK in sensory neurons. CGRP and neuropeptide Y (NPY) hyperactivate peripheral nerve endings → neuropathic pain. TRPV1 on sensory neurons activated by NGF, TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6 from tumor cells → substance P and CGRP release. miR-124 (downregulated in spinal cord by tumors) silences Synpo → enhanced synaptic signaling; miR-1a-3p silences Clcn3 chloride channel gene in DRG neurons (enhanced pain sensitivity).
- **Tumor-Activated Schwann Cell Trajectories (TAST):** Schwann cells in the pancreatic TME undergo c-Jun-dependent reprogramming to a non-myelinating phenotype, enwrap tumor cells in organized columns, and provide migratory tracks. Reprogrammed Schwann cells secrete CCL2 (recruits inflammatory monocytes → macrophages expressing cathepsin B → PNI), IL-6 (activates STAT3 → pancreatic cancer migration/invasion), and CXCL5 (activates PI3K/AKT/GSK-3β via CXCR2 → EMT: Snail/Twist upregulation → motility/metastasis).
- **Tumor microtubes (TMs) and tunneling nanotubes (TNTs):** Cancer cells form membrane-bound tubular structures for intercellular communication, therapy resistance, and metastasis. In gliomas, TM formation involves GAP-43 and Ttyh1 (neuron-associated proteins), both linked to microtubule dynamics and radioresistance.

### Neuro-immune-cancer interactions

- **TAMs:** NE → ADRB2 on TAMs induces IL-10, ARG1, VEGF (immunosuppressive/pro-angiogenic); suppresses TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-12. TAMs secrete bFGF → activates PI3K-Akt-cMyc in Schwann cells → GFAP upregulation → Schwann cell migration → PNI → IL-33 → more TAMs (positive feedback loop in PDAC).
- **Regulatory T Cells (Tregs):** NE → ADRB2 → cAMP/PKA → enhanced Treg migration and immunosuppressive function; TGF-β/Smad2/3 promotes Treg differentiation; TGF-β promotes CD4+ T cell proliferation via Foxp3; TGF-β drives Smad3 → IL-10, IL-35, cyclin D/E upregulation → G1 arrest inhibiting T cell proliferation.
- **Neutrophils (neuro-neutrophil-cancer):** Adrenergic-driven SP1 in tumor-derived exosomes (TDES) → TLR4 on neutrophils → NF-κB → IL-1β → EGFR-dependent PI3K/AKT and MEK/ERK → MMP/ADAM activation → EMT (vimentin up, E-cadherin down) → metastasis.
- **Natural Killer (NK) cells:** CGRP from nociceptor neurons binds RAMP1/CALCRL on cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs) → PKA → transcriptional suppression of IL-15 → NK cell impairment. CAF-secreted NGF → TrkA on nociceptors → more CGRP (self-reinforcing loop) → immunosuppression + nociceptor sensitization.
- **Midkine (MDK):** Neurotrophic factor produced by neurons; stimulates CD8+ T cells but paradoxically upregulates CCL4/CCL5 via NF-κB in microglia → insufficient CD8+ T cell anti-tumor activity and resistance to apoptosis.

### Therapeutic targets and candidates (Table 4)

**Pharmacological:**
- Beta-blockers (propranolol, carvedilol, atenolol): ADRB blockade; breast cancer; preclinical and retrospective evidence.
- M3 muscarinic antagonists (tiotropium for lung cancer, atropine for colorectal cancer).
- Trk inhibitors: larotrectinib, entrectinib (NTRK fusion-positive solid tumors — approved); selitrectinib (second-gen for Trk domain mutations); repotrectinib (brain metastasis); cabozantinib (TrkB/MET/VEGFR2, Phase II in osteosarcoma).
- mGluR1 inhibitor riluzole (glutamate release inhibitor; MCF-7 breast cancer xenografts).
- NTSR1 antagonist SR48692 (pancreatic cancer; preclinical).
- Low-dose naltrexone (TLR4 activation; pancreatic and breast cancer; preclinical).
- MDK inhibitor iMDK (ovarian cancer, restores CD8+ T cell activity).
- TGF-β inhibitor (basal-like breast cancer; restores CD4+ T cell function).
- Baclofen (GABA_B agonist; stabilizes PD-L1 in breast cancer; preclinical).
- Botulinum toxin (BoNT/A): blocks acetylcholine release; showed tumor reduction in three gastric cancer mouse models; vagotomy equivalent.

**Non-pharmacological:**
- Psychological interventions (CBT, mindfulness): modulate sympathetic-adrenal-medullary (SAM) axis, reduce catecholamine output; meta-analysis evidence (13 trials for CBT; 21 trials for mindfulness).
- Exercise, meditation, yoga: increase vagal tone, elevate BDNF, activate TrkB, support neurogenesis and synaptic plasticity; HRV as a non-invasive biomarker of autonomic regulation relevant to tumor control.
- Acupuncture / electroacupuncture / TEAS: ASCO-endorsed for cancer pain; IMPACT RCT (acupuncture + massage for advanced cancer pain/fatigue); mechanistic pathway via endogenous opioids, serotonin (5-HT), and descending pain inhibitory circuits.

## Relevance

**Directly relevant to `H1` (neural hijack / top-down circuitry), `H2` (immune modulation), and `H3` (aberrant developmental / oncofetal expression).**

**`H1` — Neural hijack / top-down circuitry (strong support):** The review provides the mechanistic backbone for `H1`. Tumors actively recruit nerve fibers via NGF, BDNF, semaphorin S4F, Ephrin-B1, and Netrin-1. Established receptors on tumor cells (NMDAR, AMPAR, TrkA, TrkB, ADRB2, ADRB3, CHRM1, CHRM3, CHRM4, CHRNA5) mediate direct growth, invasion, and metastasis signals from neurons. The TAST Schwann cell trajectory mechanism shows that even non-neuronal PNS cells structurally reorganize the tumor. This strongly supports the hypothesis that elevated somatic mutation rates in genes encoding neural receptors and adhesion molecules (NKAIN2, KCNIP4, RBFOX1, OPCML, LSAMP) could reflect positive selection in cell populations that have co-opted neural signaling programs.

**`H2` — Immune modulation (strong support):** The neuro-immune-cancer section provides an explicit mechanistic map of how the nervous system reshapes the TME immune landscape — TAM M2 polarization, Treg expansion, neutrophil EMT promotion, NK cell suppression, CD8+ T cell exclusion. This offers a mechanistic path by which "neural" genes could influence tumor immune evasion without requiring neural cells to be physically present.

**`H3` — Aberrant developmental / oncofetal expression (moderate support):** Tumor cells that upregulate axon guidance receptors (DCC, Eph family), neurotrophic factor receptors (TrkA, TrkB, p75NTR), or neurotransmitter receptors (NMDAR, AMPAR, mAChRs) may be expressing a developmental/oncofetal gene expression program. The review notes that cancer stem cells from gastric and colorectal cancers can differentiate into neuron-like cells in vitro (sympathetic and parasympathetic subtypes), and that small cell lung cancer cells undergo morphological changes reminiscent of neuronal cells (axonogenesis-related genes upregulated). This is direct evidence for `H3` being mechanistically plausible.

**`H4` — Brain/CNS-cancer artifact (partial mitigation):** The review covers non-CNS cancers (PDAC, gastric, colorectal, breast, lung, prostate) extensively. This shows that neural gene involvement is not restricted to brain tumors — arguing against `H4` as the sole explanation for the "neural top genes" observation.

**`H5` — Misannotation (not directly addressed):** The review does not discuss gene annotation quality or pipeline-level confounds.

**On a data-driven neural-gene definition:** The review does not provide a principled transcriptomics- or genomics-based definition of "neural genes." It catalogs genes by their established receptor/ligand/signaling function. The most useful contribution to the project's definitional challenge is the gene lists in Tables 1–2 (neurosecretory factors and their receptors on tumor cells), which constitute a curated molecular-functional definition of the cancer neurobiology gene space. Using expression data from TCGA/GTEx to ask which of the "top mutated" genes are co-expressed with neural marker genes (STMN2, MAP2, TUBB3, SYP, ENO2) in tumor RNA-seq would provide a data-driven label-free approach.

## Limitations

- Narrative review with no quantitative synthesis (no meta-analysis, no forest plots, no effect-size estimates). The directional claims (e.g., "ADRB2 promotes tumor progression") are supported by individual studies that may have publication bias.
- Most mechanistic evidence comes from mouse models (murine xenografts, transgenic models); human translational validation is sparse for most pathways.
- The gene-level resolution is receptor/ligand functional (e.g., "ADRB2 is expressed on tumor cells"), not mutation-frequency based — the review does not address somatic mutation rates in these genes.
- No CNS vs. PNS distinction made systematic; the cellular origin of "infiltrating nerve fibers" is not always specified (sympathetic, parasympathetic, sensory, or enteric), making it difficult to map pathway mechanisms to specific cancer types.
- The therapeutic table (Table 4) mixes well-validated approved drugs (larotrectinib, entrectinib for NTRK fusions) with speculative preclinical candidates at the same level of presentation, without explicit evidence-tier labeling.
- Non-pharmacological interventions (acupuncture, CBT, exercise) are reviewed favorably but the mechanistic evidence linking these to specific molecular pathways in the review's own biological framework is thin — effect sizes and reproducibility across cancer types are not assessed critically.
- Data availability statement: "No datasets were generated or analysed during the current study" — this is a pure literature review; no code, no data.
- Affiliations are from Nanjing University of Chinese Medicine and Nanjing Hospital of Chinese Medicine — the review reflects a perspective that includes traditional Chinese medicine framing for some non-pharmacological interventions (acupuncture).

## Model / Tool Availability

None. This is a narrative review with no new software, datasets, or computational tools released. All cited mechanistic studies are from the primary literature; the review does not perform reanalysis of existing datasets.

## Follow-up

- **For `H1`/`H3` testing:** Use gene lists from Table 2 (NTRK1, NTRK2, ADRB2, ADRB3, CHRM1, CHRM3, CHRM4, GRIN1, GRIN2A, GRIN2B, GABRB1/2) as a functionally curated "neural receptor" gene set. Cross-reference against cbioportal top-mutated genes list — if NKAIN2, KCNIP4, RBFOX1, LSAMP appear in this set, `H3` is directly implicated.
- **For `H2` testing:** Check somatic mutation frequency of ADRB2, MDK, CXCL5, CCL2, STAT3, RAMP1, CALCRL across cancer types in the pipeline — these are the neuro-immune bridge molecules; elevated mutation rates in immunologically cold tumor types would support `H2` via loss-of-function escape from neural immunosuppression.
- **Gene-length confound audit:** GRIN2A (≈ 1 Mb genomic span), GRIN2B, RBFOX1, NKAIN2, KCNIP4 are all very large genes. The review does not address this; the pipeline's gene-length normalization (UniProt protein lengths) should be applied before concluding these genes are truly elevated beyond expectation.
- **Netrin-1 / DCC axis:** DCC (Deleted in Colorectal Cancer) is the Netrin-1 receptor reviewed here as a neural axon guidance molecule. DCC is also a classic tumor suppressor. Check whether DCC appears in the cbioportal top-mutated list and whether its mutation type profile is consistent with tumor suppressor inactivation (LOF) vs. neural axis co-option (GOF / overexpression).
- **Schwann cell TAST mechanism:** Read Deborde et al. (2023) Cancer Discov 12:2454–73 (ref 37 in this paper) for the primary TAST discovery paper in PDAC — directly relevant to the PNI biology underlying any pancreatic cancer neural gene signal in the pipeline.
- **MDK as a candidate neural-immune bridge gene:** Midkine (MDK) is a neurotrophic factor not in the NKAIN2/KCNIP4/RBFOX1 cluster but mechanistically central here. Check MDK mutation and expression in the cbioportal data.
- **Trk inhibitor resistance:** The review notes that resistance to larotrectinib/entrectinib is a clinical challenge; next-generation Trk inhibitors (selitrectinib, repotrectinib) are in development. If the pipeline includes NTRK1/2/3 fusion-positive studies, these genes should be flagged separately from point-mutated cases.
- **Cross-batch companion reads:** Mancusi2023 (perineural invasion mechanisms), Magnon2023 (neural addiction of cancer), Venkatesh2019 (neural activity promotes cancer, the Science paper on glioma), Keough2022, and Hanahan2023 all provide mechanistic depth on specific aspects surveyed here. Reading Huang2025 alongside those papers gives the most complete picture of the `H1`/`H2`/`H3` landscape.
