## Key Contribution

A comprehensive narrative review of the bidirectional communication between the nervous and immune systems within the tumor microenvironment (TME). The review systematizes how neural components (sensory, sympathetic, parasympathetic nerves; glial cells; circadian clock; gut-brain axis; neuroendocrine cells) modulate antitumor immunity — and conversely how tumor-driven immune changes damage the nervous system. It provides the most complete current compendium of neuro-immune-cancer circuit nodes by cancer type (Table 4), covering glioma, HNSCC, melanoma, pancreatic, breast, colorectal, NSCLC, SCLC, prostate, and others, and surveys completed and ongoing clinical trials targeting this axis (Tables 2–3). The paper does not present primary experimental data; it synthesizes ~520 cited studies published through approximately 2024.

## Methods

Narrative review; no systematic search protocol, PRISMA flow, or meta-analytic synthesis is described. Evidence is selectively integrated from mouse models, cell lines, patient cohort studies, scRNA-seq datasets, and clinical trials. Key conceptual organizing axes: (1) neuroregulation of immune organs (lymph nodes, spleen, bone marrow, thymus); (2) peripheral nerve subtypes — sensory, sympathetic, parasympathetic — and their distinct immunomodulatory effects per cancer; (3) neurotransmitters/neuropeptides (NE, ACh, CGRP, SP, GABA, 5-HT, DA, HA, NAA) as signaling molecules in TME; (4) glial and neuroendocrine cells; (5) circadian rhythm disruption; (6) stress/HPA axis; (7) gut-brain axis and dysbiosis; (8) paraneoplastic neurological syndromes (PNS) as evidence for reverse (tumor→nerve) signaling.

## Key Findings

**Sensory nerve / CGRP axis**

- Nociceptor-derived CGRP promotes germinal center responses and plasma cell expansion in the spleen via CALCRL-RAMP1 on B cells, boosting humoral immunity and antibody secretion.
- In melanoma, CGRP binding to RAMP1 on CD8+ T cells directly increases exhaustion; pharmacologic blockade of RAMP1 or TRPV1 reduced T cell exhaustion, decreased cancer size, and extended overall survival in mouse models.
- CGRP acts on DCs to disrupt Kruppel-Like Factor 2 expression and impair antigen presentation; it also reduces cytokine output in macrophages and DCs, suppressing CTL function.
- Substance P (SP) promotes tumor metastasis by activating Toll-like receptor 7 on breast cancer cells via extracellular RNA; SP/NK-1R signaling boosts bone marrow stem cell survival and promotes macrophage pro-inflammatory secretion.

**Sympathetic nerve / NE / beta-adrenergic axis**

- NE (norepinephrine) via beta-AR activation: promotes secretion of IL-8, IL-6 from cancer and myeloid cells; drives VEGF/MMP/prostaglandin-E2 production from macrophages; facilitates MDSCs recruitment and immunosuppression; induces Treg differentiation from primary T cells; inhibits type I and II interferon production.
- Beta-2-AR activation on MDSCs upregulates Arg1 and Cd274 (PD-L1), promotes immunosuppression via CXCL5-CXCR2/MMPs.
- Conversely, beta-AR blockade (propranolol) reduces MDSCs infiltration, increases tumor-specific CD8+ T cells in tumor-draining lymph nodes, and synergizes with anti-CTLA-4 in fibrosarcoma and CRC models.
- NR4A1 is a key mediator of T cell dysfunction induced by adrenergic signaling; NR4A1 knockdown improved antitumor immunity in mice.
- Clinical evidence: Phase I trial (NCT03384836) of propranolol + pembrolizumab in advanced melanoma showed 78% response rate with no dose-limiting toxicity; preoperative propranolol phase II trial (ACTRN12615000889550) reduced intratumoral mesenchymal polarization and elevated CD68+ macrophages and CD8+ T cells in early-stage breast cancer.

**Parasympathetic nerve / ACh axis**

- ACh via muscarinic mAChR on macrophages: in oral cancer model CGRP-KO mice showed increased CD4+ and CD8+ T cells and NK cells vs WT, indicating net immunosuppressive role of CGRP on innate immunity.
- Vagal nerve and TFF2-expressing Tms (tissue memory T cells) in the spleen inhibit inflammation and MDSC expansion, reduce carcinogenic potential in mice with inflammatory colon cancer.
- AChE inhibitors (physostigmine, neostigmine) reduced activity and invasiveness of pancreatic cancer cells by inactivating pERK signaling in TAMs; donepezil is in clinical trials for brain tumors, breast cancer, SCLC, and head-and-neck cancers.

**Neurotransmitters and neurometabolites**

- GABA: synthesized by B lymphocytes; activates GABAA receptors on CD8+ T cells suppressing antitumor immunity; in NSCLC and CRC elevated glutamate decarboxylase 1 reprograms glutamine for GABA synthesis, activating GABAB-β-catenin-GSK3β signaling to promote tumor proliferation and suppress CD8+ T cell infiltration.
- 5-HT (serotonin): upregulates PD-L1 via histone serotonylation and epigenetic mechanisms in pancreatic/gastric cancer; fluoxetine (SSRI) inhibited melanoma growth by promoting T cell proliferation through mitogen signals.
- Dopamine (DA): DRD3 agonists reduce HCC proliferation; DRD5 promotes CD8+ T cell differentiation into CD103+ tissue-resident Tms; DRD1 reduces nitric oxide in MDSCs to reverse local immunosuppression in NSCLC/melanoma; DA transporter antagonist vanoxerine inhibits CSC function through G9a histone methyltransferase.
- Histamine (HA): elevated in cancer via L-histidine decarboxylase (HDC); H1/H2 receptor signaling on immune cells drives M2 macrophage polarization; cimetidine (H2 antagonist) inhibits lung tumor growth in mice; absence of H4 receptor correlated with improved breast cancer survival.
- N-acetylaspartate (NAA): a CNS-enriched metabolite found elevated in breast cancer cells; disrupts P300/CBP-associated factor (PCAF)-induced acetylation of laminA-K542, thereby inhibiting NK cell and CD8+ T cell cytotoxicity.
- Midkine: produced by NF1-mutated neurons via CCL4 → microglia → CCL5 circuit; a key mitogen for glioma stem cells.

**Glial cells / Schwann cells**

- Schwann cells augment chemotactic immune migration via IL-6, TGF-β, VEGF secretion; CCL2 secreted by Schwann cells promotes tumor proliferation (HeLa, ME180).
- CCL2 in TME associates with M2 polarization of TAMs, suppressing antitumor immune responses in melanoma; blocking CCL2 in glioma reverses this.
- Schwann cells also promote MDSCs recruitment and tumor growth in melanoma, pancreatic, and prostate cancers via DAMPs/TLR recognition.

**Neuroendocrine cells**

- Unregulated proliferation leads to SCLC and GEP-NETs; SCLC has a particularly immunosuppressive landscape: lymphocyte-rich but myeloid-poor compared to adjacent normal tissue; T cells from TME show four heterogeneous activation states and express exhaustion markers (PD-1, CTLA-4, TIM3, LAG3, TIGIT, LAYN).
- In prostate cancer, neuroendocrine differentiation predicts poor prognosis; GDF15 is expressed by tumor cells, TAMs, and fibroblasts and elevated in serum; GFRAL receptor binding leads to cachexia.

**Circadian rhythm**

- BMAL1 in macrophages is pro-antitumor: BMAL1 inhibits HIF-1α and ROS production, limiting M2 polarization and CD8+ T cell suppression; BMAL1 knockout in macrophages increased tumor growth and reduced CD8+ T cell infiltration.
- Clock gene mutations accumulate in cancer genomes and correlate with T cell exhaustion and upregulation of PD-L1/CTLA-4.
- RORγ promotes Th17 differentiation and reduces Tregs; RORγ agonists increased apoptosis of lymphoma cells in vivo; a clinical trial (NCT03396497) of RORγ agonists + anti-PD-1 for metastatic NSCLC is underway.
- CRY1 overexpression in endothelial cells inhibits pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α) and adhesion molecules (VCAM-1, ICAM-1, E-selectin), impairing monocyte adhesion.

**Stress / HPA axis**

- Chronic stress → sympathetic activation → elevated NE/EPI/GCs → NR4A1-mediated T cell dysfunction, beta-AR-driven MDSC immunosuppression, NK cell impairment, Treg induction.
- GCs repress BMAL1/Per1/Per2 (circadian clock gene expression), create an "aged" neutrophil phenotype, promote NET formation and tumor metastasis (via S100A8/A9, myeloperoxidase).
- Catecholamines are context-dependent: DRD2 activation on MDSCs reduces tumor infiltration in lung cancer; DRD1 targeting reduces MDSC nitric oxide in melanoma/NSCLC.
- TSC22D3 (stress-induced GC transcription factor in DCs) impairs antigen presentation and is a marker of poor ICI response in NSCLC and CRC.

**Gut-brain axis**

- SCFAs (propionate, butyrate) activate FFAR2/FFAR3 on breast cancer cells to suppress invasive phenotype; propionate reduces CRC cell proliferation/apoptosis.
- Inosine produced by Akkermansia muciniphila and Bifidobacterium pseudolongum enhances ICI efficacy via A2A receptor → cAMP-PKA → IL12Rβ2/IFN-γ signaling in Th1 cells; inosine also serves as an alternative carbon source for CD8+ T cells when glucose is scarce.

**Paraneoplastic neurological syndromes**

- Table 1 catalogs antibodies/antigens by cancer type: Anti-Hu (ANNA-1)/HuD in SCLC/thymoma/neuroblastoma; Anti-Yo (PCA-1)/CDR2 in ovarian/breast cancers; Anti-Ri (ANNA-2)/NOVA in breast/ovarian/SCLC; Anti-NMDA-R/NMDA-R in ovarian teratoma; Anti-GABA-B/GABAB in SCLC; Anti-mGluR1/mGluR1 in Hodgkin lymphoma/prostate; KCTD16 gain on chromosome 5q in anti-GABABR PNS.
- These syndromes establish that tumor-driven immune responses can cross-react with nervous system antigens — direct evidence for H2's reverse arm.

**Perineural invasion (PNI)**

- TAMs release IL-6, TNF, CCL5, CCL18 under hypoxic/high-lactate conditions, attracting neurotrophic factors (NGF, BDNF, GDNF, VEGF) driving ectopic neural sprouting and PNI.
- SP in PNI activates NK-1R in cancer cells and via Src/EGFR/HER2 upregulates MMP2/MMP9/VEGF mRNA, promoting invasion.
- Tumor-associated pain associated with Piezo2-expressing C-low threshold mechanoreceptors and sensitization of TRPV1 and ASIC channels.

## Relevance

**H2 (neural gene expression driven by immune modulation) — primary support.** This review is the most direct support for H2 among all papers in this batch. It documents exhaustively how neural components modulate immune cells in TME, and how immune cells in turn modulate neural function, dysregulate neural signaling, and cause neural damage. Key mechanistic connections:

- Tumor-driven immune responses (TAMs, MDSCs, T cells) release neurotrophic factors (NGF, BDNF, GDNF) and cytokines that alter nerve density, neural sprouting, and gene expression in peripheral neurons. This means that genes expressed predominantly in neural tissue (e.g., NKAIN2, RBFOX1, LSAMP, OPCML) could be upregulated in TME neurons responding to immune signals — making their apparent somatic mutation enrichment a product of altered transcriptional context rather than selective pressure on the gene itself.
- The NF1→Midkine→CCL4→microglia→CCL5 circuit (glioma) shows how a single somatic mutation in a "neural gene" (NF1) propagates immunomodulatory signals through glial and immune intermediaries, not just through canonical tumor suppressor pathways. This is a direct data-driven example of a neural-gene somatic mutation with immune consequence.
- The paper's Table 4 (cancer-type × immune component × neural component × key molecule matrix) is the closest thing to a structured compendium of neural-gene-immune interactions available in the literature as of mid-2025.

**H1 (neural hijack / direct neural circuitry exploitation) — partial support.** The brain-tumor neural circuit work (insular cortex neurons controlling TME via vagus/sympathetic projection; CRH neuron activation in breast cancer anxiety model) supports direct neural-circuit hijacking by tumors. Xiong et al. 2023 (cited as reference 59 in this paper) specifically describes a brain-tumor neural circuit controlling breast cancer — directly on point for H1.

**H4 (brain/CNS artifact) — addressed but not resolved.** The paper notes that CNS tumors (glioma, brain metastases) have a distinct neuro-immune landscape relative to peripheral tumors, with blood-brain barrier breakdown as a key permissive event. However, it explicitly discusses non-CNS cancers (breast, pancreatic, CRC, melanoma, NSCLC) with substantial neural innervation and neuro-immune crosstalk, which argues against H4 as a complete explanation for neural gene enrichment across cancer types.

**H3 (oncofetal/developmental expression) — indirect support.** Glioma sections describe how glioma cells arise from glial lineages (astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, ependymal cells) and exploit developmental programs; the Midkine/CCL5 loop involves genes with strong developmental expression patterns. No explicit oncofetal framing for the specific long neural genes (NKAIN2, RBFOX1, etc.) from the user's list.

**H5 (misannotation) — not addressed.** The review does not discuss gene-length confounding in somatic mutation frequency analyses.

**Data-driven neural-gene definition — absent.** This review does not provide or cite any computational approach for defining "neural genes" from data. All neural gene identifications are based on prior literature annotation (gene ontology, expression databases, known function). The closest relevant resource is Table 4, which lists neural components and key signaling molecules per cancer type — this could anchor a curated neural-gene list for specific cancer contexts, but it is not a genome-wide or label-free definition.

**Specific somatic mutation relevance.** Among the "top mutated genes" of interest:

- NF1: explicitly discussed — NF1 mutations in neurons trigger Midkine secretion, CCL4 release, and microglia activation, establishing NF1 as a neural gene with direct immune consequence in glioma.
- RBFOX1: not discussed.
- NKAIN2, KCNIP4, TAFA2/FAM19A2, RIT2, CALN1, LSAMP, SGCZ, OPCML: none explicitly discussed.

The review implicitly predicts that genes expressed in sensory/sympathetic/glial cells within or adjacent to tumors could accumulate somatic mutations under immune selection pressure, but does not operationalize this prediction.

## Limitations

- Narrative (not systematic) review; selection bias toward positive/mechanistic findings; negative or null neuro-immune results are underrepresented.
- Predominantly mouse-model and cell-line evidence for mechanistic claims; many findings await human tumor validation at scale.
- The review does not address gene-length confounding, copy-number variation, or chromatin accessibility differences between neural and non-neural genes that could generate spurious somatic mutation enrichment signals.
- Most mechanistic circuits are described for specific cancer types (glioma, breast, pancreatic); generalizability to the cancer types dominating the cBioPortal cross-study pipeline is often assumed rather than demonstrated.
- The bidirectionality claim (tumor→nerve and nerve→tumor) is central but the directionality of many associations in patient data is not established by the studies cited; reverse causation is rarely formally ruled out.
- No quantitative synthesis: effect sizes, hazard ratios, or statistical summaries across studies are not provided; the review cannot support meta-analytic claims.
- The compendium of clinical trials (Tables 2–3) highlights that most completed trials are small (28–583 patients), with mixed and often inconclusive outcomes; translation of mechanistic findings to clinical efficacy remains unproven for most neuro-immune targets.
- Neural gene definitions used throughout are based on prior literature annotations (functional/expression-based), not derived from an unsupervised data-driven analysis — a key gap for the project's goal of label-free neural gene identification.

## Model / Tool Availability

No software, dataset, or computational model is released with this review. Figures were created with BioRender. The paper is open access (CC-BY indicated by "OPEN" label). Tables 1–4 are structured resources directly usable for manual curation:

- Table 1: paraneoplastic syndrome antibody/antigen compendium by cancer type.
- Table 2: completed neuro-immune clinical trials with NCT numbers, dosing, and outcomes.
- Table 3: ongoing neuro-immune clinical trials by drug/target/cancer type.
- Table 4: cancer-type × immune × neural component × key molecule matrix — the most directly reusable resource for seeding a neural-gene annotation layer.

## Follow-up

**Directly actionable for this project:**

1. Table 4 of Pu2025 should be extracted and compared against the pipeline's cross-study high-frequency gene list. Any gene appearing both in Table 4 (as a "key molecule in pathogenesis" of neuro-immune crosstalk) and in the top-mutated list (NKAIN2, KCNIP4, RBFOX1, LSAMP, OPCML, etc.) would have mechanistic grounding for H2. This comparison can be done without new data.

2. NF1 is the bridge case: it is a Bailey 2018 driver, appears frequently in cross-study tables, and this review documents that NF1 mutations in neural cells activate the Midkine→CCL4→microglia→CCL5 immune circuit in glioma. Extending this logic to other neural-expressed genes in the top-mutated list is the next mechanistic step.

3. The RAMP1/CGRP exhaustion axis (Hwang2025a, also cited here) is directly relevant to CALCRL (calcitonin receptor-like receptor, the CGRP receptor component) and RAMP1 appearing in mutation/expression datasets. The pipeline should check whether CALCRL or RAMP1 appear in high-frequency tables for head/neck or melanoma studies.

4. The paraneoplastic antigen table (Table 1) names NOVA, CDR2, GABAB, NMDA-R, mGluR1 as tumor-expressed antigens recognized by the immune system — these are bona fide neural genes with documented immune visibility. A targeted search for somatic mutations in these genes in the pipeline outputs would directly test the "immune-visible neural gene" concept.

5. The TSC22D3 / GC / poor ICI response association (referenced in both stress axis and NSCLC sections) provides a potential negative-control covariate for h08: studies with high corticosteroid use or high psychological stress burden should show lower ICI effectiveness independent of tumor mutational burden.
