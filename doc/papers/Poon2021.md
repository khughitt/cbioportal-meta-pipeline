# Synonymous mutations reveal genome-wide levels of positive selection in healthy tissues

- **PMID:** 34737428
- **BibTeX key:** Poon2021

## Key Contribution

The paper demonstrates that synonymous (silent) somatic mutations hitchhiking with positively selected driver clones can serve as a genome-wide readout of total positive selection in healthy tissues — not just in known cancer driver genes. Applied to healthy blood and esophagus, the method reveals that canonical driver gene lists explain only a minority of detected positive selection in blood (~30% of passenger burden attributable to known SNV drivers), whereas two genes (NOTCH1 and TP53) account for more than half of the observed passengers in esophagus. The central implication is that a large fraction of clonal expansions in blood arise from drivers outside the genes currently monitored by standard panels.

## Methods

**Conceptual framework.** The method exploits a population-genetic property: when a driver mutation arises and expands clonally, all synonymous mutations co-occurring in the same cell expand in lockstep as neutral passengers. Because these passengers are not under selection themselves, their variant allele frequency (VAF) distribution follows an analytically predictable shape (a 1/f^2 Luria-Delbrück-like power law for exponentially expanding clones). Any excess of synonymous mutations above a neutral, non-expanding baseline directly quantifies the amount of positive selection that occurred somewhere in the genome, regardless of which (non-synonymous) driver mutation caused the expansion.

**Neutral baseline.** The expected neutral VAF spectrum (synonymous mutations accumulating without clonal expansion) is derived from population-genetic first principles, calibrated for tissue type, sample size, and sequencing depth. The ratio of observed to expected synonymous VAF density at intermediate-to-high VAF bins provides the "missing positive selection" signal.

**Linking passengers to known drivers.** To estimate how much of the excess synonymous burden can be explained by known cancer drivers, the authors model the clonal expansion signatures predicted by catalogued non-synonymous driver mutations (SNVs in established driver genes) and compare that prediction to the observed synonymous passenger counts. The residual fraction represents selection attributable to undiscovered or non-SNV drivers.

Esophageal data likely derives from the Martincorena 2018 / Yokoyama et al. normal esophagus sequencing studies. **Scope.** The analysis is restricted to two tissue types (blood, esophagus) in this paper. The framework is general and, in principle, extensible to any tissue with sufficient sequencing depth and sample count to detect the synonymous VAF excess.

## Key Findings

1. **Genome-wide selection exceeds known driver lists in blood.** Known SNV driver genes explain only ~30% of the total synonymous passenger burden in healthy blood, implying that ~70% of positively selected clonal expansions involve drivers not in standard gene panels. This is consistent with pervasive CH from indels, structural variants, non-coding mutations, or unannotated coding drivers.

2. **Esophagus is dominated by two known drivers.** In contrast, NOTCH1 and TP53 SNVs together account for more than half the synonymous passenger burden in normal esophagus, suggesting that the driver landscape is relatively concentrated and well-captured by existing annotations.

3. **Tissue-type specificity of selection architecture.** The contrast between blood (diffuse, largely undiscovered selection) and esophagus (concentrated in two genes) implies that the fraction of "cryptic" positive selection is strongly tissue-dependent. Methodologies calibrated on one tissue cannot be naively transferred.

4. **Quantitative framework for estimating total driver mutation rate.** The synonymous-passenger approach yields an absolute estimate of the per-cell-division genome-wide driver rate — a quantity not directly accessible by dN/dS or standard driver-gene counting approaches. ## Relevance

**Direct pipeline relevance: false-positive driver signal in cancer cohorts.**
The cbioportal pipeline aggregates mutation frequencies across cBioPortal studies to identify recurrently mutated genes as putative cancer drivers. Genes that are positively selected in *healthy* tissues (particularly blood) will appear elevated in any study that includes blood-derived tumors (leukemias, lymphomas) or suffers from CH contamination in solid tumor panels (unmatched normals). The Poon 2021 findings sharpen the concern already captured in `topic:clonal-hematopoiesis-contamination`:

- The seven Bolton 2020 CH-priority genes (DNMT3A, TET2, TP53, ASXL1, CHEK2, PPM1D, PRPF8) capture only a subset of blood selection signal. Poon 2021 shows ~70% of positive selection in blood lies outside identified driver genes — meaning there may be additional loci inflating our cross-study mutation frequencies that the current `ch_priority_gene` flag does not cover.
- For esophageal studies, NOTCH1 and TP53 are indeed high-frequency in our esophageal cancer cohorts; Poon 2021 establishes a pre-malignant, selection-driven baseline for both, which should be factored into interpretation of high esophageal NOTCH1 frequencies.

**Relationship to dndscv rule.** The existing `code/scripts/run_dndscv.R` rule (Martincorena 2017) estimates dN/dS per study. Poon 2021 is from the same Blundell lab ecosystem but addresses a complementary question: rather than per-gene dN/dS, it quantifies the genome-wide total selection rate via synonymous VAF excess. The two methods are conceptually orthogonal: dN/dS identifies *which* genes are under selection; the Poon approach estimates *how much* total selection exists above what known genes explain.

**Implication for Bailey 2018 driver overlay.** The Bailey 2018 driver list used in `annotate_drivers.py` is derived from cancer cohorts. Poon 2021 suggests that some Bailey drivers may have been positively selected in healthy tissue first (particularly blood lineage genes like DNMT3A, TET2), so their frequency in cancer datasets partially reflects pre-malignant clonal dynamics rather than tumor-intrinsic selection pressure.

## Limitations

- **Two-tissue scope.** Results are demonstrated only for blood and esophagus. Extension to other cancer types in the cbioportal pipeline (lung, colon, breast, etc.) requires separate modeling; tissue-specific estimates do not automatically transfer.
- **SNV-only driver accounting.** The "30% explained" figure for blood is based on SNVs in known driver genes. Including indels, splice-site mutations, and CNVs would likely increase the explained fraction, but this is not quantified in the paper.
- **Requires deep or single-cell sequencing.** The synonymous VAF excess approach relies on detecting clones at intermediate/high VAF. In bulk exome data from solid tumor cohorts (cBioPortal's primary data type), the signal would be dominated by tumor clones rather than pre-malignant expansions, limiting direct applicability.
- **Healthy tissue only.** The framework is designed for normal, non-cancer tissues. In our pipeline, we work with cancer sequencing data — the conceptual transfer is indirect (informing prior expectations about driver gene elevation), not a direct analysis.
- **Cohort-size sensitivity.** [UNVERIFIED] The framework likely requires large numbers of samples to detect synonymous VAF excess reliably. Applicability to individual small cBioPortal studies is unclear.

## Follow-up

- **Read next:** Martincorena et al. 2018 (Science) — normal esophagus clone size distribution; provides the esophageal data context. Also: Lee-Six et al. 2018 (Nature) for blood clone dynamics underpinning the blood dataset.
- **Question for this project:** Does the ~70% unexplained positive selection in blood translate into specific genes or loci beyond the Bolton 7-gene list that we should add to the `ch_priority_gene` flag? (See `doc/questions/q003-ch-priority-gene-completeness.md`.)
- **Approach implication:** The Poon 2021 finding argues that the current 7-gene `ch_priority_gene` list is a lower bound on CH-related signal inflation. A more conservative approach would be to apply a broader CH exclusion or to restrict "cancer driver" interpretation to non-blood lineage cancers when using unmatched-normal studies.
- **Potential extension:** If single-sample or low-purity tumor data in cBioPortal retains pre-malignant clonal structure at low VAF, the synonymous-passenger test could in principle be adapted to detect background selection elevation in our mutation frequency tables.
