# Crosstalk between cancer cells and the nervous system

- **Authors:** Meng Huang, Gu Gong, Yicheng Deng, Xinmiao Long, Wenyong Long, Qing Liu, Wei Zhao, Rufu Chen
- **Year:** 2023
- **Journal:** Medicine Advances, 1(3): 173–189
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1002/med4.27

- **Source:** PDF

## Key Contribution

A comprehensive narrative review proposing that bidirectional crosstalk between cancer cells and
the nervous system — via the CNS and PNS alike — constitutes an emerging hallmark of cancer.
The review synthesizes seven mechanistic axes of cancer-neuron interaction: (1) direct
neuroglioma synapses and synapse-mediated signaling, (2) autonomic (sympathetic, parasympathetic,
enteric) nerve influence on the tumor microenvironment, (3) tumor-secreted neurotrophins and
axon guidance molecules driving axonogenesis, (4) perineural invasion (PNI), (5) cancer stem
cell transdifferentiation into neurons, (6) cancer cells promoting neural progenitor migration,
and (7) cancer-induced neuron reprogramming. The review covers CNS tumors (glioma) and four
non-CNS cancer types: pancreatic, prostate, gastrointestinal, and breast-to-brain metastases,
identifying specific molecular mediators and therapeutic angles for each.

## Methods

Narrative review (no original data). Covers primary literature on:

- **CNS tumors:** glioma synaptogenesis with neurons via AMPA/NMDA receptor currents, NLGN3,
  BDNF/TrkB, and gap junction-coupled networks.
- **Non-CNS cancer types:** pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC), prostate cancer,
  gastrointestinal cancers, breast-to-brain metastasis (B2BM), small cell lung cancer (SCLC).
- **Mechanisms:** synapse-mediated currents, PNI mediators (neurotrophins, SEMA3D, GDNF/RET),
  axonogenesis factors (NGF, BDNF, GDNF, EphrinB1, netrin-1), autonomic receptor signaling
  (ADRB2, CHRM1, CHRM3), cancer stem cell neurogenesis (MAP2, EGR2, SOX2, HOX genes), and
  enteric neuron reprogramming.
- **Cancer types covered:** glioma, PDAC, prostate, gastric, colorectal, SCLC, B2BM, oral
  cavity squamous cell carcinoma (OCSCC).

No new cohort data, sequencing data, or computational analysis is presented. All quantitative
claims derive from cited primary literature.

## Key Findings

### Mechanism 1 — Synapse-mediated neuron-to-cancer signaling

- **Glioma:** Glioma cells form functional bona fide synapses with glutamatergic neurons via
  AMPA receptors (AMPARs). Neuron-secreted NLGN3 (neuroligin-3) — shed by ADAM10 — drives
  glioma proliferation and synapse-related gene expression via PI3K-mTOR. Gap junction networks
  couple glioma cells and amplify postsynaptic currents. ADAM10 inhibition blocks NLGN3
  shedding and suppresses glioma growth in vivo. NLGN3 knockout in mice blocks engraftment of
  GBM, DIPG, and adult GBM patient-derived xenografts.
- **B2BM:** Breast-to-brain metastasis cells supplant astrocytes and form tripartite synapses;
  glutamate from presynaptic neurons stimulates B2BM colonization via NMDARs (GluN2B-NMDAR).
- **SCLC:** SCLC cells establish functional synapses with glutamatergic neurons in vitro and
  in vivo; insertional mutagenesis/transcriptome screens identified Nrxn1, Nlgn1, Dcc, and Reln
  as highly expressed in murine SCLC models and human specimens. Anti-glutamatergic agents
  (AMPA+NMDA antagonists, riluzole) block SCLC proliferation. Targeting GRM8 (inhibitory
  metabotropic glutamate receptor) with riluzole improved SCLC tumor response to chemotherapy.

### Mechanism 2 — Autonomic nervous system

- **Sympathetic nerves:** Release noradrenaline activating ADRB2 on endothelial cells,
  triggering angiogenesis in prostate and breast cancer. Catecholamines promote PDAC
  axonogenesis via ADRB2/Trk. ADRB2/ADRB3 ablation prevents prostate tumorigenesis. Feed-
  forward loop: sympathetic activation → cancer cells produce more BDNF and NGF → increased
  innervation. Beta-adrenergic receptor blockers have anti-cancer potential (clinical trials:
  NCT02944201, NCT03152786, NCT01847001, NCT03245554, NCT02962947).
- **Parasympathetic nerves:** Effects are cancer-type specific. Vagotomy suppresses gastric
  cancer but promotes pancreatic cancer growth. CHRM1 signaling inhibits MAPK/EGFR and
  PI3K/AKT, reducing PDAC cancer stem cell population. CHRM3 promotes gastric tumorigenesis
  and stem cell expansion.
- **Enteric nervous system (ENS):** NDRG4, expressed in enteric neurons, acts as a biomarker
  for colorectal cancer; NDRG4 loss enhances intestinal adenoma growth. Colorectal cancer cells
  produce tryptophan hydroxylase-2 (in enteric neurons) → 5-hydroxytryptamine (5-HT); 5-HT
  activates Wnt signaling in colorectal CSCs via HTR1B/1D/1F, driving CSC self-renewal and
  colorectal tumorigenesis. 5-HT levels positively associate with gastric cancer stem cell
  marker LGR5.

### Mechanism 3 — Axonogenesis: tumors recruit nerve supply

- Cancer cells secrete NGF, BDNF, GDNF, netrin-1, EphrinB1, and semaphorin 4F (S4F) to
  promote axonogenesis (increase in nerve fiber density in tumor stroma).
- GDNF recruits nerves to PDAC via binding to RET51 receptor isoform; activates Ras-Raf-MEK-ERK
  and PI3K pathways.
- Extracellular vesicles (EVs/exosomes) from cancer cells contain full-length EphrinB1 and
  neurotrophins, promoting axonogenesis; EV release inhibition reduces cancer innervation.
- S4F in prostate cancer modulates axonogenesis and is associated with PNI.

### Mechanism 4 — Perineural invasion (PNI)

- Cancer cells invade epineural, perineural, and endoneural spaces of nerves (>1/3 of the
  nerve circumference).
- Key mediators: NGF family, GDNF family, midkine, SEMA3D, NCAM1 (in PDAC), MUC1, EphrinB1.
- GDNF promotes PNI via RET. NCAM1-dependent mechanism in PDAC.
- Schwann cells attract cancer cells via L1CAM and TGFB; regulate TAMs in the TME.
- Neuropeptide Substance P (SP) acts via NK1-R receptor activating JAK-STAT signaling in PDAC.
- Somatostatin (SST) inhibits endocrine/exocrine secretion via GPCRs (sst1–5); SST agonists
  are effective as anti-tumor agents in gastro-pancreatic neuroendocrine tumors.
- Sensory neuron-derived CCL21 and CXCL10 promote PDAC cell migration toward sensory neurons
  via CCR7 and CXCR3 receptors.
- In PDAC TME: Schwann cells collaborate via IL-6 and TIMP1-CCL7 paracrine loop; CAFs
  (cancer-associated fibroblasts) subtype expresses peripheral nerve-related genes.

### Mechanism 5 — Cancer stem cell transdifferentiation into neurons

- A subset of CSCs in gastric and colorectal carcinomas can transdifferentiate into sympathetic
  or parasympathetic neurons (2017 Lu et al. discovery). MAP2-targeting shRNA knockdown
  abolished xenograft tumor growth.
- ALDH+ CSCs in colon cancer patient organoids/xenografts enriched for neural development
  genes. Neural crest stem cell regulator EGR2, HOX genes, neural stem cell (NSC) master
  regulator SOX2, and master cell fate regulator SOX2 are involved.

### Mechanism 6 — Cancer promotes neural progenitor migration

- Prostate tumors recruit DCX+ (doublecortin-positive) neural progenitors from the
  subventricular zone; these cross the blood-brain barrier, enter circulation, and infiltrate
  the prostate tumor, generating new adrenergic neurons. DCX+ cell depletion inhibited early
  prostate cancer development; DCX+ transplantation promoted tumor growth and metastasis.
- CXCL12/CXCR4 axis and CXCL4 may direct NSC migration toward prostate tumors.

### Mechanism 7 — Neuron reprogramming by cancer cells

- OCSCC p53-deficient cancer cells release EVs that promote neurite outgrowth in dorsal root
  ganglia (DRG) via RAB27A and RAB27B (EV exocytosis genes).
- OCSCC p53-loss leads to dense TH+ adrenergic nerve formation and sensory nerve reprogramming
  via loss of miRNA-34a → neo-adrenergic neurogenesis.
- Sensory denervation or adrenergic receptor blockade significantly inhibited OCSCC growth.

### Glioma-specific BDNF biology

- Pro-BDNF (precursor) and p75NTR are elevated in high-grade glioma; pro-BDNF/p75NTR
  signaling inhibits glioma proliferation.
- Mature BDNF promotes glioma cell proliferation and migration via BDNF-TrkB pathway.
- BDNF/TrkB and pro-BDNF/p75NTR levels positively correlate with glioma grade.
- Differentiated glioblastoma cells (DGCs) and glioblastoma stem cells (GSCs) form a
  paracrine BDNF/TrkB loop; VGF (BDNF pathway) contributes to GSC survival and stemness.
- PI3KCA driver variants in gliomas promote brain hyperexcitability via cell-autonomous
  mechanisms or glypican3; different PI3KCA variants linked to gliomagenesis.

## Relevance

### Bearing on `H1`-`H5` (neural gene hypotheses)

**`H1` (hijack neural circuitry — top-down):** Strongly supported as the dominant mechanistic
framework. The review provides abundant evidence that cancers actively exploit neural
signaling pathways for proliferation, invasion, and metastasis. The key genes and pathways
documented (NLGN3, AMPAR/NMDAR, NRXN1, DCC, RELN) are core synaptic genes. Crucially, the
hijacking is not one-directional: cancer cells *acquire expression* of synaptic genes (Nrxn1,
Nlgn1, Dcc, Reln in SCLC) and neural stem cell genes (EGR2, SOX2, HOX in CSCs) to *become*
synaptically active. This is mechanistically distinct from incidental mutation accumulation.

**`H2` (neural modulation via immune cells):** Partially supported. The review documents that
sympathetic nerves shape TAM populations (sympathectomy increases CD163+ macrophages in PDAC),
parasympathetic signaling modulates TME T-cell infiltration, Schwann cells interact with immune
cells in PNI, and neuropeptides (SP via NK1-R) activate oncogenic pathways. However, the review
does not propose `H2` as a primary mechanism; immune modulation is treated as downstream of
direct cancer-nerve crosstalk.

**`H3` (aberrant developmental/oncofetal expression):** Directly evidenced by Mechanism 5
(CSC transdifferentiation into neurons via EGR2, SOX2, HOX genes) and Mechanism 6 (DCX+
neural progenitor recruitment). The review demonstrates that cancer cells can re-activate
developmental neural programs in a fully mechanistic way.

**`H4` (brain/CNS-cancer artifact):** Explicitly countered for CNS tumors (glioma sections are
detailed and mechanistic). However, the review also demonstrates that PNS-innervated tumors
(PDAC, prostate, gastric, colorectal) show equally deep neural gene expression — NDRG4 in
enteric neurons of colorectal, Nrxn1/Nlgn1/Dcc/Reln in SCLC (a thoracic cancer). `H4` as an
exclusive explanation for the "top mutated neural genes" list is therefore weakened but not
entirely eliminated — CNS-contaminating samples remain a possible contributor.

**`H5` (misannotation):** Not addressed; outside this review's scope.

### Data-driven neural gene definition

The review does NOT provide a data-driven gene list from an unbiased genomic screen. However,
it does cite two instances of unsupervised genomic discovery:

1. **SCLC synaptic genes (Schmitt et al. 2023, bioRxiv):** Insertional mutagenesis screens,
   whole-genome sequencing, and transcriptome sequencing in murine SCLC models and human SCLC
   specimens identified Nrxn1, Nlgn1, Dcc, and Reln as highly expressed — these emerged from
   unbiased screens, not prior hypothesis.
2. **CSC neural development gene signature (Regan et al. 2022, iScience):** Single-cell analysis
   of colon cancer patient-derived organoids revealed enrichment for neural development genes
   in ALDH+ CSCs, and identified EGR2 as a neural crest stem cell regulator required for
   tumor growth.

These represent partial examples of label-free discovery of neural gene expression in cancer.
A fully agnostic definition using, e.g., somatic mutation frequency per-cancer-type versus
expected background (dN/dS) would require accessing the primary genomic data from the cited
studies, which this review does not provide.

## Limitations

- Narrative review with no systematic literature search protocol; coverage is selective, with
  particular depth in glioma and PDAC, and lighter treatment of breast and lung cancers.
- Does not provide an unbiased, data-driven definition of "neural genes" in cancer — the
  candidate genes are identified post-hoc from mechanistic studies.
- Quantitative claims (e.g., clinical trial outcomes, gene expression fold-changes) are sourced
  from primary literature; trace any specific numerical claim to the cited primary paper before
  reuse.
- The SCLC synapse paper cited as Schmitt et al. 2023 was later published as a Nature article
  [@Schmitt2025], supporting Nrxn1/Nlgn1/Dcc/Reln as primary evidence rather than preprint-only
  evidence.
- The review does not quantify how much of the high mutation frequency of neural genes in
  cBioPortal could be explained by the mechanisms it describes versus gene-length confound
  (`H3`/Lawrence et al. [@Lawrence2014] bias). These are orthogonal analytical questions.
- Coverage of immune-modulatory and microenvironmental mechanisms (`H2`) is secondary to direct
  cancer-neuron signaling; a dedicated neuroimmunology review would be needed to fully assess `H2`.
- No population-level or pan-cancer genomic data; the mechanistic findings are predominantly
  from mouse models and cell lines.

## Follow-up

- **Schmitt et al. 2025** [@Schmitt2025]: primary evidence for functional SCLC-neuron synapses
  and Nrxn1, Nlgn1, Dcc, Reln in SCLC.
- **Regan et al. 2022 (iScience 25:104498):** Neural development gene signature in colon cancer
  CSCs (ALDH+ cells) with EGR2 as functional driver — relevant for defining whether "neural
  genes in CRC" reflects oncofetal reprogramming (`H3`) or contamination (`H4`).
- **Magnon et al. 2013 / Science 341:1236361 [ref 46 in Huang2023]:** Autonomic nerve
  development contributes to prostate cancer progression — foundational paper for `H1` in
  prostate; already in `paper:Magnon2023` context.
- **Gene-level follow-up:** Test whether NRXN1, NLGN1, DCC, RELN, NLGN3, GRM8, NDRG4, EGR2,
  and LSAMP appear as significantly mutated in cBioPortal pan-cancer MAF after length
  normalization (dN/dS), or whether they drop out (distinguishing `H1`/`H3` from `H4` length-bias).
- **LSAMP connection:** The cBioPortal top-mutated neural gene list includes LSAMP (limbic
  system-associated membrane protein). LSAMP is a GPI-anchored cell adhesion molecule
  expressed in limbic neurons; whether it has a documented cancer-neuroscience role analogous
  to NLGN3 merits a targeted search.
- **SGCZ, OPCML, CALN1, RIT2, RBFOX1, KCNIP4, NKAIN2, TAFA2:** The other neural genes
  from the cBioPortal top-mutated list. This review does not mention them; their absence from
  the cancer-neuroscience literature is itself informative and could support `H4` (length bias
  on genes incidentally annotated as neural) over `H1`. A targeted dN/dS analysis against the
  background mutation rate for each gene's cancer type would be the definitive test.
- **CHRM1 / CHRM3 therapeutic angle in PDAC:** Clinical relevance of cholinergic receptor
  modulation — CHRM1 inhibits cancer stem cells but CHRM3 promotes them; the net effect
  depends on cancer type. Worth tracking in the context of TME-modulating annotations.
