# 3D clusters of somatic mutations in cancer reveal numerous rare mutations as functional targets

- **Authors:** Gao J, Chang MT, Johnsen HC, Gao SP, Sylvester BE, Sumer SO, Zhang H, Solit DB, Taylor BS, Schultz N, Sander C
- **Year:** 2017
- **Journal:** Genome Medicine (confirmed via PubMed; earlier project notes citing *Cancer Cell* were incorrect)
- **PMID:** 28115009
- **PMC:** PMC5260099
- **DOI:** 10.1186/s13073-016-0393-x
- **BibTeX key:** Gao2017

## Key Contribution

Extends Chang 2016's single-residue (1D) hotspot detection into three dimensions by testing for
statistically significant spatial clustering of missense mutations on mapped PDB protein structures.
The method recovers a large "long tail" of rare functional mutations — residues individually too
infrequent to be called as 1D hotspots but spatially contiguous with hotspots or with other rare
mutations in the folded protein. Of 3,404 residues in 943 significant 3D clusters, only 103 are
also 1D hotspots; the rest (≈97%) are novel, including experimentally validated activating drivers
in RAC1 and MAP2K1.

## Methods

- **3D cluster definition:** A cluster = one central residue plus all neighbor residues sharing
  at least one atom pair within **5 Å** (BioJava Structure Module). Clusters are not merged, so
  a residue may appear in multiple clusters.
- **Structural data:** PDB structures from RCSB; UniProt↔PDB alignments via MutationAssessor and
  SIFTS, requiring ≥90% sequence identity. 32,445 structures covering 7,390 of 18,100 genes
  (1,307 genes with >90% residue coverage).
- **Statistical model:** Per-gene permutation test (10⁵ permutations) that shuffles residue
  indices while preserving total mutation count. P-value = fraction of decoys whose cluster
  mutation count ≥ observed. Critically, the per-residue count in decoys is capped at the
  observed cluster's max so that genes dominated by a single huge hotspot (e.g. BRAF V600)
  don't wash out secondary clusters.
- **Cohort:** 11,119 tumors across 32 cancer types, 1,182,802 missense mutations in 1,025,590
  residues, pulled from TCGA + ICGC + published studies. All samples are **whole-exome or
  whole-genome** — no targeted-panel cohorts are included. Filters: germline variants in 1000
  Genomes / NHLBI ESP removed; genes with TPM < 0.1 in ≥90% of tumors removed; GRCh37.
- **Comparison vs Chang 2016 (1D):** Every residue in a significant 3D cluster is classified as
  (i) itself a 1D hotspot, (ii) rare but co-clustered with a 1D hotspot, or (iii) rare and
  co-clustered only with other rare residues. Also benchmarked against Mutation3D, HotMAPS, and
  Hotspot3D.

## Key Findings

- **943 significant 3D clusters** spanning **3,404 residues in 503 genes** across 2,382 structures.
- **1D-vs-3D overlap is small:** only **103/3,404 (3%)** of 3D-cluster residues are 1D hotspots;
  **263** are rare residues paired with a 1D hotspot in the same 3D cluster; **3,038** are rare
  residues clustered only with other rare residues (fully 3D-only signal). Conversely, **367**
  of Chang 2016's 1D hotspots fail to appear in any significant 3D cluster (no structure, or no
  additional spatial recurrence). So the two catalogs are largely complementary, not redundant.
- **Clinical yield:** Across 5,038 samples lacking any 1D-hotspot driver, the 3D method supplies
  a candidate driver in 865 (17% of the sub-cohort, ~8% of all tumors).
- **3D-only examples:** PTEN (48 residues in 15 clusters around the phosphatase catalytic core);
  CDH1 (11 rare residues, 0.01–0.06% individual frequency, clustering around a calcium-binding
  junction despite sequence positions 165–291); KEAP1 (26 rare residues in the NRF2-binding
  interface, enriched in lung adenocarcinoma).
- **Experimental validation:**
  - **MAP2K1 / MEK1:** 7-residue cluster (p = 0.03) on helix A (negative-regulatory interface
    with kinase domain). Five rare mutations tested (G128D, Y130C, F53L, Q56P, K57N) all
    increased ERK1/2 phosphorylation; Y130C and Q56P remained trametinib-sensitive.
  - **RAC1:** 4-residue cluster (p = 0.009) containing known hotspots P29/A159 plus rare G15/C18.
    G15S and C18Y showed elevated GTP-bound RAC1 (PAK1 pulldown) comparable to P29S / A159V.
- **Cross-method overlap:** only 15 residues detected by all four 3D methods; 2,908 of this
  paper's residues are unique to it vs Mutation3D/HotMAPS/Hotspot3D.
- **Downloadable resource:** catalog published at **http://3dhotspots.org** (searchable + web API)
  and integrated into cBioPortal (mutations flagged when part of a 3D cluster). Full tables of
  clusters and residues ship as Additional files 2–5 (Tables S1–S4).

## Relevance

3D-spatial generalization of Chang 2016's residue-level hotspot model. Detects hotspots that
are adjacent in protein tertiary structure rather than primary sequence — recovers low-recurrence
drivers missed by the 1D method. Closes the loop on the `hotspot-based-driver-detection` topic
and provides another downloadable hotspot reference for our gene x cancer outputs.

## Limitations

- **Structural coverage gap:** only 41% of genes (7,390/18,100) map to PDB; only 1,307 have
  >90% residue coverage. Most structures are single domains — cross-domain / interface clusters
  are under-detected. Authors point to future use of cryo-EM structures and EVfold contact
  predictions.
- **Power at long-tail frequencies:** known activating AKT1 D323 fails significance (p = 0.11)
  because its 3D neighbors (R15, E17, W22) are each too rare at n ≈ 11k. Expanding cohorts will
  promote more of these.
- **WES/WGS-only derivation:** the frequency-based permutation null is calibrated on exome-wide
  mutation distributions; applying it directly to panel data (MSK-IMPACT, Foundation) would bias
  toward the deeply sequenced driver genes on the panel. The published catalog itself is static,
  so it can be *applied to* panel variant calls, but the discovery procedure is not panel-ready.
- **Method-to-method inconsistency:** only 15 residues overlap across the four 3D-clustering
  tools, indicating each encodes different sensitivity/specificity trade-offs.

## Follow-up

- 3dhotspots.org catalog is a natural companion to Chang 2016's cancerhotspots.org — both get
  consumed in the same way (annotate our per-sample variant tables, flag driver-candidate rare
  mutations in gene × cancer summaries).
- MSK later integrates both 1D and 3D hotspot calls into OncoKB evidence tiers; worth checking
  whether our pipeline should pull the merged OncoKB "hotspot" flag instead of the two catalogs
  separately.
- Open question: can we re-run a 3D test restricted to panel-covered genes on MSK-IMPACT cohorts
  with a panel-aware null? Not done in this paper.
