# Deciphering gene contributions and etiologies of somatic mutational signatures of cancer

- **Authors:** Xiangwen Ji, Edwin Wang, Qinghua Cui
- **Year:** 2023
- **Journal:** Briefings in Bioinformatics, 24(2), 1–15
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1093/bib/bbad017
- **BibTeX key:** Ji2023
- **Source:** PDF

## Key Contribution

The paper introduces Gene Somatic Genome Pattern (GSGP), a computational framework that assigns NMF-derived mutational signatures at the gene level rather than only at the sample level, enabling identification of which genes drive each signature and providing molecular-mechanism anchors for etiology inference. The method extends classical SBS analysis by using eQTL-derived weights to distribute mutation-context frequencies across genes, then solves the NMF factorization per gene simultaneously, recovering per-gene signature scores (GSGP scores) alongside the standard per-sample scores. A first-of-its-kind application to RNAseq-derived somatic mutation calls is demonstrated, successfully predicting an RNA-derived signature (SigD) associated with dMMR and MSI in colorectal cancer — showing the framework generalises beyond WES/WGS data.

## Methods

**Algorithm (GSGP):**
1. For each sample, mutation sites are annotated to genes (exon, intron, upstream, downstream) using bedtools; weights per gene per mutation context are derived from the density of cis-eQTLs in each genomic compartment (obtained from TCGA per-cancer-type eQTL analysis using Beagle-imputed germline genotypes and TMM-normalised expression; the MAGENTA enrichment framework informed weight-threshold selection). A "NONE" gene captures mutations far from any gene.
2. A weighted frequency matrix X (genes × 96 mutation contexts) is constructed per sample; all samples are stacked. NMF is applied with H fixed to the 78 COSMIC SBS v3.2 reference signatures to solve for the GSGP weight matrix W (genes × 78 signatures), adding a sample-level row simultaneously.
3. GSEA (ClusterProfiler) on ranked GSGP scores provides gene-set–level functional interpretation per signature.

**Data:** Somatic mutations from 10,114 TCGA samples across 33 tumor types downloaded from the GDC; cis-eQTLs from 10,389 TCGA patients (Huang et al. 2018); RNAseq mutation calls from two CRC cohorts (ZN-CRC n=72, CP-CRC n=45) processed with STAR + Mutect2; microsatellite status from TCGA COAD/READ and CPTAC CRC. Signature validation used SigProfiler (de novo extraction) across multiple independent melanoma cohorts.

**Statistical:** Wilcoxon rank-sum tests for group comparisons; log-rank + Kaplan–Meier for survival; F1 scores for MSI/MSS discrimination; Benjamini–Hochberg FDR; hierarchical clustering (complete linkage, Euclidean distance) for signature-cancer-type co-occurrence maps.

## Key Findings

**TP53 as a positive control:** GSGP consistently ranks TP53 in the top 10 of 44,260 genes for 11 signatures with known etiology — the expected result given TP53's high mutation rate and its role as a key tumor suppressor.

**SBS2 (APOBEC) gene biology:** Across 11 TCGA cancer categories, 493 ubiquitous high-scoring genes and 7,822 category-specific genes were detected for SBS2. Ubiquitous genes are enriched for cell–cell adhesion (GSEA FDR << 0.01), consistent with APOBEC-mediated invasion and metastasis biology. Melanoma had the most specific genes (2,343); pheochromocytoma the fewest (136).

**SBS4 (tobacco smoking) and cholinergic receptors:** GSGP of SBS4 is enriched for genes involved in sensory perception of chemical stimuli and behavioral response to nicotine, with the top-contributing genes being cholinergic receptor genes (CHRNA3, CHRNA4, CHRNA5, CHRNB1, etc.) — consistent with GWAS findings linking these loci to tobacco addiction and lung cancer risk.

**SBS7 / UV subtypes (SBS7a–d, SBS55):** GSGP profiles of the four SBS7 subtypes cluster distinctly by t-SNE. GSEA on SBS7a genes finds "response to UVC" enrichment (NES = 1.71); SBS7c genes show "response to UVB" enrichment. GSGP identifies SBS55 (a de novo signature, previously classified as possible artifact) as UV-exposure-related: (i) GSEA on SBS55 genes covers all three UV-wavelength pathways (UVA/B/C) simultaneously; (ii) SBS55 presence significantly associates with worse overall survival in TCGA SKCM (log-rank P = 0.00422); (iii) SBS55 is significantly elevated in acral melanoma vs cutaneous/mucosal subtypes (Wilcoxon P = 9.98 × 10⁻⁴), consistent with acral sites being areas of poor UV defense rather than high UV exposure; (iv) de novo extraction of an independent cohort signature refits 49.52% from SBS55.

**SBS54 as a new dMMR/MSI signature:** Hierarchical clustering of 78 SBS signatures by cancer-type co-occurrence groups SBS54 with 6 of 7 established dMMR signatures. GSGP analysis of the 6 etiology-unknown signatures in this cluster finds 5/6 related to MMR/BER pathways. SBS54 maximally discriminates MSI from MSS CRC samples in TCGA COAD+READ (FDR = 1.10 × 10⁻¹⁶) and CPTAC CRC (FDR = 8.14 × 10⁻¹¹) — outperforming all 7 established dMMR signatures. This is validated in TCGA STAD as well.

**SBS34 as a candidate dHRR signature:** SBS34 shares tissue distribution with SBS3 (the proven dHRR signature); GSGP of SBS34 genes is enriched in the homologous recombination process (supplementary data).

**RNAseq MS analysis:** Applying GSGP to RNAseq mutation calls from two CRC cohorts extracts 23 (CP-CRC) and 21 (ZN-CRC) de novo signatures. Seven stably-paired signatures are reconstructed into SigA–SigG. SigD is most enriched for MMR gene sets (GSEA, highest NES) and best discriminates MSI from MSS patients in both cohorts (F1 score = 0.988 and 0.985 respectively). This is an RNA-level MSI-associated signature not previously characterised.

**Driver gene overlap check:** Top-ranked GSGP genes are *not* generally enriched for driver genes (IntOGen), ruling out simple reflection of driver mutation rates. The enrichment is specific to a few cases (SBS2 and SBS13 show some driver enrichment in certain cancer types).

**Web tool:** An online interface at https://www.cuilab.cn/gsgp accepts VCF files and outputs per-gene GSGP scores, heatmaps, and aggregated 2D views; Python scripts are also provided.

## Relevance

This paper is directly relevant to **hypothesis h08** (agnostic covariate↔signature-exposure association), especially the **H08a positive-control prong**:

- **H08a positive-control validation:** GSGP is itself an agnostic, data-driven method that recovers known aetiologies without being told them. The paper demonstrates successful recovery of UV↔SBS7 (H08a arm 1), smoking↔SBS4 (H08a arm 2, via cholinergic receptor enrichment confirming the exposure link), and the APOBEC/SBS2 story (H08a arm 3 analog, via cell-adhesion gene enrichment). The method also surfaces MMR/BER-related genes for dMMR signatures — parallel to the MSI↔MMR-signature association that h08 will test with clinical MSI status as a covariate. This constitutes independent methodological precedent that the positive-control recovery is achievable by an algorithm rather than a curator.

- **SBS54 as a new MSI marker:** The identification of SBS54 as a stronger MSI discriminator than the 7 established dMMR signatures is directly actionable for h08's within-tissue association design — including SBS54 in the set of tested signatures may increase power to recover the MSI↔MMR-signature positive control.

- **RNAseq MS analysis:** The CRC RNAseq application demonstrates that signature decomposition can be applied to transcriptomic mutation calls and that an RNA-derived dMMR/MSI signature (SigD) emerges — relevant to q018 (feasibility of downstream signature extraction from the cbioportal export pipeline, which now optionally includes expression data).

- **Gene-level GSGP scores vs sample-level H matrix:** The GSGP framework provides a complementary perspective to the per-sample signature exposure (H matrix) used in h08. GSGP scores characterise *which genes* are affected by each signature process — these could serve as a secondary evidence layer when interpreting expression-module↔signature associations (expression of the implicated genes should cohere with the signature's GSGP profile).

- **Cross-study aggregation context:** The pipeline in this project pools somatic mutations across cBioPortal studies. GSGP applied to such a pooled matrix would require careful study-level normalisation (the tool currently processes one cohort's VCF at a time); nonetheless, the analytical framing — using per-mutation-context gene weights to decompose signatures into gene-level contributions — is conceptually applicable to the aggregated gene × cancer matrix, potentially extending the current count/ratio framework.

## Limitations

- **SBS-only scope:** Only single base substitution signatures are analysed; doublet base substitutions, indels, and copy-number signatures are excluded. This is acknowledged as a future direction.
- **eQTL data dependency:** Weight calibration relies on cancer-type-specific cis-eQTL data from TCGA; applying GSGP to non-TCGA cohorts requires either re-computing eQTLs or using GTEx weights, introducing a potential mismatch. The authors show GTEx and BRCA-cohort eQTLs give cosine similarity >0.996 with TCGA eQTLs for the SBS3 test case, suggesting robustness, but this is one signature in one cancer type.
- **RNAseq mutation calling limitations:** Not all DNA mutations are transcribed; RNA editing, allele-specific expression, and splice artifacts confound RNAseq-based mutation calling. The authors apply extensive filtering (STAR alignment, Mutect2, depth >10, VAF >1%, GTEx artifact filters) but acknowledge that RNAseq signatures will differ from WGS/WES counterparts.
- **Reference-list constraint:** Using all 78 COSMIC SBS signatures as the reference for NMF factorization can introduce false positives (signatures that do not exist in the studied tumors) — a limitation the authors acknowledge and contrast with de novo extraction.
- **Sample size for RNAseq cohorts:** The two CRC RNAseq cohorts are small (n=45 and n=72), limiting statistical power for the RNA MS analysis beyond the main MSI discrimination result.
- **Driver gene enrichment confound:** High GSGP scores may partly reflect high driver mutation rates rather than signature-specific biology. The authors address this by examining the top-10% overlap with IntOGen driver genes and finding it is generally not significant, but the check is per-signature rather than a formal correction.

- GSGP's gene-level signature scores could serve as a validation layer for expression-module↔signature associations in h08: if APOBEC3A/B expression correlates with SBS2/13 exposures, GSGP should show APOBEC3 genes near the top of the SBS2/13 gene rankings.
- The paper cites Hayward et al. (Nature 2017) for SBS7 in melanoma subtypes and Saini et al. (PLoS Genet 2016) for environmental/endogenous contributions to SBS7 — both are relevant background for the h08 UV positive-control arm.
- The RNAseq MS application is a proof-of-concept that mutation-calling on RNA-seq data can recover biologically meaningful signatures; relevant to any future expansion of the cbioportal pipeline to RNA-based cohorts that lack matched WES.
- The smoking/cholinergic-receptor finding raises the question of whether GWAS germline SNPs in nicotinic receptor genes are in LD with somatic mutation hotspots in lung cancer — a potential cross-decomposition concordance test for h08b.
