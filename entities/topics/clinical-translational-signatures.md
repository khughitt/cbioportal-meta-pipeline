# Clinical and translational applications of mutational signatures (incl. TMB)

## Summary

Mutational signatures have matured from descriptive catalogues of cancer aetiology into actionable clinical biomarkers. Established links between specific signatures and therapy response — HRD/SBS3 predicting PARP inhibitor sensitivity, MMR-deficiency signatures predicting immunotherapy benefit, APOBEC/SBS2-SBS13 associating with ATR-inhibitor sensitivity — now span multiple cancer types and are beginning to enter clinical decision-making. TMB (tumour mutational burden) sits at the boundary between a crude summary statistic and a signature-informed measurement, and the field is actively negotiating what refinements (clonal, expressed, neoantigen-corrected) best predict ICI response beyond the FDA-approved 10 mut/Mb threshold. For hypothesis `h08`, this literature constitutes both the positive-control benchmark (known aetiology-to-signature links that any agnostic scan must recover) and the intellectual precedent for the discovery arm (novel covariate-to-signature associations as a source of clinical insight).

---

## Key Concepts

**Signature-based biomarker.** A mutational signature exposure, rather than the presence of a specific gene mutation, used to stratify patients for therapy. Unlike single-gene biomarkers, signature exposures integrate the cumulative effect of a mutagenic process across thousands of mutations and are therefore robust to the specific genes affected.

**Tumour mutational burden (TMB).** The count of somatic mutations per megabase of sequenced genome (mut/Mb). TMB is a first-order summary of mutational process activity; it conflates contributions from many signatures and is confounded by sequencing platform, tumour purity, panel size, and patient ancestry. The FDA approved pembrolizumab for TMB-high (≥10 mut/Mb) solid tumours in 2020 based on Keynote-158, but the EMA rejected the same application given insufficient evidence of clinical benefit across histologies (paper:Budczies2024).

**HRD score / HR-deficiency signatures.** A composite of SBS3 (flat C>T background), indel signature ID6 (microhomology-mediated deletions, a proxy for alternative NHEJ), and structural variant signatures (RS3/RS5), together reflecting functional homologous recombination loss. HRDetect combines six features (two SBSs, one ID, two RSs, one LOH measure) achieving AUC ≈ 0.98 for biological BRCA1/2 deficiency in breast/ovarian cancer (paper:Koh2021).

**Therapy-induced signatures.** Signatures generated by prior treatment rather than by tumour-intrinsic biology: SBS87 (thiopurines), SBS11 (temozolomide in MMR-deficient glioma), SBS31/SBS35 (platinum), and radiation-associated indels. These are clinically informative in two directions — identifying treatment exposure and flagging potential mutagenic contraindications (paper:Brady2022).

**Multimodal SBS+ID signatures.** Joint NMF extraction on concatenated SBS96 + ID83 count matrices, as opposed to SBS-only extraction. This approach resolves signatures that are degenerate in the SBS space alone — most importantly splitting SBS3 into BRCA1-linked m-SBS3a (ID8-rich, large deletions) and BRCA2-linked m-SBS3b (ID6-rich, microhomology deletions) (paper:FerrerTorres2025).

## Current State of Knowledge

### Therapy-predictive signatures: what is settled

The field has converged on a short list of signature-to-therapy links with substantial clinical evidence.

**HR deficiency (SBS3 and composites) and PARP inhibitors / platinum.** SBS3 prevalence in gastric cancer (7-12%, independent of BRCA1/2 mutations — paper:Alexandrov2015) extends the actionable HRD population beyond BRCA carriers. HRDetect captures ~30% of HRD tumours that targeted sequencing misses (paper:Koh2021). Multimodal m-SBS3b is a significantly stronger survival predictor in HGSOC than standard SBS3 or any existing HRD classifier (C-index advantage over CHORD, GIS, HRDetect; paper:FerrerTorres2025), with the survival benefit replicating across four independent cohorts. Refitting with MuSiCal onto the multimodal catalogue achieves comparable results to de novo extraction, enabling clinical deployment without per-cohort NMF.

**MMR deficiency (SBS6/15/21/44 + ID signatures) and immunotherapy.** MMR deficiency is the most robustly approved ICI biomarker across solid tumours. Signature-based MMR detection captures promoter-hypermethylation and focal-deletion causes that escape exome sequencing of MMR genes (paper:Brady2022). Multimodal m-SBS44+ID7 was more specifically associated with survival and immunotherapy response in colorectal cancer (AUC 0.789 vs 0.730 for SBS-only; paper:FerrerTorres2025), reinforcing that indel information is load-bearing for MMR detection.

**APOBEC (SBS2/13) and ATR inhibition / ICI.** APOBEC signatures correlate with ATR-inhibitor sensitivity across breast and ovarian cancers (paper:Brady2022). APOBEC3A/B overexpression drives SBS2/13 and correlates with PD-L1/PD-L2 expression; SBS2/13 independently associate with higher ICI response in NSCLC beyond bulk TMB (paper:Budczies2024; paper:Ostroverkhova2023). The mechanistic link — APOBEC deaminates ssDNA at stalled replication forks; ATR stabilises those forks; combining APOBEC activity with ATR loss is synthetic-lethal — is supported by both biochemical evidence and cell-line pharmacogenomics (paper:Levatic2022).

**POLE/POLD1 (SBS10) and immunotherapy.** POLE-deficient tumours generate ultra-high TMB with a striking C>T pattern; they are enriched responders to immune checkpoint blockade and are now used in clinical triage. The review literature treats this as settled (paper:Koh2021; paper:Ostroverkhova2023).

**Tobacco smoking (SBS4) and ICI.** SBS4 is associated with improved anti-PD-1 response in NSCLC, independent of TMB, likely through an elevated functional neoantigen burden from tobacco-specific C>A transversions at lung-driver hotspots such as KRAS G12C (paper:Budczies2024; paper:Ostroverkhova2023).

**Systematic drug-sensitivity screen.** Across 930 GDSC cell lines and 518 drugs, ancestry-corrected mutational signature exposures predict drug response at least as well as driver mutations, CNAs, and DNA methylation, yielding 290 replicated associations (paper:Levatic2022). Repair-deficiency signatures predict sensitivity (MMR → AKT inhibitors; HR → PARPi; NER → EGFR/ERBB2 inhibitors); prior-exposure signatures (SBS25L chemotherapy, SBS42L haloalkane, SBS4/45L tobacco) predict resistance, consistent with selection for mutational robustness in treated-survivor cells.

### Therapy-contraindication signatures

Therapy-induced mutagenesis is underappreciated clinically. Thiopurines in MMR-deficient ALL generate SBS87, which drives TP53, NR3C1, and NT5C2 driver mutations at relapse; in MMR-deficient clones, the mutagenic rate is 10-fold higher (paper:Brady2022). Temozolomide in MMR-deficient glioblastoma generates SBS11 and CDKN2A/PI3K driver mutations. Radiation generates indel signatures enriched at relapse and is associated with worse survival in relapsed glioma. Importantly, platinum-induced SBS31/SBS35 rarely produce recurrent driver-gene variants, making platinum mutagenesis less clinically actionable than thiopurine or temozolomide mutagenesis.

### TMB as biomarker: evolving but contested

The 10 mut/Mb FDA cut-off receives mixed clinical validation. Panel size, tumour purity, and ancestry-related SNP database gaps are major confounders; panels below 1 Mb reliably underestimate TMB, and TMB at <40% tumour purity is substantially deflated (paper:Budczies2024). Proposed refinements — clonal TMB (cTMB), persistent TMB (pTMB), expressed TMB (eTMB), HLA-corrected TMB, and tumour neoantigen burden (TNB) — show variable improvements in retrospective studies but lack prospective randomised evidence. Cancer-type-specific or relative (top-20-percentile) cut-offs consistently outperform the fixed 10 mut/Mb threshold for most histologies. The community increasingly views TMB as a surrogate for underlying signature activity rather than a direct predictor; disaggregating TMB into its signature components is mechanistically more coherent and often more predictive.

## Controversies and Open Questions

**Unimodal vs multimodal signature extraction.** SBS-only analysis conflates mechanistically distinct HR-deficiency states (BRCA1 vs BRCA2 deficiency) and MMR contexts that indel signatures cleanly separate (paper:FerrerTorres2025). The field has not yet converged on whether multimodal SBS+ID should be the default for clinical contexts — this requires WGS, excludes most panel-sequenced cohorts, and demands validated re-fitting tools (MuSiCal, SigProfiler Assignment extensions). The clinical benefit in HGSOC is compelling, but pan-cancer generalisation is not yet demonstrated.

**Ongoing vs historical signatures and temporal inference.** Clock-like signatures (SBS1, SBS5) accumulate at low constant rates; APOBEC signatures fluctuate with replication stress; tobacco SBS4 is historical once the patient stops smoking. An agnostic scan associating a clinical smoking-history label with SBS4 will be attenuated if many ex-smokers have ceased accumulating SBS4 (paper:Koh2021). This temporal mismatch is not systematically corrected by any current clinical biomarker framework.

**Ancestry bias in TMB measurement.** Non-white patients have inflated apparent TMB due to incomplete germline SNP database coverage of their ancestral backgrounds (paper:Budczies2024). Ancestry-matched germline correction (as demonstrated in cell lines by paper:Levatic2022) substantially reduces this bias but is not standard clinical practice.

**Reverse causation and bidirectionality.** A mutational signature causes specific driver hotspots (UV→BRAF V600E, APOBEC→PIK3CA), but driver mutations also alter the mutational landscape (POLE mutations generate SBS10). Therapeutic expression associations (APOBEC3B overexpression as a signature correlate) may reflect downstream consequences of the same upstream HR/replication stress pathways rather than an independent driver of mutagenesis (paper:Ostroverkhova2023; paper:Koh2021). Signature-to-drug-response links in cell lines may be confounded by the evolutionary history of the line rather than by current repair capacity.

**Signature detection on panel-sequenced samples.** Most clinical and cBioPortal data is from targeted panels covering <1 Mb. Signature decomposition at this scale is unreliable for all but the most dominant signatures, and indel-channel sparsity makes ID signatures essentially inaccessible (paper:Koh2021). The per-sample reliability of signature exposures derived from panel data is an open question bearing directly on `h08` implementation (question:0018).

**ICI combinations with chemotherapy abolish TMB predictive value.** When ICIs are combined with chemotherapy (Keynote-189, Keynote-407, Keynote-21), TMB loses its predictive value, making TMB-based patient selection specific to ICI monotherapy contexts (paper:Budczies2024). Mutational signature strategies for these combination regimens are undeveloped.

## Implications for `h08` and the cross-study signature-aetiology aggregation

**Positive-control validation (H08a).** `topic:clinical-translational-signatures` consolidates the ground-truth aetiology-to-signature links that H08a must recover without prompting: UV↔SBS7 (melanoma), tobacco smoking↔SBS4 (NSCLC), APOBEC3 expression↔SBS2/SBS13 (breast/ovarian/cervical/bladder), and MMR-loss/MSI↔SBS6/15/21/26 (colorectal/endometrial). These associations have computational, experimental, and clinical validation across multiple independent cohorts and modalities. A cross-study agnostic scan that fails to rank these known associations among the top hits would be strong evidence of a systematic flaw — in covariate selection, signature assignment, or study-level confounding.

**Mechanistic depth for interpretation.** The distinction between SBS2 (C>T, uncorrected APOBEC deamination) and SBS13 (C>G, REV1-mediated) is mechanistically informative: APOBEC3A preferentially generates SBS2, APOBEC3B preferentially generates SBS13. An agnostic scan associating per-gene APOBEC3A vs APOBEC3B expression against SBS2 vs SBS13 exposures within cancer types would test this mechanistic split — a specific H08b refinement that this literature supports.

**SBS3/HRD sub-signature resolution.** The multimodal SBS+ID m-SBS3a/m-SBS3b split (paper:FerrerTorres2025) reveals that standard SBS-only assignment will conflate BRCA1 and BRCA2 HRD contexts. Any `h08` analysis using SBS3 as a signature endpoint should consider whether WGS/WES-sufficient studies can run multimodal extraction, and flag SBS3 associations in panel-only studies as unreliable. The confounded signatures SBS39 and SBS8 (non-HRd; paper:FerrerTorres2025) should be quarantined from HRD positive-control interpretation.

**Drug resistance as a confound.** Prior-exposure signatures (SBS25L chemotherapy, SBS4/45L tobacco, SBS42L haloalkane) predict resistance in the Levatic cell-line screen (paper:Levatic2022). In the cBioPortal context, studies that enrich for pre-treated or relapsed patients will carry chemotherapy-exposure signatures that co-occur with poor outcomes, potentially producing spurious covariate-to-signature associations. The treatment-history confound (`h08` alternative explanation R4) must be addressed by auditing study-level treatment metadata before finalising the H08a association scan.

**TMB as an intermediate variable, not a direct covariate.** The cBioPortal pipeline's hypermutator annotation (`is_hypermutator`, `hypermutator_reason`) and per-cancer-type GMM (`fit_per_cancer_tmb_gmm`) are consistent with this topic's consensus that cancer-type-specific relative thresholds outperform the fixed 10 mut/Mb criterion. For `h08`, TMB should be a covariate to condition on (or a stratum to analyse within) rather than a signature endpoint — high TMB itself is not a single aetiology but a consequence of multiple mutagenic processes.

**Panel-data limitation for signature endpoints.** Most cBioPortal studies are panel-sequenced. This means per-sample signature exposures will be unreliable for all but the most abundant signatures (POLE-driven SBS10, MSI-driven SBS6/15, possibly APOBEC SBS2/13 in high-burden cases). The `h08` discovery arm must pre-register which signatures are testable at panel scale versus which require a WGS/WES stratum (question:0018). The gastric cancer SBS3 finding (7-12% by WES; paper:Alexandrov2015) provides a floor estimate: approximately 7-12% of gastric samples carrying SBS3 would be the minimum detectable prevalence at WES scale; panel-based detection will miss most of these.

**cfDNA and on-treatment monitoring.** Brady et al. [@Brady2022] and Koh et al. [@Koh2021] both flag liquid-biopsy signature detection as an emerging frontier, but current sensitivity at low ctDNA fractions is limiting. This is out of scope for the present cross-study pipeline but should be noted as the direction toward which signature biomarkers are moving — the cBioPortal aggregation targets archival primary-tumour biopsies and its findings would need separate validation in cfDNA contexts.
