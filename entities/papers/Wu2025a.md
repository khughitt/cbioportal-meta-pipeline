# Neuronal Reshaping of the Tumor Microenvironment in Tumorigenesis and Metastasis: Bench to Clinic

- **Authors:** Yimao Wu, Ruowei Sun, Shuai Ren, Gokhan Zengin, Mengyao Li (Wu and Sun are co-first authors)
- **Year:** 2025
- **Journal:** Medicine Advances, 3: 364–371
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1002/med4.70044
- **BibTeX key:** Wu2025a
- **Source:** PDF

## Key Contribution

This neuronal-tumor-microenvironment review note links paper:Hanahan2023.

This open-access commentary systematically reviews the three principal axes through which peripheral nerves remodel the tumor microenvironment (TME): nerve–tumor crosstalk (neurotransmitter and neurotrophic factor signaling that drives proliferation, invasion, and metabolic reprogramming), neuro–vascular crosstalk (pro-angiogenic, permeability-increasing, and ECM-remodeling effects on tumor vasculature), and neuro–immune crosstalk (immunosuppressive rewiring via PD-1/PD-L1 modulation, macrophage polarization, T-cell exhaustion, and MDSC recruitment). The review synthesizes clinical translation prospects, cataloging six drug classes that target the neural–TME interface, and tables three categories of challenges (technical, ethical, safety) that impede translation. The paper situates cancer neuroscience as a third major TME-regulatory system beyond immune and vascular stromal components.

## Methods

Narrative review / commentary. No primary data are generated. The authors:
- Survey ~76 references spanning 2018–2025, weighted toward recent mechanistic and translational studies.
- Organize findings around three crosstalk modalities (nerve–tumor, neuro–vascular, neuro–immune), with tabular summaries (Table 1: core neuronal crosstalk modes; Table 2: drugs targeting neural pathways in TME; Table 3: clinical-translation challenges).
- Represent cancer types with the most mechanistic literature: glioblastoma, pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC), prostate cancer, breast cancer, melanoma, and colorectal cancer.
- Schematic (Figure 1) maps the molecular actors across the three axes with receptor-level granularity.

## Key Findings

**1. Nerve–Tumor Crosstalk: neurotransmitters and neurotrophic factors drive tumor-type-specific growth programs.**

- Norepinephrine (NE) from sympathetic nerves activates **β₂-adrenergic receptors (β₂-AR)** on PDAC cells, driving glycolytic metabolism and invasion (late-stage TME adaptation). In early-stage breast cancer, the same NE acts via **β₃-ARs** to promote cell proliferation rather than metabolic reprogramming — illustrating that the same ligand elicits distinct programs depending on receptor subtype and disease stage.
- Acetylcholine (ACh) from cholinergic neurons binds **M3 muscarinic receptors (M3 mAChR)** on glioblastoma cells to promote growth; knock-down reverses this. In colorectal cancer, ACh activates **nicotinic receptors (nAChRs)** on tumor stem cells to maintain self-renewal.
- Substance P (neuropeptide from sensory nerves) binds **tachykinin receptor 1 (TACR1)** to trigger metastasis; it also promotes apoptosis in cells with high TACR1 expression, generating single-stranded RNA that activates **toll-like receptor 7 (TLR7)** via paracrine signaling — a novel heterogeneity-driven metastatic mechanism.
- **Nerve growth factor (NGF)** drives stage-specific neural recruitment: in prostate cancer, early-stage tumor cells upregulate NGF to recruit sympathetic nerves that secrete NE → androgen-independent progression (feed-forward loop). In melanoma, NGF promotes primary tumor innervation; its homolog **neurotrophin-3** regulates neural crosstalk in lymph node metastases.
- **Brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF)** via **tropomyosin receptor kinase B (TrkB)** is predominantly expressed in late-stage metastatic niches (e.g., bone marrow in small-cell lung cancer), inducing sensory nerve infiltration and supporting colonization.
- **Galectin-3 (Gal3)** and **leukemia inhibitory factor (LIF)** are cited as MDSCs-associated actors in the nerve–tumor panel (Figure 1 legend).

**2. Neuro–Vascular Crosstalk: neural signals amplify angiogenesis and alter barrier function.**

- NE binds **β₂-ARs** on breast cancer vascular endothelial cells → cyclic AMP / **protein kinase A (PKA)** signaling → upregulation of **vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF-A)** → endothelial proliferation, migration, and lumen formation. Simultaneously, **matrix metalloproteinase-9 (MMP-9)** is upregulated, degrading ECM to expand the vascular network.
- In glioblastoma, ACh activates **M1 mAChRs** on endothelial cells → phospholipase C / **IP3** → Ca²⁺ influx and tight junction disassembly (occludin, claudin-5 loss) → increased vascular permeability enabling tumor cell intravasation.
- **NGF** binds **TrkA** on non-small cell lung cancer brain metastasis endothelial cells → **PI3K/Akt** pathway → upregulation of tight junction proteins, paradoxically preserving blood–brain barrier (BBB) integrity at early metastatic stages; at late stages, NGF overexpression disrupts tight junctions, enabling tumor dissemination.
- Neurons engage bidirectional crosstalk with cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs) via the **Efna5–EphA5 ligand–receptor pair** in pancreatic cancer. Efna5 activation causes CAFs to secrete **VEGF-C**, promoting lymphatic vessel formation. Neuropeptide Y (NPY) from neurons induces platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF) production by CAFs, stabilizing new blood vessels by recruiting pericytes.
- **SLIT2** (a SLIT guidance molecule) is depicted in Figure 1 as a neuro–vascular regulatory factor.

**3. Neuro–Immune Crosstalk: nerves establish an immunosuppressive TME.**

- NE exerts multilayered immunosuppressive effects: (a) NE binds **β₂-ARs** on CD8⁺ T cells → cAMP / PKA → upregulates **PD-1** expression → T-cell exhaustion and reduced cytotoxicity; (b) NE promotes MDSCs to secrete **PD-L1**, creating a "double-negative" loop for anti-tumor immunity; (c) in glioblastoma, sympathetic nerve-derived NE enhances **PD-L1** on glioma cells via **β₃-ARs** while reducing PD-1-blockade-responsive T-cell infiltration.
- ACh modulates the PD-1/PD-L1 axis in colorectal cancer: cholinergic signaling via **α7-nicotinic receptors (α7-nAChR)** on tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) downregulates PD-L1 expression, partially reversing T-cell exhaustion — bidirectional and context-dependent.
- Cholinergic signaling drives **M2 macrophage polarization** → upregulated PD-L1 and cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated protein 4 (CTLA-4) ligands on T cells → immune checkpoint suppression (noted especially for glioblastoma).
- Substance P / TLR7 pathway (described above) recruits macrophages and neutrophils into tumors via an extracellular RNA–toll-like axis; within the TME this process is hijacked for pro-tumor growth rather than immune clearance.
- A higher-order systemic neural control of anti-tumor immunity is proposed: the nervous system can create an "immunological engram" in the brain, establishing a memory-like regulatory pattern that modifies subsequent immune function, extending neural influence from local TME to systemic long-term immune modulation.
- **GABA** (from cerebellar neurons, via the anterior cingulate cortex–glutamate / basolateral amygdala–glutamate circuit) and serotonin are listed as neuro-immune actors in Table 1; **TGM2** (transglutaminase 2) appears alongside CGRP, ACh, and GABA as neuro-immune key molecules.

## Relevance

**`H1` (hijack top-down neural circuitry) — directly supported.** The review provides extensive mechanistic grounding for `H1`. Sympathetic NE, cholinergic ACh, sensory substance P, and neurotrophic factors (NGF, BDNF) are all shown to promote tumor growth, invasion, angiogenesis, and immunosuppression through receptor-mediated programs. The paper explicitly frames peripheral nerves as a third major TME regulatory system. The "immunological engram" concept (Section 4.2) extends `H1` beyond local TME into systemic neural modulation of immune memory — the strongest form of `H1` in this review.

**`H2` (neural effects via immune modulation) — directly supported, and mechanistically entangled with `H1`.** The neuro–immune axis section (Section 4) demonstrates that neural signals remodel immune function at multiple levels: PD-1/PD-L1 axis (T-cell exhaustion via NE/β₂-AR/PKA), M2 macrophage polarization (cholinergic), MDSC recruitment and PD-L1 secretion, and neutrophil recruitment (substance P/TLR7). The PD-L1 upregulation by NE on glioma cells creates a direct mechanistic overlap between `H1` and `H2`: the same neural signal (NE) acts simultaneously on cancer cells (`H1`) and immune cells (`H2`). Distinguishing `H1` from `H2` in mutation-frequency data would require separating cancer-cell-intrinsic neural receptor expression from neural effects mediated entirely through immune intermediates.

**`H3` (aberrant developmental / oncofetal expression) — partial support.** The stage-dependent divergence of neurotrophic factor function (NGF promoting primary innervation vs. neurotrophin-3 governing lymph node crosstalk in melanoma; BDNF shifting from proliferative to colonization roles in late-stage SCLC) is consistent with oncofetal/developmental gene re-expression logic. However, the review does not directly address developmental gene re-expression; this connection remains speculative.

**`H4` (brain/CNS-cancer artifact) — not directly supported or refuted.** The review focuses on peripheral nerve innervation of non-CNS tumors and on glioblastoma. It does not address whether neural-gene enrichment in cBioPortal mutation data reflects a CNS-study artifact. However, the paper shows that peripheral tumors (prostate, breast, PDAC, colorectal) are heavily innervated and express functional neural receptors — which would predict neural-gene mutations in peripheral cancers independent of CNS-cancer contamination.

**`H5` (misannotation) — not addressed.** The paper works from named functional gene products (receptors, kinases, growth factors), not from chromosomal annotation.

**Key `H1`+`H2` implication for the cBioPortal project:** If genes like NKAIN2, KCNIP4, RBFOX1, LSAMP, OPCML etc. are somatically mutated in tumors that depend on neural signaling for growth, their mutation frequency may reflect positive selection for loss of neural-inhibitory signals (consistent with `H1`: losing a brake on neural-axis activation) rather than random gene-length confounding. This is testable: do these genes have documented roles as inhibitors/regulators of the neural-receptor programs described here? RBFOX1 (RNA splicing regulator of neuronal transcripts), LSAMP (neuronal adhesion molecule that inhibits receptor tyrosine kinase signaling), and OPCML (GPI-anchored tumor suppressor that inhibits receptor tyrosine kinases including FGFR, EphA2, HER2) are plausible candidates for neural-axis regulatory genes whose loss might enhance TME innervation signaling.

**Data-driven neural-gene definition implication:** This paper does not provide a transcriptomic or proteomic neural-gene list. However, its receptor/ligand catalog (β₂-AR/ADRB2, β₃-AR/ADRB3, M1/M3 mAChR/CHRM1/CHRM3, nAChR/CHRNA7, TACR1, TrkA/NTRK1, TrkB/NTRK2, TrkC/NTRK3, NGF, BDNF, NTF3, SLIT2, EFNA5, EPHA5, MMP9, VEGFA) constitutes a manually curated "neural signaling interface" gene set. Genes that are both (a) expressed in tumor cells and (b) encode neural receptors or neurotrophic factor signaling components constitute a functional definition distinct from anatomical origin.

## Limitations

- This is a commentary/narrative review, not a systematic review or meta-analysis; citation selection reflects the authors' emphasis and may be incomplete.
- The mechanistic evidence reviewed is predominantly from cell-line and mouse model experiments; clinical-grade causal evidence for most neural–TME interactions remains limited.
- The three-axis (nerve–tumor, neuro–vascular, neuro–immune) framework is conceptually useful but artificially separates processes that are deeply interdependent (e.g., NE simultaneously acts on cancer cells, endothelium, and T cells).
- Cancer-type coverage is uneven: glioblastoma, PDAC, prostate, breast, and melanoma dominate; hematologic malignancies and rare solid tumors are absent.
- The "immunological engram" concept (Section 4.2) is speculative and not supported by direct experimental evidence in the paper.
- No genomic or transcriptomic datasets are analyzed; the paper provides no data-driven neural-gene definition that could be imported directly into the cBioPortal project.
- Spatial and temporal heterogeneity of neural signaling within the TME is acknowledged as a challenge but not resolved; the review presents static snapshots of complex dynamic processes.
- Drug development section (Table 2) is largely early-phase; most agents lack efficacy readouts from randomized trials.

**Testable questions raised for the cBioPortal project:**

1. Do the specific neural-receptor genes catalogued here (ADRB2, ADRB3, CHRM1, CHRM3, CHRNA7, TACR1, NTRK1, NTRK2, NTRK3) show elevated somatic mutation frequency in cancer types where the review documents strong neural innervation (prostate, PDAC, breast, glioblastoma, colorectal, melanoma) relative to cancer types not discussed?

2. Is the enrichment of neural genes (NKAIN2, KCNIP4, RBFOX1, LSAMP, OPCML) in the "top mutated genes" list driven by gene-length confounding (`H4`/`H5`), or do these genes show mutation frequencies that exceed expectation given their coding lengths — consistent with positive selection (`H1`)?

3. LSAMP and OPCML are established tumor suppressors that inhibit receptor tyrosine kinase signaling; do their mutation frequencies co-vary with NTRK1/2/3 or VEGFA amplification across cancer types, as would be expected if they restrain the neural-axis TME programs described here?

4. Does the neuro–immune PD-L1 upregulation finding (NE → β₂-AR → PKA → PD-L1 in glioma; described in Section 4.1) predict that ADRB2/ADRB3-mutant tumors in cBioPortal also show elevated TMB or immunosuppressive gene signatures?

**Datasets / tools for label-free neural-gene definition:**
- Human Protein Atlas (HPA) neural expression specificity scores: genes with brain/neuron-specific RNA expression but also present in tumor cell lines are candidates for "ectopically expressed neural genes."
- PanglaoDB or CellMarker 2.0: neuron cell-type marker gene sets usable as a reference list for testing overlap with mutation-enriched genes.
- STRING/Reactome: protein interaction neighborhoods of ADRB2, NTRK1/2/3, CHRM3, NGF — expand via network diffusion to identify co-functional neural-axis genes.
- TCGA pan-cancer RPPA / RNA-seq: correlate neural receptor expression with PD-L1 / immune infiltrate scores (CYT, TIDE, GSVA immune signatures) to test the neuro–immune hypothesis computationally across cancer types.
