## Key Contribution

This community roadmap commentary, authored by 33 leading researchers who attended the 2024 MD Anderson Cancer Neuroscience Symposium and responded to a field-wide survey (n = 330 for one question; n = 188 for a sub-question), synthesizes the current state of cancer neuroscience and defines three strategic priority areas for the next phase of the field: **multidisciplinary collaboration**, **diversification** (of investigators, methods, and cancer types studied), and **training and career development**. The paper's structural argument is that cancer neuroscience has grown 22-fold in scholarly output (216 → 4,852 articles, 2000–2023) and now stands at an inflection point requiring cohesion and strategic investment rather than further fragmentation. It identifies the primary barriers to clinical translation — surveyed as: lack of fundamental knowledge (42.0% of respondents), lack of financial support (32.5%), and barriers to pharmacotherapeutic delivery (12.2%) — and lays out specific technology and methodology priorities for overcoming them. Critically for the project, the roadmap explicitly endorses **spatial and molecular targeting technologies** (viral vectors for retrograde neural circuit tracing, spatial omics, single-cell approaches, bispecific antibody-drug conjugates, bioelectronic methods) as the near-term methodological frontier. The paper also advocates for repurposing existing neuromodulatory drugs as a translational shortcut.

## Methods

This is a commentary / expert-survey roadmap (no original experimental data). Methods include:
- A field-wide survey administered to attendees of the 2024 MD Anderson Cancer Neuroscience Symposium; two survey questions are reported (n = 330; n = 188 for sub-question on barriers to clinical translation).
- Expert synthesis from 33 co-authors representing diverse institutions across North America, Europe, and Australia.
- Figure 1: Schematic of four cancer neuroscience research domains — (A) neural regulation of tumor onset/growth/progression; (B) tumor-derived substances influencing the nervous system; (C) cancer treatment impacts on neuronal function; (D) parallels between normal neural development and tumorigenesis.
- Figure 2: Bar graph + pie chart of survey responses on barriers to translation.

No datasets, code, or experimental models are generated or released.

## Key Findings

### Priority 1 — Multidisciplinary collaboration

1. **The primary translational bottleneck is lack of fundamental knowledge** (42.0% of survey respondents), not technological limitations. Cross-disciplinary integration and dual-expertise scientists are the identified remedies.

2. **The field needs a formal working group** embedded at cancer and neuroscience conferences and societies — comparable to existing sub-fields of cancer biology. This is a structural recommendation, not a scientific finding, but signals the community's recognition that ad-hoc collaboration is insufficient.

3. **Robust platforms for cross-institutional data and resource sharing** are called for explicitly. No specific platform is named, but the framing strongly implies the need for shared atlases, reference datasets, and federated cohort access — this is the closest the paper comes to endorsing a data-infrastructure play.

### Priority 2 — Diversification (methods, cancer types, geography)

4. **Technology priorities for selective neural targeting:** The paper identifies a specific set of technologies needed to advance mechanistic work and therapeutic specificity:
   - **Viral vectors injected into tumors to retrogradely label different CNS areas** — to map which brain circuits are connected to which tumor sites.
   - **Spatial and molecular targeting** using a combination of viral vector approach + spatial omics to understand tumor-innervating neurons versus normal-physiology neurons.
   - **Bispecific antibody-drug conjugates** for tumor-nerve junction specificity.
   - **Bioelectronic methods** to precisely manipulate, stimulate, or inhibit specific neuronal circuit components.
   - **Minimally invasive tools** to stimulate cancer-suppressing nerve pathways while blocking cancer-promoting ones.

5. **Drug repurposing is flagged as a near-term translational pathway.** Two examples are given:
   - **Lamotrigine** (first-line anti-epileptic) inhibited NF1-associated optic pathway glioma progression in preclinical mouse models [citing Pan et al. 2021, Nature Genetics].
   - **Propranolol** (sympathetic neuronal signaling blocker) inhibited cancer progression in a preclinical variety of cancers [citing Magnon et al. 2013, Science].
   - The systemic effects of repurposed neuromodulatory drugs need careful monitoring given pleiotropic CNS effects throughout the body.

6. **Glial cells are explicitly elevated as underappreciated actors** alongside neurons: they modify behavior of neurons, guide axonal growth within tumors, create invasive tracks, support angiogenesis, and regulate anti-tumor immune responses. The paper calls for methods that selectively target cancer-promoting neurons AND glia without disrupting physiological nervous system function.

7. **The brain-body axis and microbiome** are called out as emerging areas requiring investigation — microbial modulation of cancer-nerve interactions is cited as an area of active clinical trials (microbiome-based therapies in combination with neuromodulation [citing Villemin et al. 2023, Trends Immunol]).

8. **Neural stem cell migration from brain to peripheral tumor sites** is flagged as a discovery warranting further study [citing Mauffrey et al. 2019, Nature — CNS-derived progenitors migrate to prostate cancer via the bloodstream].

### Priority 3 — Training and career development

9. **Co-mentorship across cancer biology and neuroscience labs** emerged as the highest-rated training strategy in the survey (preferred by trainees; non-trainees preferred dual-discipline sequential training during graduate/post-doc stages). No specific program is named.

10. **Geographic diversity** is identified as a gap: the 2024 symposium drew mainly North American and European attendees with very limited representation from other world regions.

## Relevance

**H1 (tumors hijack top-down neural circuitry): IMPLICITLY SUPPORTED AS FIELD CONSENSUS.** The paper's framing of cancer neuroscience as studying tumors as "tissues fully integrated into the nervous system" (abstract) and its emphasis on mapping brain-to-tumor neural circuits via retrograde viral vectors both presuppose H1 as a foundational premise. The proposal to use viral vectors for circuit tracing is motivated precisely by the assumption that tumors receive and respond to descending CNS input.

**H2 (neural signal via immune modulation): SUPPORTED AS A CO-EQUAL RESEARCH DOMAIN.** The paper cites the neuro-immune axis explicitly (via Villemin et al. 2023 on microbiome-immune-nerve interactions and the role of Schwann cells in immune regulation [Deborde and Wong 2022]) and frames neuro-immune cross-talk as one of the key reasons for multidisciplinary collaboration. H2 is treated as inseparable from H1.

**H3 (oncofetal / developmental neural program re-expression): REFERENCED VIA FIGURE 1D.** The explicit inclusion of "parallels between normal neural development and the genesis of tumors" as a fourth research domain of cancer neuroscience (Figure 1D) directly validates H3 as a recognized mechanistic hypothesis in the field. Neural progenitor migration from CNS to prostate cancer (Mauffrey et al. 2019) is cited as an example. This is the paper's clearest endorsement that H3 deserves systematic investigation, not just mechanistic case studies.

**H4 (CNS/brain cancer artifact): NOT DIRECTLY ADDRESSED.** The paper does not discuss somatic mutation frequency or genomic database composition biases. It covers a broad range of cancer types (glioma, prostate, gastric, pancreatic, melanoma, oral cavity, breast) suggesting the field does not consider H4 an artifact issue in its mechanistic work. However, from the pipeline's perspective, the fact that glioma is the richest model system (most citations, most mechanistic detail) still supports H4 as an explanation for database-level enrichment of neural genes in mutation frequency tables.

**H5 (misannotation): WEAKENED.** The paper's central claim that tumors are functionally integrated into the nervous system implies that genes carrying "neural" annotations are legitimately neural in function within tumors, not misannotated. However, the paper also highlights that many neuromodulatory drugs have pleiotropic effects (arguing for careful monitoring), which indirectly acknowledges that "neural" molecules have functions beyond canonical neural signaling — a nuance consistent with H5 for some gene subsets.

**Data-driven / label-free neural-gene identification — MOST IMPORTANT SECTION FOR THE PROJECT:**

The paper does NOT itself provide a computational recipe for label-free neural-gene identification. However, it is the strongest current roadmap endorsement of the technologies that could enable such an approach:

1. **Retrograde viral vector tracing** (tumor → CNS) combined with spatial omics: if tumor-innervating neurons can be labeled and their transcriptomes profiled spatially, one can extract the gene programs that are shared between tumor-innervating neurons and cancer cells — providing a data-driven "neural-tumor interface" signature that does not depend on GO annotation.

2. **Single-cell transcriptomics of tumor-innervating nerve populations**: The paper implicitly endorses sc-RNAseq of the neural compartment of tumors as the route to identifying which neuronal cell types and gene programs are co-opted. The OPC/NPC transcriptional similarity of synaptically integrated glioma cells (cited in the paper via Venkatesh et al. 2019, Nature) is precisely the kind of reference-atlas-based label-free identification the project needs.

3. **Spatial omics** is explicitly named as a key technology for understanding cancer-nerve interactions in situ. This directly maps to the project's goal: if spatial transcriptomics datasets from public repositories (e.g., 10x Visium, MERFISH, Slide-seq data in the Human Cell Atlas or Allen Brain Cell Atlas) can resolve neural versus non-neural gene programs in tumor sections without relying on gene name annotations, one can define a "neural program score" for any tumor sample.

4. **The Allen Brain Atlas and Human Cell Atlas are implied reference frameworks** (not named explicitly, but the paper's call for cross-institutional data sharing and reference datasets maps onto these resources as the existing infrastructure).

## Limitations

- This is a community opinion / roadmap paper based on a single conference (2024 MD Anderson Cancer Neuroscience Symposium) whose attendees are self-selected for interest in the field; the survey responses may not represent the broader cancer biology or oncology communities.
- The survey sample sizes (n = 330, n = 188) are not large, and response categories shown in Figure 2 do not always sum visually cleanly — the pie-chart breakdown of the "fundamental knowledge" category is only partially legible in the published figure.
- The paper does not distinguish between CNS cancers and peripheral cancers in its priority recommendations; the field's mechanistic evidence is heavily glioma-centric, but the roadmap speaks as if priorities are uniform across cancer types.
- No specific computational tools, datasets, atlases, or analysis pipelines are named or released. The technology endorsements (spatial omics, viral vectors, bioelectronics) are aspirational rather than operational.
- The paper does not address somatic mutation frequency or genomic epidemiology — it is entirely focused on the mechanistic biology of cancer-nerve interactions and on research infrastructure/training. It cannot directly inform whether neural genes are enriched in mutation frequency tables due to gene-length confounding or true selection.
- Publication date is January 2025; some cited "emerging" tools (e.g., specific bioelectronic devices) may have advanced since then.

## Follow-up

- **Label-free neural program definition using spatial omics:** The paper's endorsement of spatial omics + viral vector tracing as the frontier method suggests that publicly available spatial transcriptomics data (10x Visium in GEO, MERFISH in Human Cell Atlas, Slide-seq data) for glioma, prostate cancer, and gastric cancer could be used NOW to extract co-expression modules that define "tumor-innervating neural programs" without gene name annotations. These modules could then be tested for overlap with the project's top-mutated gene list.

- **Schwann cell / OPC / NPC gene signatures as label-free neural references:** The paper's elevation of glial cells (Schwann cells, astrocytes, OPCs, microglia) as co-equal actors suggests extending the label-free definition beyond neurons. Published single-cell reference atlases (Allen Brain Cell Atlas, Human Cell Atlas, PanglaoDB) contain validated marker gene sets for each glial subtype. Enrichment of the project's top-mutated genes in these signatures (beyond length confounding) would objectively classify them as "neural program" genes.

- **NF1 and the lamotrigine connection:** NF1 is already in the Bailey 2018 driver gene list used by the pipeline. The paper's citation of Pan et al. 2021 (NF1 mutation drives neuronal activity-dependent optic glioma growth via NLGN3) provides a specific mechanistic chain: NF1 loss → elevated RAS → NLGN3 shedding → glioma growth. Cross-checking whether NF1 mutation frequency is systematically elevated in glioma studies in the pipeline's gene × cancer matrix would test whether this mechanism leaves a somatic mutation signature.

- **Propranolol / adrenergic receptor repurposing signal:** The citation of Magnon et al. 2013 (prostate cancer, sympathetic nerves) as a propranolol repurposing example invites testing ADRB1/ADRB2 somatic mutation frequency across prostate cancer studies in the pipeline. A deficit of ADRB mutations in prostate cancer (relative to expectation from gene length) could indicate positive selection pressure to *maintain* adrenergic signaling, rather than mutate it.

- **Survey-derived knowledge gap as pipeline motivation:** The field's self-identified #1 bottleneck — lack of fundamental knowledge about cancer-nerve biology (42% of survey) — maps directly onto what systematic somatic mutation analysis across cBioPortal studies could contribute: systematic genomic evidence for which neural-program genes are under somatic selection across cancer types, providing a complementary data layer to mechanistic experimental studies.

- **Mauffrey 2019 and H3 (oncofetal neural progenitor migration):** If CNS-derived neural progenitors migrate to peripheral tumor sites, those cells would carry neural gene expression programs in non-CNS cancer contexts. This could explain why "neural" genes appear mutated/expressed in pancreatic or prostate cancers: the cells expressing them are neural immigrants, not cancer cells with aberrant expression. This is a form of H3 that is distinct from simple oncofetal reactivation and deserves explicit testing in the pipeline via immune cell deconvolution (can neural progenitor markers be detected in non-CNS tumor RNA-seq?).

- **Brain-body axis / microbiome-nerve connection:** The paper's call for investigation of microbiome-nerve-cancer interactions is a research frontier without clear genomic correlates yet. Lower priority for the current pipeline, but worth noting for future covariate annotation in studies with matched microbiome data.
