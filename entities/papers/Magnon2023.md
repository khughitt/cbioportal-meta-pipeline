# The neural addiction of cancer

- **Authors:** Claire Magnon, Hubert Hondermarck
- **Year:** 2023
- **Journal:** Nature Reviews Cancer, Volume 23, May 2023, pp. 317-334
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41568-023-00556-8
- **BibTeX key:** Magnon2023
- **Source:** PDF

## Key Contribution

This Perspective synthesizes the emerging field of cancer neuroscience, arguing that peripheral and central nervous system circuitries are not incidental bystanders but active drivers of tumor initiation, growth, and metastasis. The paper introduces the concept of the "neural addiction of cancer" — the progressive co-option of neural signaling pathways by tumors — and organizes the bidirectional crosstalk between the nervous system and solid tumors into two major axes: (1) how specific brain areas and efferent neuroendocrine pathways (HPA axis, sympatho-adrenal system, circadian circuitry in the SCN) regulate peripheral tumor development, and (2) how peripheral tumors feed back to the brain via adipokines, neurotrophic factors, pro-inflammatory cytokines, and afferent sensory neurons. The paper also covers two tumor-autonomous mechanisms: axonogenesis (tumors building their own autonomic nerve networks from pre-existing fibers) and tumor neurogenesis (neural progenitors from the SVZ differentiating into adrenergic neo-nerves within the TME). It closes by cataloging ongoing clinical trials of repurposed neuropsychiatric drugs (beta-blockers, antidepressants, anti-cholinergics, antiepileptics) as a translational frontier. This is a conceptual review; it does not present new primary data.

This perspective is interpreted together with paper:Mancusi2023, paper:Venkatesh2019, paper:Hwang2025, paper:Kulke2012, and paper:Xiong2023 when separating canonical cancer-neuroscience mechanisms from gene-length or cohort-composition artifacts.

## Methods

This is a Perspective article; no new experimental data are generated. The paper synthesizes preclinical (rodent orthotopic and xenograft models, genetic manipulation of autonomic nerves, surgical sympathectomy/vagotomy) and clinical (epidemiological cohort studies, phase I/II clinical trials) evidence published through approximately early 2023. Key evidence types cited include:

- Mouse models of prostate, gastric, pancreatic, breast, skin, and lung cancer with surgical or genetic nerve ablation.
- Orthotopic xenograft models for breast, ovarian, colon, and prostate cancer testing autonomic pharmacological blockade.
- Epidemiological retrospective cohort analyses of beta-blocker use and cancer survival.
- Immunohistochemical profiling of nerve fiber density in human tumor biopsies.
- Circulating biomarker studies (glucocorticoids, catecholamines, adipokines, neurotrophic factors, pro-inflammatory cytokines) in cancer patients.
- Clinical trials data from ClinicalTrials.gov for neuropsychiatric drug repurposing (Table 1 in the paper).

No genomic or transcriptomic datasets are produced or reanalyzed. No statistical methods are applied by the authors directly; evidence is qualitatively synthesized.

## Key Findings

1. **Bidirectional tumor-nervous system crosstalk operates through six distinguishable channels** (Fig. 1 in the paper): (1) central regulation via brain areas, (2) HPA axis (hypothalamus-pituitary-adrenal), (3) sympatho-adrenal system (SAS), (4) tumor axonogenesis, (5) tumor neurogenesis, and (6) afferent nerve/humoral signals returning to the brain.

2. **Specific brain regions are implicated as tumor-supporting hubs.** The paraventricular nucleus (PVN) of the hypothalamus regulates glucocorticoid release; the sympatho-adrenal splanchnic nerve drives catecholamine secretion; the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) controls circadian rhythms linked to cancer incidence; the subventricular zone (SVZ) supplies DCX+ neural progenitors that migrate to peripheral prostate tumors; and the lateral hypothalamus (LH) orexinergic neurons modulate sleep and cancer-associated metabolic changes.

3. **HPA-axis glucocorticoids promote metastasis and immune suppression.** Glucocorticoids bind glucocorticoid receptors on cancer and stromal cells, promoting proliferation, dissemination, thymic involution, and lymphocyte depletion. Activated by chronic stress or by afferent tumor signals, the HPA axis represents a systemic brake on antitumor immunity.

4. **Adrenergic signaling (epinephrine/norepinephrine) drives multiple pro-tumorigenic programs via beta-adrenergic receptors (ADRB2, ADRB3).** Downstream effects include: direct oncogene expression, proliferation, invasion, inhibition of DNA repair, survival, EMT; recruitment of tumor-associated macrophages; promotion of myeloid-derived suppressor cells; angiogenesis (via VEGFA, FGF2, IL-6, IL-8); immune checkpoint upregulation; and cancer-associated fibroblast activation. Evidence spans prostate, breast, pancreatic, neuroblastoma, melanoma, and nasopharyngeal cancers.

5. **Cholinergic (muscarinic) signaling has a cancer-type-specific role.** Parasympathetic (cholinergic) fibers also innervate tumors; muscarinic receptors CHRM1/CHRM3 control dissemination after binding acetylcholine. In prostate and gastric cancers, cholinergic signaling promotes tumorigenesis; in pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma, blocking cholinergic input has the opposite effect. A Phase II clinical trial of the selective CHRM3 inhibitor solifenacin is underway.

6. **Tumors actively build their own autonomic nerve networks via axonogenesis** — the dynamic outgrowth of pre-existing autonomic/cholinergic nerve fibers driven by overexpression of neurotrophic factors (NGF, BDNF) in tumor and stromal cells. This has been demonstrated across gastric, pancreatic, breast, skin, prostate, and lung cancers.

7. **Tumor neurogenesis supplements axonogenesis**: neural progenitor cells expressing doublecortin (DCX+) leave the SVZ of the brain, cross the blood-brain barrier, circulate, and differentiate into adrenergic neo-nerves inside peripheral tumors (demonstrated in prostate and breast primary tumors and metastases). DCX+ progenitor density in human prostate biopsies positively correlates with aggressiveness, invasiveness, and recurrence.

8. **Peripheral tumors alter brain function via afferent humoral and neural signals.** Cancer-associated adipokines (leptin, adiponectin, ghrelin), neurotrophic factors (CNTF, BDNF), and pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β, IL-2, IL-4, IL-6, IL-8) enter the bloodstream and modulate activity in the PFC, LH orexinergic neurons, VTA dopaminergic neurons, and hippocampus — providing mechanistic grounding for cancer-associated cognitive impairment and neuropsychiatric symptoms independent of chemotherapy.

9. **Circadian disruption (SCN dysregulation) is linked to cancer incidence and progression.** Clock genes PERI, PER2, CRY1, CRY2, and BMAL1 are dysregulated in cancer, and night-shift work is classified by IARC as a probable carcinogen. SCN-mediated control of melatonin secretion from the pineal gland, and melatonin's inhibitory effects on innate and adaptive immunity, provide a neuroendocrine mechanism.

10. **Named key genes and receptors.** Recurring molecular actors include: NGF, BDNF, CNTF (neurotrophins); ADRB2, ADRB3 (adrenergic receptors); CHRM1, CHRM3 (muscarinic receptors); VEGFA, FGF2 (angiogenic factors); NF1, COL1A2 (neurofibromatosis/Schwann cell biology); MYC (oncogene activated by adrenergic signaling); GLUT1, VGAT (glutamate/GABA transporters in brain tumors); HCRT/HCRT1/HCRT2 (orexin/hypocretin in LH); DCX (neural progenitor marker); P2RY14 (purinergic receptor in Schwann cells); CGRP (sensory nociception); PERI, PER2, CRY1, CRY2, BMAL1 (circadian clock genes).

11. **Clinical trial landscape.** Table 1 catalogs active phase I/II trials of: beta-blockers (carvedilol, propranolol) in prostate, glioblastoma, melanoma, pancreatic, breast, colon/rectal cancer; antidepressants (imipramine, SSRIs) in breast cancer; antiepileptics (valproate) in neuroendocrine tumors and colorectal cancer; anti-cholinergics (solifenacin) in breast cancer; and anti-NGF antibody tanezumab for cancer pain.

## Relevance

**Bearing on local hypotheses `H1`-`H5` for the cbioportal neural-gene enrichment observation:**

**`H1` (tumors hijack top-down neural circuitry — active selection for neural genes):** This paper provides the strongest mechanistic support for `H1` of any single review to date. The evidence for active adrenergic (ADRB2/ADRB3), cholinergic (CHRM1/CHRM3), and neurotrophic (NGF, BDNF) signaling directly driving proliferation, invasion, and immune evasion across multiple cancer types is extensive. The axonogenesis and tumor neurogenesis findings show tumors actively remodel their neural microenvironment, consistent with positive selection for cells that respond to neural signals. If genes like NKAIN2, KCNIP4, RIT2, CALN1, or RBFOX1 encode proteins functional in these neural-tumoral signaling loops, `H1` predicts they should be mutated at elevated frequency (gain- or loss-of-function depending on signaling polarity). The paper does not resolve `H1` definitively because it reviews pharmacological and ablation evidence, not somatic mutation data, but it strongly motivates the hypothesis.

**`H2` (neural enrichment is mediated primarily through immune modulation):** Partially supported as a mechanism downstream of `H1` rather than an alternative. The paper documents adrenergic suppression of CD8+ T cells and enhancement of MDSCs, cholinergic modulation of tumor dissemination, and HPA-glucocorticoid thymic involution as immune effectors of neural signaling. `H2` is not cleanly separable from `H1` in this framework — autonomic signaling simultaneously drives cancer-cell-intrinsic programs and immunosuppression. For the mutation-frequency question, `H2` alone would predict enrichment of immune-modulatory genes rather than canonical neural genes.

**`H3` (oncofetal/developmental re-expression artifact):** The tumor neurogenesis section (SVZ progenitors differentiating into adrenergic neurons within the TME) is the most relevant finding for `H3`. It raises the possibility that developmentally regulated neural genes active in neural progenitors are re-expressed in tumors that have recruited or mimicked neural progenitor fates. This is distinct from `H3` as originally framed (transcriptional noise), but it does suggest that a class of "neural" somatic mutations could reflect selection in a developmental rather than fully differentiated neural context. The paper does not present transcriptomic evidence and cannot confirm or refute whether the neural gene enrichment in somatic mutations reflects this pathway.

**`H4` (artifact of CNS/brain cancers dominating the cohort):** The paper actually argues against an exclusively brain-cancer-centered interpretation by presenting extensive evidence across prostate, breast, gastric, pancreatic, skin, lung, and ovarian cancers, all non-CNS tumors. Brain tumors (glioblastoma, medulloblastoma) are discussed only peripherally (glutamate/GABA signaling, olfactory neurons in glioma). This suggests the "neural gene" observation in the cbioportal cohort may not require a brain-cancer-dominated cohort to be real — peripheral cancers also engage neural gene products. That said, the paper does not address the gene-length confound that is the primary technical concern for `H4`.

**`H5` (misannotation):** Not directly relevant. The paper uses standard gene names and neural pathway annotations consistent with GO/UniProt, so it neither resolves nor worsens the misannotation risk.

**Critical limitation for this project's context:** This paper is entirely about neural signaling at the protein/pathway level (receptors, ligands, circuits). It does not address whether neural genes are somatically mutated at elevated frequency, does not present any mutation frequency data, and does not provide a data-driven definition of "neural gene" from expression atlases. It therefore cannot directly adjudicate `H4` (gene-length artifact) or resolve the technical cbioportal enrichment question. Its value is in establishing the mechanistic prior for `H1` and `H2` and providing a curated gene list of functionally confirmed neural-cancer interface molecules.

**On label-free neural gene definition:** The paper offers no computational framework for defining neural genes without human labels. It does, however, point to expression-based criteria implicitly: the neural genes most relevant to cancer (per this review) are those with demonstrated expression in the TME or in circulating neural progenitors. The DCX+ SVZ progenitor finding suggests that developmental atlas expression (Human Cell Atlas, Allen Brain Atlas, single-cell fetal atlases) of neural progenitor marker genes might provide one label-free axis for defining "neural" relevance to the somatic mutation question.

## Limitations

- This is a narrative Perspective, not a systematic review or meta-analysis; study selection is not exhaustive and may reflect author emphasis on supporting examples over contradictory ones.
- No primary genomic or transcriptomic data are presented. The review cannot address whether neural genes are somatically mutated at elevated frequency or whether such enrichment is gene-length-confounded.
- The paper does not define "neural gene" computationally. Its gene lists are curated by domain expertise (pharmacological targets, receptor families), not derived from expression atlases or orthogonal data sources.
- Evidence quality is heterogeneous: some claims rest on in vitro cell-line data or single mouse model results; others on retrospective epidemiological studies with confounding factors (e.g., beta-blocker use as a proxy for cardiovascular disease comorbidity).
- Many cited studies are in prostate, breast, and gastric cancer; generalizability to other histologies in the cbioportal cohort (colorectal, lung, bladder, etc.) is stated but not uniformly evidenced.
- The paper conflates correlation (nerve density in tumors) with causation (nerve signaling drives tumor growth); causal evidence is available for some cancer types (e.g., prostate, gastric via surgical nerve ablation) but not all.
- The specific genes dominating the project's top-mutated-gene list (NKAIN2, KCNIP4, TAFA2/FAM19A2, RIT2, CALN1, RBFOX1, LSAMP, SGCZ, OPCML) are not mentioned anywhere in this review, suggesting they are not the canonical cancer-neuroscience effectors this field has focused on. This either means (a) they are functionally upstream/downstream of the known pathway, (b) they reflect gene-length artifact, or (c) they represent a distinct biology the cancer neuroscience field has not examined.

## Follow-up

- **Resolve the gene-length confound first.** The genes topping the project's mutation table (NKAIN2: ~1.3 Mb genomic span; KCNIP4: ~1.1 Mb; RBFOX1: ~2.5 Mb) are ultralong genes with high baseline mutation probability. This paper's framework provides mechanistic motivation for `H1` but cannot distinguish signal from length-artifact. Apply gene-length normalization (e.g., mutations-per-kb or dNdS correction) before evaluating whether the project's gene list represents genuine neural enrichment.

- **Build a data-driven "neural gene" reference set from expression atlases.** This paper curates neural genes by pharmacological/functional criteria. For a label-free definition, cross the Human Cell Atlas (single-cell RNA-seq, neural vs. non-neural cell types) and the Allen Brain Atlas developmental expression data against the top-ranked genes in the project mutation tables. Genes that are neural by expression but not by prior literature annotation could resolve whether NKAIN2, KCNIP4, RBFOX1, etc. belong to the functional cancer-neuroscience circuit.

- **Test whether the top neural-appearing genes are enriched in cancer types known to be highly innervated.** This paper establishes that prostate, gastric, pancreatic, and breast cancers are the best-documented cases of neural co-option. If the project's cohort is dominated by these histologies, `H4` (cohort artifact) should be re-evaluated against `H1`. Stratify mutation frequency by cancer type and examine whether the neural gene enrichment is concentrated in highly-innervated cancer types.

- **Examine ADRB2, ADRB3, CHRM1, CHRM3, NGF, BDNF in the project's mutation tables.** The canonical cancer-neuroscience genes named in this review should appear in the cross-study tables; check their mutation frequencies and whether they are elevated relative to gene-length-matched controls. If they are not enriched, this suggests the project's top neural genes are not reflecting the same biology described here.

- **CGRP-sensory neuron axis as a testable `H2` variant.** The paper describes CGRP+ sensory neurons in the parabrachial nucleus as key mediators of cancer-induced anorexia and pain, and notes that CGRP inhibition reduces tumor growth in Lewis lung carcinoma. If immune-modulatory sensory genes (CGRP pathway) appear in the mutation tables, this supports a neuroimmune (`H2`) rather than purely autonomic (`H1`) mechanism.

- **Consider Schwann cells and NF1 as a bridging biology.** Box 1 of the paper documents that NF1 mutations drive neuronal hyperexcitability and tumor-promoting paracrine signaling. NF1 is a large gene (~350 kb) and a known cancer driver on the Bailey et al. [@Bailey2018] list. Its presence in any top-mutated gene list would not be a neural artifact but a genuine tumor suppressor signal; checking NF1's rank in the project tables would clarify whether the neural gene signal mixes driver biology with long-gene artifact.
