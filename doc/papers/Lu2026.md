## Key Contribution

A comprehensive narrative review synthesising the cellular and molecular biology of perineural invasion (PNI) — the process by which tumour cells invade into, around, and through peripheral nerves — with direct therapeutic implications. The review frames PNI as an active, bidirectional crosstalk between tumour cells and the neural microenvironment (not merely passive spread along nerves) and organises the field across four axes: (1) clinical detection methods and prognostic biomarkers, (2) cellular and molecular mechanisms driving PNI-related cancer hallmarks, (3) emerging models and technologies for studying PNI, and (4) therapeutic strategies targeting tumour-nerve interactions. A key conceptual contribution is cataloguing PNI biomarkers into seven mechanistic classes and enumerating the axon-guidance molecule axis as a distinct, named molecular axis driving PNI. The review is notable for its explicit treatment of axon-guidance genes — including SEMA3A/Plexin-B1, SLIT2/ROBO, L1CAM, and NCAM — alongside neurotrophic factor pathways (NGF/TrkA, GDNF/RET, BDNF/TrkB, Artemin), providing a named gene list directly relevant to disentangling neural gene mutation patterns in pan-cancer somatic datasets.

## Methods

This is a narrative review (no primary data). The authors conducted a systematic literature survey of PNI research covering molecular biology, clinical pathology, and translational cancer neuroscience. Evidence is organised into:

- **Biomarker taxonomy** (Table 1): 7 classes of PNI biomarkers catalogued with mechanism, specific molecule, and cancer type.
- **Cancer-type incidence table** (Table 2): PNI incidence, prognostic significance, staging/grading systems, and detection methods summarised for cervical cancer (14.8%), colorectal cancer (9%–33%), prostate cancer (25%–90%), head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (2.5%–14%), pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (100%), and gastric cancer (6.8%–75.6%).
- **Figure timeline** (Figure 1): Milestone advances in PNI research 1835–2025.
- **Cellular interaction diagram** (Figure 2): Schwann cell, tumour-associated macrophage (TAM), cancer-associated fibroblast (CAF), immune cell, and ECM contributions to PNI.
- **Molecular signalling diagram** (Figure 3): Neurotrophic factors, neurotransmitters, and axon-guidance molecules mediating tumour-nerve crosstalk.
- **Cancer hallmarks diagram** (Figure 4): Five PNI-driven hallmarks (proliferation, invasion/metastasis, immune evasion, metabolic reprogramming, cancer pain).

No statistical analyses, cohort data, or bioinformatic analyses are performed by the authors themselves. Evidence quality across cited studies is not formally graded.

## Key Findings

### PNI is a bidirectional neural niche, not passive spread

PNI is reframed as a process in which tumour cells and host cells (Schwann cells, TAMs, CAFs, immune cells) collectively construct a specialised "tumour-nerve bidirectional crosstalk network." Schwann cells are the central cellular mediators: they are "co-opted" by cancer cells, migrate toward tumours before PNI occurs, secrete NGF and GDNF, form tumour-activated Schwann cell tracks (TAST), and activate L1CAM and RAC2 GTPase to promote invasion. The nerve is simultaneously reshaped by the tumour (axonogenesis driven by tumour-secreted factors) and exploited as a migration highway.

### Named neurotrophic factor pathways

- **NGF / TrkA (NTRK1) / p75NTR:** NGF secreted by cancer cells binds TrkA on tumour cells (promoting PNI via Ras-MAPK and MAPK-Erk) and p75NTR (inhibitory effect). NGF also acts on nerve cells to induce neurite outgrowth, providing a migration path. NGF is a key driver in pancreatic cancer (PC), oral squamous cell carcinoma (OSCC), and colorectal cancer (CRC). Over-expressed NGF associates with poor prognosis in cervical cancer.
- **GDNF / GFRα1 / RET:** GDNF secreted by cancer cells enhances tumour migration and invasion via GFRα1-RET signalling; GDNF-induced MUC21 phosphorylation promotes PC perineural infiltration; the GFRA2-RET axis (activated by Neurturin) potentiates glycolysis in PC; GDNF/RET drives PNI in both PC and prostate cancer.
- **BDNF / TrkB (NTRK2):** BDNF-TrkB signalling drives EMT, proliferation, migration, and anoikis resistance, particularly in prostate cancer. BDNF also enhances migration via VEGF/HO-1/ERK/PI3K/Akt pathways and is relevant to salivary gland adenoid cystic carcinoma (SACC).
- **NT3 / TrkC (NTRK3):** NT3 binds TrkC to promote PNI in PDAC.
- **Artemin (GDNF family):** Artemin enhances cancer cell invasiveness in PC and CCA; promotes TRPV1 function at lower doses than NGF.

### Named axon-guidance molecules (most directly relevant to H5/length-confound analysis)

- **Semaphorin 3A (SEMA3A) / Plexin-B1:** SEMA3A secreted by cancer cells promotes PNI by inducing axons to grow toward the tumour. SEMA3D / PLXND1 combination enhances migration and invasion of pancreatic cancer cells and promotes axon regeneration. Semaphorin 4D / Plexin-B1 promotes tumour cell proliferation and migration in squamous cell carcinoma (SCC).
- **SLIT2 / ROBO:** The SLIT2-ROBO pathway can inhibit PNI of PC, providing a tumour-suppressive role. In gastric cancer, cancer cells secrete sEV-circVAPA to inhibit SLIT2 translation, thereby reducing SLIT2 expression in nerve cells, ultimately inducing neuro-invasion. SLIT2 expressed by CAFs affects PNI in PDAC by influencing nerve remodelling. This bidirectional (pro- and anti-PNI) role of SLIT/ROBO makes its mutational status in somatic datasets ambiguous.
- **L1CAM (L1 cell adhesion molecule):** L1CAM is a critical mediator of SC-driven perineural infiltration. Schwann cells secrete L1CAM to activate MAPK signalling in tumour cells; L1CAM induces perineural invasion of PC by upregulating matrix metalloproteinases; L1CAM promotes melanoma progression; the enteric nervous system guides tumour cell migration through L1CAM and N-cadherin. L1CAM is upregulated in nerves with prostate cancer invasion.
- **NCAM (neural cell adhesion molecule):** NCAM reduces cancer cell aggregation and increases migration, thereby promoting PNI in PCa and PDAC. NCAM is listed as a neuro-tumour microenvironment biomarker for PNI.
- **GAP43:** GAP43 is a marker for neuronal outgrowth and regeneration; used as a neural structure biomarker for PNI detection in squamous cell carcinoma.

### Named chemokine axes

- **CXCL12 / CXCR4:** Stromal-derived CXCL12 chemotaxis promotes perineural invasion in PC; ApoD promotes axonal growth from DRG in a CXCR4-dependent manner in SACC. CXCL12-CXCR4 is one of the most extensively cited PNI-promoting chemokine axes.
- **CCL2 / CCR2, CX3CR1, CCL5 / CCR5, CXCR5-CXCL13, CCL7-CCR2 / STAT3:** Multiple chemokine axes promote cancer cell nerve invasion (Table 1, CRC, SACC, PDAC, PCa, PC).
- **CXCL5/CXCR2 and PI3K/AKT/GSK-3β/Snail-Twist:** Mediates Schwann cell EMT contribution to lung cancer PNI.

### Other named molecular mediators

- **MMP-9, MMP-13, MMP-2:** Matrix metalloproteinases promote PNI via ECM remodelling; GDNF induces MMP secretion; L1CAM upregulates MMPs in PC; MMPs are promoted by tumour-activated Schwann cells.
- **TGF-β:** Induces EMT, tumour cell proliferation, axon and myelin sheath formation; TGFBI Schwann cell subpopulation promotes neural invasion in PC; CAFs promote EMT via TGF-β.
- **Substance P / NK-1R:** Promotes tumour cell proliferation, invasion, and PNI in PC.
- **CGRP (calcitonin gene-related peptide):** Promotes proliferation and migration of cancer cells in OSCC via inhibition of tumour-infiltrating lymphocytes.
- **PlGF / sFlt1:** Drives PNI in PDAC by promoting neurite outgrowth and bidirectional chemotaxis.
- **Claudin 1:** Overexpression enhances invasiveness in OSCC.
- **ICAM-5 (telencephalin):** High expression associated with PNI in HNSCC.
- **TIMP1 / CCL7:** PDAC-derived TIMP1 promotes SC proliferation/migration; SC-secreted CCL7 enhances PDAC migration in a paracrine loop.
- **ANGPTL4 / AMACR:** Metabolic reprogramming markers associated with PNI.
- **Notch (Notch1, Notch4):** Notch signalling closely related to PNI in OSCC and SACC.
- **MAGEA6:** High expression associated with PNI in CRC via YY1-dependent transcription.
- **Synuclein-γ:** Closely related to PNI in PC (novel prognostic marker).
- **ApoD:** Promotes axonal DRG growth in CXCR4-dependent manner in SACC, inducing neuronal-like tumour cell transformation.
- **NAT10:** Promotes tumour cell migration, invasion, and PNI by enhancing ITGB5 mRNA stability and activating ITGB5-pFAK-pSrc signalling in PDAC.
- **PLAU (plasminogen activator urokinase):** Prognostic biomarker for PNI in HNSCC.

### Cancer types where PNI is most prevalent and studied

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) has 100% PNI incidence and is the most intensively studied; prostate cancer (25%–90%) and head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (2.5%–14%) are major foci. Colorectal (9%–33%), gastric (6.8%–75.6%), and cervical cancers (14.8%) also have substantial PNI literature. PNI is an independent poor-prognosis factor in all of these except possibly cervical and gastric cancer, where evidence is mixed.

### PNI-driven cancer hallmarks

The review organises five PNI-driven cancer hallmarks: (1) malignant proliferation of tumour cells (via NGF-TrkA, BDNF-TrkB, neurotransmitters, tumour angiogenesis), (2) invasion and metastasis (PNI as a fourth mode of metastasis alongside peritoneal, haematogenous, and lymphatic dissemination), (3) immune evasion (tumour-associated nerves degrade myelin sheath of TANs; 5-HT downregulates MHC-I; cholinergic signalling reshapes immune TME), (4) metabolic reprogramming (ANGPTL4 via PI3K-AKT; GFRA2-RET via glycolysis; glutamate from nerve cells via m6A epigenetic modifications), and (5) cancer pain (NGF/GDNF mediate chronic pain; TRPA1 in SCs; neuro-inflammation).

### Emerging technologies

A pan-cancer PNI transcriptomic classifier trained on 2,029 patients across 12 cancer types can detect occult PNI from RNA-seq data, improving histopathological detection. Trace-n-Seq enables retrograde neuronal tracing with single-cell resolution. A proposed 3D microfluidic organ-on-chip model integrates neural and tumour microenvironments. AI/ML models for PNI detection in PDAC achieve up to 81% accuracy. Dynamic optical coherence tomography (DOCT) claims 100% accuracy for non-invasive intraoperative PNI detection.

## Relevance

### Bearing on H1–H5 (neural gene mutation patterns in cbioportal top-mutated list)

This review is primarily relevant to distinguishing **H5 (gene-length confound / misannotation)** from **H1 (hijack top-down neural circuitry)** via the following reasoning:

**H5 / length-confound is the most parsimonious explanation for observing axon-guidance and neural cell-adhesion genes at the top of a mutation-frequency list.** The genes most extensively implicated in PNI — L1CAM, NCAM, SEMA3A, SLIT2, ROBO1/2, PLXND1, CDH2 (N-cadherin), MMP-9/13 — are uniformly large genes. The review confirms that L1CAM, NCAM, SLIT2/ROBO, and SEMA3A/PLXND1 are genuine, extensively replicated cancer-biology actors, but their biological relevance in PNI does NOT resolve whether their apparent mutation prevalence in a cross-study aggregation reflects driver selection or simply greater length-weighted target area. The review provides no mutation-frequency data that would allow disentanglement of these hypotheses.

**H1 (neural-circuit hijacking sensu stricto)** is partially supported: tumour cells exploit neurotrophic signalling (NGF, GDNF, BDNF) and co-opt Schwann cells to create a pro-invasive neural niche. However, the genes most prominently driving this (NTRK1/NGF, RET/GDNF, NTRK2/BDNF) are not flagged as the top-mutated genes in the user's observed list, which contains NKAIN2, KCNIP4, TAFA2, RIT2, CALN1, RBFOX1, LSAMP, SGCZ, OPCML. Among these, **LSAMP** and **OPCML** are notably addressed by this review as cell-adhesion / GPI-anchored molecules within the axon-guidance / neural cell-adhesion class. LSAMP (limbic system-associated membrane protein) and OPCML (opioid-binding protein/cell-adhesion molecule-like) are both GPI-anchored IgCAM superfamily members, structurally related to L1CAM and NCAM. They are large genes and are known tumour suppressors in some contexts. Their presence at the top of the mutation list is more consistent with H5 (large-gene length confound) or H4 (CNS-cancer artifact from brain metastasis-enriched studies) than with H1 (active perineural invasion drivers), because neither LSAMP nor OPCML is cited by this review as a PNI mediator.

**H2 (immune modulation)** is tangentially supported: PNI drives immune evasion via 5-HT/MHC-I suppression, TAM M2 polarisation, and cholinergic signalling reshaping the immune TME. However, the specific top-mutated genes (NKAIN2, KCNIP4, RBFOX1) are not implicated.

**H3 (oncofetal / developmental re-expression):** The review describes tumour cells undergoing "neuronal-like transformation" (ApoD in SACC), but this is a relatively minor mechanistic thread and not specifically connected to the genes in the user's list.

**H4 (brain/CNS-cancer artifact):** The review focuses on peripheral nervous system (PNS) invasion; central nervous system biology is not a focus. LSAMP and OPCML are highly expressed in brain tissue and are frequently mutated in brain tumours — their appearance in a pan-cancer top-mutated list may reflect overrepresentation of brain/CNS cancer studies rather than a general PNI mechanism.

**Critical implication for the pipeline:** The axon-guidance genes named in this review (SLIT2, ROBO1/2, SEMA3A/3D, PLXND1, L1CAM, NCAM) have known biological roles in PNI but are extremely large genes. Any analysis that does not length-normalise mutation counts will systematically overstate their apparent mutation frequency. The overlap between the user's observed top-mutated list and the neural/axon-guidance gene universe described here is therefore expected under H5 and does not by itself constitute evidence for H1.

### Toward a data-driven definition of "neural genes"

This review does not provide a data-driven gene set definition. However, it implicitly defines three functional categories of neural genes relevant to PNI:

1. **Neurotrophic receptor / ligand genes** (mechanistically validated, moderate size): NTRK1 (TrkA), NTRK2 (TrkB), NTRK3 (TrkC), RET, GFRA1, GFRA2, NGF, GDNF, BDNF, NTF3 (NT3), ARTN (Artemin).
2. **Axon-guidance and neural cell-adhesion molecules** (mechanistically validated, large genes): SEMA3A, SEMA3D, PLXNB1, PLXND1, SLIT2, ROBO1, ROBO2, L1CAM, NCAM1, CDH2, GAP43, LSAMP [UNVERIFIED as explicit PNI actor in this paper], OPCML [UNVERIFIED].
3. **Peripheral neural structure markers** (detection utility, not necessarily driver): S100B, PGP9.5, neurofilament (NEFL/NEFM/NEFH), NeuN (RBFOX3), GAP43.

A genuinely data-driven definition (e.g., genes whose expression correlates with neural infiltration score across TCGA tumours, or genes upregulated at tumour-nerve interfaces by single-cell RNA-seq) would require analysis of datasets such as the Trace-n-Seq data or the Chen et al. 2025 Cancer Cell integrated single-cell/spatial transcriptomics study of pancreatic cancer neural invasion (cited as ref [28] in this review).

## Limitations

- As a narrative review, no systematic literature search protocol, PRISMA statement, or formal evidence grading is provided. The breadth of the coverage (~295 references) means that selectivity and framing biases cannot be assessed.
- The majority of cited mechanistic studies use cell lines or mouse models; direct human in vivo evidence is more limited and largely observational (biopsy series, retrospective cohorts).
- The review conflates very different cancer types as if PNI mechanisms are uniform; the molecular axes driving PNI in PDAC (GDNF/RET, CXCL12/CXCR4) differ substantially from those in HNSCC or prostate cancer.
- Axon-guidance genes (SLIT2/ROBO, SEMA3A/Plexin) are described with both pro-PNI and anti-PNI roles in different contexts; the review does not resolve this context-dependence systematically.
- No mutation frequency or somatic mutation data are presented; the review cannot distinguish genes that are mutated in PNI-active tumours vs genes whose expression level is altered.
- The review does not provide or cite a canonical, data-driven "neural gene" list or gene set resource that could be used for length-normalisation or enrichment testing.
- Therapeutic strategies described (CAR-T, nano-drug delivery, Trace-n-Seq) are largely preclinical or conceptual.

## Model / Tool Availability

No new tool or dataset is released by the authors. The following external tools and data resources are cited and relevant:

- **Trace-n-Seq** (Chen et al. Cancer Cell 2025): Single-cell + spatial transcriptomic retrograde neuronal tracing in pancreatic cancer — the most relevant dataset for defining a neural gene signature from data.
- **Pan-cancer PNI transcriptomic classifier**: Developed from 2,029 patients across 12 cancer types; no tool name or accession is given in the review.
- **Dynamic optical coherence tomography (DOCT)**: Intraoperative non-invasive PNI detection; preclinical.
- **3D microfluidic organ-on-chip** for PNI modelling: Conceptual/proposed, not yet available.
- **GDNF / RET inhibitors, TrkA inhibitors (anti-NGF):** Pharmacological approaches in clinical/preclinical development; not bioinformatic tools.

## Follow-up

1. **Length-normalise before interpreting neural gene enrichment.** The axon-guidance and IgCAM genes named in this review (SLIT2, ROBO1/2, SEMA3A, PLXND1, L1CAM, NCAM1) are large genes. Cross-check the user's top-mutated list (LSAMP, OPCML, SGCZ, NKAIN2, KCNIP4, CALN1, RBFOX1, RIT2, TAFA2) against UniProt protein lengths and CDS lengths — if these genes are systematically longer than the pipeline's background gene set, H5 is confirmed as the primary confound.

2. **LSAMP and OPCML as tumour suppressor candidates.** Both are IgCAM superfamily members with known tumour-suppressor roles (loss in ovarian, glioma, lung cancers). Check whether their mutation pattern is consistent with tumour suppressor inactivation (truncating mutations, biallelic loss) vs. background-rate noise inflated by gene length.

3. **Obtain the Chen et al. 2025 Cancer Cell single-cell / spatial transcriptomics dataset** (ref [28]: Chen MM et al., "Integrated single-cell and spatial transcriptomics uncover distinct cellular subtypes involved in neural invasion in pancreatic cancer," Cancer Cell 2025;43(9):1656-1676, DOI 10.1016/j.ccell.2025.06.020). This dataset is the strongest current source for a data-driven neural gene signature from tumour-nerve interface profiling.

4. **Stratify cBioPortal studies by PNI-prevalent cancer type.** PDAC (100% PNI incidence), prostate cancer (25%–90%), and HNSCC (up to 14%) dominate PNI biology. If these cancer types are overrepresented in the pipeline's study set, neural/axon-guidance genes may appear enriched as a statistical artifact of cancer-type composition rather than a pan-cancer signal.

5. **Check RET mutation frequency and context.** RET is a known oncogene and PNI driver (via GDNF/GFRα1/RET); it appears in Bailey 2018 driver annotations. Verify whether RET ranks highly in the cBioPortal pipeline's frequency tables and whether it does so in PNI-prevalent cancer types specifically.

6. **Consider PNI status as a covariate in the h08 agnostic association scan.** If any cBioPortal studies report PNI status as a clinical variable, this could be directly integrated as a covariate in the agnostic association model to test whether neural gene mutations associate with PNI-positive tumours above background.

7. **Semaphorin / Plexin class is relevant to SEMA gene interpretation.** This review names SEMA3A, SEMA3D, and SEMA4D as PNI drivers. The semaphorin gene family is large and diverse; check whether any SEMA family members appear in the pipeline's top-mutated list and whether their sizes explain the signal.
