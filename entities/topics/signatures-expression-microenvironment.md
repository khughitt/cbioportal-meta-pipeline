# Mutational signatures, gene expression, and the tumour microenvironment

## Summary

Mutational signatures encode the cumulative imprint of past mutagenic exposures in a tumour's
somatic genome. A growing literature demonstrates that those exposures do not act in isolation:
the aetiological context that generates a given signature is frequently coupled — via shared
upstream causes, immune selection pressure, or downstream pathway consequences — to measurable
features of the tumour's transcriptome and immune microenvironment. This topic synthesises the
pan-cancer evidence for those couplings, identifies where mechanistic consensus is solid versus
speculative, and draws out the implications for
`hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and`, which
aims to recover known signature aetiologies from agnostic covariate associations.

---

## Key Concepts

**Mutational signature.** A weighted 96-trinucleotide profile (SBS) or analogous indel/DBS
profile that represents the mutation spectrum generated by a given process over a tumour's
lifetime. Reference signatures catalogued in COSMIC v3.x serve as the decomposition basis.
Exposures are the per-sample weights assigned by tools such as SigProfilerAssignment.

**Aetiology group.** Multiple COSMIC SBS signatures often trace to a single biological process.
In paper:Luo2023a, 47 non-artefact signatures are collapsed into 13 labelled etiology groups:
Clock-like (SBS1/5/40), AID/APOBEC (SBS2/13), Tobacco (SBS4/29), UV (SBS7a–d), dMMR
(SBS6/14/15/20/21/26/44), HRD (SBS3/8), ROS (SBS18), and rarer exposure-linked groups. This
aggregation reduces compositional noise and enables robust pan-cancer comparisons.

**Tumour microenvironment (TME).** The non-tumour cellular and acellular compartment surrounding
and infiltrating the tumour mass, including immune cells (CD8+ T cells, CD4+ T cells, regulatory
T cells, NK cells, macrophages, dendritic cells, B cells, plasma cells), stromal cells, and
extracellular matrix. Cell-type abundances are estimated from bulk RNA-seq by deconvolution
methods (Kassandra, EPIC, quanTIseq, TIMER 2.0) and serve as a proxy for immune phenotype.

**Compositional data analysis (CoDa).** Because the relative contributions of multiple
signatures within a sample sum to a fixed total (the total mutation count), simple Pearson or
Spearman correlations between signature activities are confounded by compositional structure.
CoDa, as applied in paper:Hakobyan2024, uses symmetric pivot coordinates that correct for this
artefact and is applicable to any two signatures that are co-active in the same sample.

**Signature-pathway interaction.** An empirical association between the activity of a mutational
signature and the number or type of driver mutations in a defined oncogenic pathway (TP53, PI3K,
RTK-RAS, MYC, WNT, etc.), detected via Fisher's exact test or linear/logistic regression.

## Current State of Knowledge

### Settled: known aetiologies produce recognisable immune phenotypes

The three positive-control aetiologies for the agnostic covariate-association hypothesis each have
documented pan-cancer immune correlates:

- **UV / SBS7 (SKCM):** UV signature activity is associated with lymphocyte and T-cell
  infiltration in skin melanoma (paper:Luo2023a) and enriches for RTK-RAS driver mutations
  (paper:Hakobyan2024). The causal chain UV exposure → high mutational load → neoantigen
  presentation → immune infiltration is well-supported, though the direction of the prognostic
  benefit is context-dependent (T-cell infiltration improves survival only when UV activity is
  low in SKCM — see Tensions below).

- **Tobacco / SBS4 (LUAD/LUSC):** Tobacco signature is synergistic with immune infiltration for
  survival in LUAD (paper:Luo2023a); high myeloid/lymphocyte/T-cell infiltration prolongs
  survival in high-Tobacco tumours. The exposure → immune recruitment mechanism mirrors the UV
  case but is less extensively documented at the interaction level.

- **APOBEC / SBS2-SBS13:** APOBEC (AID/APOBEC etiology) is associated with immune cell types
  across 15 cancer types and 20 cell types — the second-widest pan-cancer impact in the
  paper:Luo2023a map. In paper:Hakobyan2024, APOBEC interacts positively with HRD signatures in
  breast, esophageal, and pancreatic cancers, consistent with APOBEC dysregulation promoting
  genomic instability that activates HR. Both papers agree that APOBEC has broad multi-tissue
  immune reach; the direction of that reach is cancer-type-specific (see below).

- **dMMR / MSI-H:** MMR-deficiency signatures associate with neoantigen burden and immune
  infiltration across CRC, STAD, UCEC, and several other cancer types (paper:Luo2023a). In
  paper:Hakobyan2024, stomach adenocarcinoma hierarchically clusters into a dMMR-high C1 subtype
  (high NOTCH/WNT/TGFb/HIPPO/PI3K driver burden) and a SBS40/SBS17/APOBEC-high C2 subtype,
  confirming that signature compositions predict driver pathway enrichment.

### Settled: Clock-like signatures have the broadest pan-cancer immune impact

The Clock-like etiology group (SBS1, SBS5, SBS40) is associated with TME cell types across
26+ of 32 cancer types and 17 of 21 cell types in paper:Luo2023a — a wider span than any
exposure-linked group. In paper:Hakobyan2024, SBS5 is the most common shared interaction
partner across PCAWG and TCGA tissues, embedded centrally in the signature interaction network.
Clock-like activity also drives several survival effects: positive SBS1+SBS40 interaction in
hepatocellular carcinoma; negative MMR+SBS40 interaction worsening colorectal survival (HR =
24.54); negative SBS29+SBS40 interaction in hepatocellular carcinoma (HR = 6.42).

Despite this breadth of empirical association, the upstream aetiologies of SBS5 and SBS40
remain poorly understood. The two signatures appear behaviourally distinct — SBS5 is more
clonal and likely early-stage; SBS40 is enriched subclonally and associated with later
progression — yet both are conflated in the "Clock-like" label used by most TME association
studies. This split is directly relevant to the clock-like discovery arm of the agnostic
covariate-association hypothesis.

### Settled: Signature-pathway links recover known causal chains

paper:Hakobyan2024 validates its framework by recovering two known exposure→driver links:
(1) UV → RTK-RAS driver mutations in melanoma (UV exposure directly causes C>T transitions at
codons targeted by RTK-RAS hotspot drivers); (2) SBS1/aging → TP53 mutations in pancreatic
adenocarcinoma (CpG deamination as a source of recurrent C>T TP53 hotspots). These
confirmations establish that agnostic association methods applied to TCGA/PCAWG data can
successfully recover known mechanistic chains — the same claim that the positive-control arm must
demonstrate.

### Settled: TMB is a weak universal prognostic signal

Both papers converge on TMB having limited independent prognostic value. In paper:Hakobyan2024,
TMB had no survival effect in 17 of 23 evaluated tissues. The more informative unit is the
specific signature composition rather than aggregate mutation burden — a finding consistent with
the agnostic association design, which stratifies by signature identity rather than total count.

## Controversies and Tensions

### Direction-reversal of APOBEC immune effects across tissues

APOBEC shows antagonistic effects on immune survival benefit in LUAD but synergistic effects in
HNSC and BLAC (paper:Luo2023a). The mechanism underlying this sign reversal is unknown.
Candidate explanations include (a) different APOBEC3A vs APOBEC3B isoform dominance across
tissues, (b) distinct HLA evolutionary contexts that alter neoantigen
immunogenicity, or (c) confounding by co-occurring driver events (e.g., EGFR/ALK in LUAD may
suppress immune recruitment independently). Resolving this requires a within-cancer-type
expression covariate design — exactly what the agnostic association design proposes.

### Immune phenotype as cause or consequence of mutational process

The central interpretive tension across both papers is whether associations between signatures
and TME represent: (a) the mutational process shaping the immune environment (mechanistic
causation); (b) the immune environment selecting which tumour clones survive and are sequenced
(immunoediting); or (c) a shared upstream driver — tissue type, patient age, or exogenous
exposure — that determines both (common cause). Neither paper can resolve this from
observational cross-sectional data. The SKCM×UV paradox (T-cell infiltration only prognostic
when UV activity is low) illustrates point (b): UV-high tumours may already have been shaped
by immune selection pressure before measurement. This tension maps directly to the R3 alternative
interpretation in
`hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and`
(selection/immunoediting rather than causal exposure).

### CoDa vs Spearman for compositional signature data

paper:Hakobyan2024 makes a methodological argument that Spearman and Pearson correlations
between signature activities are confounded by the compositional structure of attribution
(signatures sharing the same total mutation count as the denominator). Their CoDa approach
addresses this via symmetric pivot coordinates. However, CoDa can only be computed for samples
where both signatures are co-active, reducing effective sample size for rarer signatures and
potentially introducing selection bias. Neither paper tests whether CoDa and Spearman yield
substantively different biological conclusions in practice; in the agnostic association layer this
remains an open methodological question.

### Aggregated etiology groups vs individual SBS signatures

paper:Luo2023a collapses 47 signatures into 13 etiology groups to gain statistical power and
interpretability. This aggregation discards within-group heterogeneity: SBS1 and SBS5 have
different biological properties (CpG deamination vs unknown replication timing process) despite
co-membership in "Clock-like". Similarly, SBS2 and SBS13 — the two canonical APOBEC signatures
— may have different tissue and cell-cycle-phase distributions. The aggregation may also mask
interactions that require signature-level resolution, as demonstrated by paper:Hakobyan2024's
finding that SBS40 and SBS5 have distinct interaction profiles and survival associations.

## Gaps

- **No systematic expression-covariate scan.** Both papers use TME cell-type abundances
  (deconvolved from RNA-seq) or driver pathway alteration status as the non-signature covariate.
  Neither paper uses unsupervised transcriptomic modules or continuous gene-expression covariates
  as the association axis. The agnostic expression scan in `method:h08-agnostic-association-model`
  occupies this gap.

- **No cross-study replication.** Both papers rely exclusively on TCGA (and PCAWG for
  paper:Hakobyan2024). The cbioportal pipeline's multi-study design is complementary: if an
  association between, say, APOBEC expression and SBS2/13 activity holds across independent
  cBioPortal studies, it is less likely to reflect TCGA-specific batch effects.

- **Clock-like aetiological resolution.** SBS5 and SBS40 have the broadest pan-cancer immune
  and survival impact but are the least causally understood. Whether their immune associations
  reflect true exposure-driven processes (reactive oxygen species? replication timing errors?
  viral insertions?) or are artefacts of age-as-confounder remains unresolved.

- **Substrate and burden links.** `topic:signature-decomposition-unmatched-normal` carries the
  practical signature-extraction substrate concerns, while `topic:tumor-mutational-burden`
  captures the aggregate-burden comparator that this topic treats as too coarse for most
  aetiology questions.

- **Indel and DBS signatures.** Both papers restrict to SBS. Frameshift indels in microsatellite
  regions (dMMR) generate neoantigen loads that may partially account for immune activation;
  DBS signatures may capture additional exposures (platinum chemotherapy, aflatoxin). Omitting
  these creates gaps in the exposure-immune map.

- **Clonal vs subclonal signature attribution.** Clonal mutations (present in all cells)
  represent early-stage exposures; subclonal mutations are late or ongoing. SBS40 is thought to
  be more subclonal than SBS5, yet most association analyses treat all attributed mutations as
  equivalent. Including clonality information (VAF distributions) would improve causal inference.

## Implications for the cross-study signature-aetiology aggregation

**Positive-control grounding.** The pan-cancer evidence in paper:Luo2023a and the
signature-pathway recovery in paper:Hakobyan2024 jointly confirm that the positive-control arms of
`hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and` —
UV→SBS7 in SKCM, tobacco→SBS4 in LUAD, APOBEC expression→SBS2/13 — are grounded in reproducible
empirical associations detectable from TCGA/PCAWG data. If the agnostic scan using cBioPortal
expression modules fails to recover these, the failure is more likely a method or data problem than
a false biological prior.

**Within-tissue conditioning is non-negotiable.** Both papers condition on tissue type as a
primary covariate. paper:Hakobyan2024's within-tissue bootstrapped null and paper:Luo2023a's
cancer-type linear model adjustment both demonstrate that pan-cancer pooling without tissue
stratification would generate spurious cross-tissue associations driven by tissue composition.
Prediction 4 in
`hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and` — that
associations attenuate when not conditioned on tissue — is directly supported.

**CoDa should be evaluated for the expression-covariate layer.** The compositional
structure of signature attributions is a genuine statistical problem when regressing one
signature's activity against another. When the covariate is an independent gene-expression
module (not derived from the same mutation catalogue), CoDa is not required; but when using
signature-pair relationships as part of the association model, CoDa correction would be
appropriate. The mutsigapp R package from paper:Hakobyan2024 provides a starting implementation.

**SBS40 vs SBS5 distinction is a priority target for the clock-like discovery arm.** The survival and interaction
evidence in both papers singles out SBS40 as behaviourally distinct from SBS5 despite their
co-labelling as "Clock-like". If the expression-module covariate scan can separate SBS40 from SBS5
activity — whether through aging-related inflammation modules, replication-timing-proximal
expression changes, or oxidative-damage pathways — it would resolve the most consequential gap in
the current pan-cancer signature literature.

**The immunoediting / common-cause ambiguity must be pre-registered.** Both papers acknowledge
but cannot resolve whether signature-TME associations are causal or selected. The pre-registration
for `hypothesis:0007-agnostic-covariate-association-recovers-known-signature-aetiologies-and`
should explicitly commit to a falsifiable criterion for the selection alternative (R3): for example,
if an APOBEC3-expression→SBS2/13 association holds in the tumour compartment after controlling for
immune infiltration estimates from the same RNA-seq, this would partially rule out a scenario in
which immune cells — rather than cancer cells — are driving the expression signal.

**APOBEC direction-reversal is a secondary test case.** The tissue-specific sign flip
(antagonistic in LUAD, synergistic in HNSC/BLAC) identified by paper:Luo2023a is testable in
the agnostic association scan if cBioPortal studies include both LUAD and HNSC tumours with paired
expression data. If APOBEC3A/B mRNA levels explain the direction difference, this would constitute a
mechanistically informative secondary finding beyond the primary positive-control test.
