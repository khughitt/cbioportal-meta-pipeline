# Genome-wide mutational signatures in low-coverage whole genome sequencing of cell-free DNA

- **Authors:** Jonathan C. M. Wan, Dennis Stephens, Lingqi Luo, James R. White, Caitlin M. Stewart, Benoit Rousseau, Dana W. Y. Tsui, Luis A. Diaz Jr.
- **Year:** 2022
- **Journal:** Nature Communications
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-022-32598-1
- **BibTeX key:** Wan2022
- **Source:** PDF

## Key Contribution

This paper introduces **Pointy**, a proof-of-concept pipeline for extracting genome-wide single-base substitution (SBS) mutational signatures from ultra-low-coverage (0.3–1.5×) plasma whole-genome sequencing (WGS) of cell-free DNA (cfDNA). The central finding is that both pathological (cancer-derived: aging/SBS1, MSI/SBS20-21, APOBEC/SBS2-13) and physiological (age-correlated clock-like) mutational signatures can be identified in plasma without matched germline or deep sequencing. A random forest classifier trained on these plasma mutation profiles distinguishes stage I–IV cancer patients from healthy individuals with an AUC of 0.96 (95% CI 0.94–0.98) across multiple cancer types.

## Methods

**Pipeline (Pointy):** Plasma cfDNA WGS libraries were generated at 0.3–1.5× coverage (target 10M paired-end reads after downsampling). Key quality filters: minimum base quality 30, mean BQ 30, mapping quality 60, mutations present in both R1 and R2 reads. GC-bias normalization was applied per-sample using Picard and a generalized additive model (GAM) to generate batch-averaged GC profiles; mutation counts were normalized relative to the averaged GC profile.

**Signature fitting:** 96-SBS mutation profiles were generated per sample. Mutational signatures were fitted using `fit_to_signature()` from MutationalPatterns v1.10.0, using the Alexandrov et al. 2020 COSMIC reference matrix. Background subtraction (subtracting the median control count per SBS context) was applied to reduce noise before fitting. Sequencing artifact signatures were included in the database to minimize misattribution. SNP filtering was explored as an additional denoising step (SNP coordinates from 1000 Genomes and dbSNP 151).

**ML classification:** For cancer vs. healthy classification, SNP-subtracted 96-SBS profiles were first dimensionality-reduced via PCA (removing PCs with <1% variance). PCA-transformed profiles were input to a nested 10-fold cross-validation framework repeated 10 times. Four classifiers were tested: xgboost, random forest (RF), SVM, and logistic regression. RF achieved the highest performance (AUC 0.98, 95% CI 0.90–1.00 for PGDX; AUC 0.99, 95% CI 0.95–1.00 in DELFI validation). Adding ichorCNA ctDNA fraction as a feature improved AUC to 1.00 (95% CI 1.00–1.00) in some settings.

**Cohorts:**
- *PGDX (discovery):* 16 stage IV CRC patients + 19 healthy controls, sequenced to median 31.0×10^6 reads, downsampled to 10M (0.28× median coverage).
- *DELFI (validation):* 199 cancer patients (NSCLC n=37, CRC n=27, breast n=48, gastric n=27, ovarian n=26, pancreatic n=34) + 206 healthy controls, sequenced at 25M reads (0.75×). All cancer types combined, multi-sequencer; analyses batched by sequencer to control for GC-bias batch effects.

**Fragment size analysis:** Short (<150 bp) vs. long (>150 bp) fragment ratio calculated as a quality control metric; CRC, NSCLC, and breast cancer patients showed significantly shorter cfDNA fragments than healthy controls.

**Aging signature profiling in healthy individuals:** 159 healthy individuals (49–75 years, median 54) from the DELFI study were sequenced at 50M reads (1.5×). SBS1 and SBS5 (clock-like signatures) were significantly correlated with chronological age (SBS1: R=0.20, q=0.01; SBS5: R=0.16, q=0.02; one-sided Pearson), validating the biological signal in plasma.

## Key Findings

1. **Pathological signatures recoverable at 0.3× coverage:** In stage IV CRC plasma, aging (SBS1, SBS5) and MSI-related (SBS20, SBS21) signatures were significantly elevated vs. healthy controls (BH-adjusted p≤0.05). Both SBS1 and MSI signatures correlated significantly with ichorCNA ctDNA fraction and tumor mutation burden (TMB).

2. **Per-sample detection at 95% specificity:** Aging signatures were detected in 10/16 (62.5%) CRC patients; MSI signatures in 11/16 (69.0%) CRC patients. Signature detection agreed with targeted sequencing-based signature calls in 77.6% of cases across all signatures.

3. **Multi-cancer classification (DELFI):** Overall AUC 0.96 (95% CI 0.94–0.98) across all cancer types and stages. Per-cancer AUCs: NSCLC 0.99, breast 0.99, CRC 0.98, gastric 0.92, ovarian 1.00, pancreatic 0.88. Per-stage detection (≥1 signature at 95% specificity): stage I AUC 0.96, stage II 0.95, stage III 0.97, stage IV 0.97.

4. **Cancer-type-specific signature profiles:** In NSCLC, APOBEC signatures were most prevalent (24/37, 65%); in CRC, aging signatures were dominant; in breast cancer, aging signatures were most frequent (27/48, 56%). APOBEC detected in 14/27 (51%) breast patients, MSI in 11/27 (41%) CRC patients.

5. **Clock-like signatures increase with age in healthy plasma:** SBS1 and SBS5 showed significant positive correlation with chronological age in 159 cancer-free individuals. Thirteen additional SBS1-correlated signatures (including SBS2^-, SBS30^-, SBS33^-, SBS46^-) were also significantly age-correlated after multiple testing correction.

6. **SNP subtraction tradeoff:** Removing SNP-reads reduces aging-signature bias in healthy controls but lowers cosine similarity of case profiles (0.982 vs. 0.999); SNP-retained data better estimates fitted signatures, while SNP-subtracted data improves cancer classification signal-to-noise.

7. **Cancer type classification:** Median balanced accuracy of 0.80 (95% CI 0.52–0.89) for classifying samples into individual cancer types using PCA-derived mutation profiles — a moderate result consistent with substantial signature overlap across cancers.

8. **Error-suppression by read concordance:** Requiring mutations in both R1 and R2 significantly increased classification AUC (0.93 without vs. 0.98 with error-suppression, p=0.004), validating the importance of paired-read concordance as an error filter in ultra-low-coverage WGS.

## Relevance

**h08 (agnostic covariate–signature-exposure association):** This paper is directly relevant to the H08a positive-control arm. It demonstrates that canonical exposure→signature links — aging/clock-like (SBS1/SBS5), MSI (SBS20/SBS21), APOBEC (SBS2/SBS13), and tobacco (SBS4 recovered in NSCLC) — can be recovered in a novel biological medium (cfDNA plasma) using ultra-sparse mutation profiles. The cfDNA context is important: if biological exposures are recoverable even at 0.3× coverage with high background noise, they should be even more reliably recoverable in the tumor tissue WGS/WES data the cbioportal pipeline processes. This provides a floor on sensitivity for H08a positive-control recovery experiments.

**Correlation with clinical covariates:** The paper demonstrates significant correlations between signature exposures (SBS1, SBS5, SBS20, SBS21) and ctDNA fraction and TMB — exactly the kind of structured covariate-to-signature association that h08 proposes to scan agnostically. The MSI↔SBS20/21 and age↔SBS1/SBS5 links recovered here are textbook positive controls that H08a pre-registers as mandatory recovery targets.

**Batch/artifact signatures:** The paper explicitly includes artifact signatures (SBS27/43/45–60 class) in the fitting database to reduce misattribution — directly relevant to the R4 (batch artifact) alternative explanation in h08, and to the pipeline's existing sequencing artifact decontamination problem.

**cfDNA-specific context (group J_cfdna):** The paper establishes that cfDNA plasma WGS is a viable substrate for mutational signature profiling. The low ctDNA fraction (median 11.4% quantifiable by ichorCNA) and ultra-low depth represent an extreme version of the low-signal problem encountered in any unmatched-normal somatic calling setting, including cBioPortal panel-sequencing studies. The Pointy noise-handling approaches (GC correction, paired-read concordance, background subtraction, SNP filtering) are directly applicable to signal-quality considerations in cross-study aggregation.

**Cross-study meta-analysis relevance:** The batch effects observed between cohorts (healthy control SBS profiles differed between PGDX and DELFI despite GC-correction) mirror the cross-study heterogeneity issues in the cbioportal pipeline. The authors mitigate this by batching cases with controls from the same sequencer — analogous to the per-study normalization strategy in the pipeline.

## Limitations

- **No matched germline sequencing:** The pipeline operates without germline, making SNP removal necessary but imperfect. Germline SNP misattribution to SBS1 (aging) is explicitly documented. The project's cBioPortal studies also frequently lack matched normals — the unmatched-normal contamination problem (`topic:signature-decomposition-unmatched-normal`) is analogous.

- **Ultra-low coverage:** The 0.3–1.5× depth means many genomic positions have zero coverage, severely limiting per-sample mutation counts (median ~9886 after filtering, before SNP removal). Signatures requiring many mutations for reliable decomposition (e.g. SBS4-tobacco, rare signatures) have low sensitivity.

- **No independent replication of the aging-plasma correlation:** The healthy-individual aging analysis used data from a single sequencer (HISEQ) to avoid batch confounding; generalizability to other sequencers is unconfirmed.

- **Batch effects between cohorts:** Despite GC correction, healthy control SBS profiles differed between PGDX and DELFI cohorts, attributed to multi-site sample collection vs. single commercial source. Limits cross-cohort generalizability of classifier.

- **Limited stage I sensitivity:** While overall AUC is high (0.96), signature detection at 95% specificity in stage I disease was bootstrapped median ~70%, reflecting the challenge of detecting rare ctDNA-derived mutations.

- **Closed-code repository:** Pointy code is available for academic research only via a restricted-access Zenodo repository (DOI 10.5281/zenodo.6666951), requiring license approval — limits open replication.

- **Cancer type classification is moderate:** Balanced accuracy of 0.80 for cancer type classification suggests substantial signature overlap across cancers; mutation profiles alone are insufficient for precise cancer-of-origin determination.

## Follow-up

- **Relevance to H08a positive-control design:** The aging↔SBS1/SBS5 and MSI↔SBS20/SBS21 correlations confirmed in cfDNA at 0.3× coverage set a sensitivity floor — these should be easily detectable at the mutation counts available in cBioPortal WGS/WES studies. If h08's within-tissue association scan fails to recover these, the scan is likely underpowered or confounded.
- **SNP contamination of aging signatures:** The paper's detailed treatment of how SNPs contaminate SBS1 calls is relevant to h08's artifact-alternative (R4) and the pipeline's `annotate_ch.py` concern that aging mutations may be misattributed (the SNP-in-aging-signature problem occurs in cfDNA; it will be even more acute in unmatched-normal tumor sequencing where germline reads are not removed).
- **Papers to read next:** Cristiano et al. 2019 (Nature) — the DELFI fragmentation paper whose cohort is reused here; Alexandrov et al. 2020 (Nature) — the COSMIC v3 reference used for fitting; Georgiadis et al. 2019 — MSI/TMB detection paper for the PGDX cohort.
- **Question raised:** Can the Pointy background-subtraction + GC-normalization + artifact-signature approach be adapted to improve per-study signature fitting in the cBioPortal pipeline, particularly for studies without matched normals?
